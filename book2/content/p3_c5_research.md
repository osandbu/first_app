# Research Notes: p3_c5 — Why Reorgs Rarely Work

## Core Thesis
Reorganizations are predominantly a management action reflex — a structural answer to what are usually incentive, communication, or strategy problems. Because structure is visible and changeable on a short timeline, it gets changed. Because incentives, informal power, and culture are harder to see and slower to change, they don't. The result: new boxes on the org chart, same pathologies underneath.

---

## 1. Historical Parallels

### 1.1 IBM's Decade of Restructuring (1985–1993)
IBM is the canonical case of repeated restructuring that failed to arrest decline. Under CEO John Akers (CEO 1985–1993), IBM ran through at least two major restructuring waves:

- **January 1988**: Sweeping bureaucratic restructuring aimed at streamlining profit focus and reducing layers. Akers was credited with "simplifying the company's bureaucracy to focus more on profits" — language that signals the restructuring was aimed at symptoms, not the underlying loss of competitive position in personal computers and Unix systems.
- **1991–1992**: Far more radical. IBM began decentralizing into semi-autonomous "lines of business" — separate units for storage, software, processors, printers, services. The logic was that IBM's integrated model couldn't compete with focused specialists. The practical result was increased internal complexity: transfer pricing disputes between units, conflicting sales forces selling competing products to the same customers, and a management class that, in Gerstner's later description, "presided rather than acted."

By 1993, IBM posted losses exceeding $8 billion — one of the largest in U.S. corporate history at that point. The plan to spin out the lines of business as independent companies was actively under consideration when Lou Gerstner arrived.

**Gerstner's diagnosis** (from *Who Says Elephants Can't Dance?*) was that the prior restructurings had attacked structure while leaving the real problems intact: internal politics, preoccupation with process over customer, and misaligned incentives. His famous observation — "culture isn't just one aspect of the game — it is the game" — is a direct indictment of structure-first thinking. Gerstner reversed the decentralization, reorganized around customer industries rather than product lines, and critically, changed the incentive structure: executives were required to personally own company stock, not just hold options, aligning them with shareholder outcomes rather than divisional performance metrics.

The key lesson: IBM restructured three times in eight years, losing progressively more money, until a leader changed the incentive architecture instead of the org chart.

### 1.2 Kodak's Structural Drift Toward Bankruptcy (1989–2012)
Kodak is a cleaner example of the structural problem that reorgs cannot fix: a successful business with deeply embedded incentives to defend the existing revenue stream against the technology that will displace it.

Key structural moves and why they failed:
- **Kay Whitmore era (1989–1993)**: Whitmore was a film-business lifer who returned Kodak to its "core" — a structural and strategic decision that reinforced investment in film at the expense of digital exploration. Restructuring away from the pharmaceutical diversification (the $5.1B Sterling Drug acquisition, 1988) was itself a reorg — but it narrowed focus onto the declining core rather than building bridges to digital.
- **George Fisher era (1993–1999)**: Fisher, an outsider recruited from Motorola, created a dedicated "Digital and Applied Imaging" group — a structural change to signal seriousness about digital. He poured billions into digital R&D. And yet, Fisher himself told the *New York Times* that Kodak regarded digital photography "as the enemy" — an "evil juggernaut that would kill the chemical-based film and paper business." The incentive problem was explicit: every dollar of digital revenue cannibalized far higher-margin film revenue. The "Digital and Applied Imaging" group existed structurally, but was funded from film profits it was tasked with eventually destroying.
- The post-Fisher era brought more layoffs and structural trimming, but the core incentive conflict — film is high-margin, digital is low-margin, and they serve the same customers — was never resolved through any structural move. Kodak filed for bankruptcy in January 2012.

Kodak invented the digital camera sensor in 1975. The organization had the knowledge; it lacked incentive alignment to act on it.

### 1.3 Matrix Organization: The Structure That Keeps Returning
Matrix organization — dual reporting to both a functional manager and a project/product manager — has a cyclical history that perfectly illustrates how structural solutions get abandoned, then reintroduced when people forget why they were abandoned:

- **Origins (1960s)**: Matrix management emerged in U.S. aerospace in the early 1960s, specifically in NASA's Apollo program and at contractors like TRW Systems. The problem it addressed was real: how to run mission-specific project teams that needed to draw on specialists who also had to maintain functional excellence. NASA's dual mandate — both build systems and advance engineering knowledge — required dual accountability.
- **Mass adoption (1970s)**: Matrix spread widely in the 1970s, adopted by companies without the same dual-mandate problem that justified it in aerospace. The pathologies emerged: unclear accountability, political conflict between functional and project managers, decision paralysis when the two reporting lines disagreed, excessive coordination overhead. By 2004, it was broadly considered a failed structure.
- **Reintroduction**: Nokia in 2004 attempted what was labeled "matrix management 2.0." Other organizations have periodically reintroduced dual-reporting structures under new names (chapter-tribe models, platform-stream models). The underlying dynamic is the same: organizations face a coordination problem, adopt matrix as the structural solution, and eventually find that the coordination problem had a communication and incentive dimension that the structure alone cannot fix.

The matrix history is a microcosm of the broader reorg cycle: a genuine problem, a structural solution, a new set of problems created by the structure, eventual abandonment, eventual rediscovery.

## 2. Key Frameworks

### 2.1 Structural Inertia Theory (Hannan & Freeman, 1984)
Michael Hannan and John Freeman published "Structural Inertia and Organizational Change" in the *American Sociological Review* in 1984, building on their 1977 population ecology framework. Core claims:

- Organizations exhibit **structural inertia** — they resist change in their fundamental architecture because reliability and accountability are the primary sources of survival advantage. Organizations are rewarded for reducing variability and fulfilling stakeholder expectations consistently.
- Sources of inertia are both internal (sunk costs in equipment and personnel, political coalitions, precedents that become norms) and external (stakeholder relationships, regulatory obligations, market expectations of consistency).
- Critically: Hannan & Freeman argue that structural change actually *increases* mortality risk. Changing fundamental organizational characteristics makes an organization more vulnerable during the transition period because it disrupts the reliability routines that legitimize the organization.
- **Implication for reorgs**: The theory predicts that restructuring will create a period of increased organizational fragility and will not neutralize the inertial forces it aims to overcome — those forces are embedded in culture, coalition, sunk cost, and precedent, not in reporting lines.

### 2.2 The Iceberg Model (Culture, Behavior, Incentives)
A layered model useful for explaining why structural changes fail: what's visible (org chart, reporting lines, headcount allocation) sits atop a much larger invisible mass (informal power dynamics, norms, embedded incentives, psychological contracts).

- **Visible layer**: titles, reporting relationships, team names, budget envelopes
- **Behavior layer**: how people actually make decisions, who gets consulted, whose approval matters regardless of the org chart
- **Culture and norms layer**: what's rewarded in practice vs. on paper, which behaviors are tacitly penalized, what the real criteria are for advancement
- **Incentive architecture layer**: compensation structures, metric definitions, performance review criteria, career path dependencies

Reorgs almost exclusively operate on the visible layer. The behavior layer shifts slowly and partially. Culture and incentive architecture change only when explicitly targeted — and they require much longer time horizons than a structural change. The IBM case illustrates: Akers changed structure repeatedly; Gerstner changed incentive architecture and culture, which is why his changes held.

### 2.3 Organizational Homeostasis
Organizations, like biological systems, have homeostatic mechanisms that resist perturbation and return to prior equilibria. Informal power networks re-establish themselves around new titles. Political coalitions reform. Budget control migrates back to whoever controls the funding decisions, regardless of org chart position. The formal structure changes; the informal structure heals around it.

This is why the post-reorg period so often disappoints: the organization functionally resembles its pre-reorg state within 12–24 months, because the informal systems are more durable than the formal ones.

### 2.4 Change Management Research
- McKinsey research finds that only **~23% of corporate reorganizations** are deemed fully successful by the companies undertaking them. A further 44% bog down during implementation and are never completed. Another 23% are completed but fail to meet objectives. Net result: roughly **three-quarters of redesign efforts fail** to meet objectives and improve performance.
- A separate McKinsey finding: over **80% of restructuring initiatives** fail to deliver the desired value within the desired timeline.
- The "70% failure rate" for organizational change initiatives — popularized by John Kotter (Harvard, *Leading Change*, 1996) — has been debated methodologically, but the directional finding is consistent across multiple research streams: most large organizational change efforts do not achieve their intended goals.
- Chronic reorganization frequency: some organizations restructure every **12–24 months**, a cadence that prevents any structure from demonstrating its effectiveness before the next disruption begins.

## 3. The Mechanics: Why Reorgs Feel Like Progress

### 3.1 Motion Is Mistaken for Direction
Reorganizations create visible, large-scale change activity — new org charts circulated, all-hands meetings held, headcount shuffled, managers introduced in new roles. This activity is phenomenologically indistinguishable from progress, especially to the management layer initiating it. Action bias (the psychological tendency to prefer action to inaction, especially under uncertainty) makes restructuring appealing precisely because it *looks like* addressing the problem.

The illusion of control is a related mechanism: simply being assigned to a management role increases individuals' felt sense of control over outcomes. Initiating a structural change compounds this — it feels like directly causing organizational improvement, when the causal link between reporting-line changes and business outcomes is weak at best.

### 3.2 Social Dynamics Reset
A less-discussed benefit of reorgs — which is also the source of their seductiveness — is that they reset the social dynamics of the organization. Organizational politics accumulate over time: specific people become blocking nodes, coalitions calcify, particular managers acquire disproportionate informal authority. A reorg disrupts all of this, at least temporarily.

The problem: this reset effect is genuine but temporary. Within a year or two, the political equilibrium re-establishes itself, often with the same people in functionally equivalent positions, just with new titles. The political problem that the reorg temporarily dissolved was itself a symptom of incentive and communication structures that the reorg left intact.

### 3.3 The New-Manager Reorg Reflex
There is a near-universal tendency for new senior managers to reorganize their area within the first six months. Structurally, this makes some sense — the new manager needs to build their own team and establish their own authority. But it also means that the organization undergoes structural disruption every time leadership changes, regardless of whether the underlying business problem warrants it. Veterans learn to wait out the new-manager reorg, knowing it will happen regardless of merit.

## 4. Concrete Scenarios (No Company Names)

**Scenario A: Reporting lines change, budget authority doesn't.**
A product engineering organization is reorganized so that platform teams report to a newly-created Infrastructure VP instead of the CTO. The intent is to give infrastructure more autonomy and reduce bottlenecks. But infrastructure budget is still allocated annually by the same CFO approval process that was the actual bottleneck, and the CFO's mental model of infrastructure as a cost center hasn't changed. Eighteen months later, the same funding debates occur, with the same outcomes, just with a different VP in the meeting.

**Scenario B: The team split that must still coordinate on everything.**
A large team is split in two to create "clearer accountability" between product A and product B. Both teams continue to share the same underlying platform, the same deployment pipeline, the same on-call rotation, and the same customers. Every cross-cutting concern now requires a coordination meeting between two teams with separate managers who have separate performance incentives. The coordination overhead is higher than before the split, and the shared dependencies mean neither team has the autonomy the reorg was meant to create.

**Scenario C: The merger that assumes away the real problem.**
Two teams with a history of conflict over technical direction are merged under a single manager, on the theory that unified management will resolve the friction. The conflict was about genuine architectural disagreement, not org-chart ambiguity. The new manager now owns both sides of the debate but still has to make the architectural call. The conflict re-emerges in the first design review, now as intra-team dysfunction rather than inter-team friction.

**Scenario D: The "center of excellence" that has no authority.**
A reorg creates a new horizontal Center of Excellence to standardize engineering practices across divisions. The CoE is staffed, has a charter, produces guidelines. It has no budget authority over the divisions and no role in hiring or performance review. The divisions continue to make their own decisions. The CoE produces documents that are read and then ignored. Two years later it is quietly disbanded in the next reorg.

**Scenario E: The decentralization that creates 40 fiefdoms.**
A centralized organization with coordination problems decentralizes: each division gets its own engineering team, its own roadmap, its own tech stack choices. The coordination problem (slow decisions at the center) is solved. A new problem emerges: 40 incompatible systems, no shared identity, no economies of scale, and a talent market that now requires 40 separate hiring funnels. The next reorg recentralizes.

**Scenario F: The "we need to be more like a startup" reorg.**
A mature organization reduces management layers, eliminates program management, and declares itself "flat." The informal coordination work that program managers did — dependency tracking, cross-team alignment, timeline management — doesn't disappear; it just becomes informal and invisible. Senior engineers spend 40% of their time on coordination that was previously handled by a role that was eliminated. The flatness is organizational, not operational.

## 5. Counter-Arguments

### 5.1 Reorgs Are Sometimes Genuinely Necessary
The argument that reorgs rarely work can slide into an argument that reorgs never work, which is false. There are situations where structure is the problem:

- **When the product architecture has genuinely changed**: Conway's Law (Mel Conway, 1967) states that organizations produce systems that mirror their communication structure. If a company built its org around a monolithic architecture and has migrated to distributed services, misalignment between team structure and system architecture creates real friction — not just political friction, but technical friction in the form of team boundaries that don't match service ownership boundaries. Here, reorg is load-bearing.
- **When two groups are so politically locked that the only resolution is structural**: Sometimes a conflict between teams or functions is genuinely irresolvable without a structural change that changes who reports to whom, because the informal relationship has become too toxic. This is rare, but it's real.
- **When the business model has changed so dramatically that the old structure is simply wrong**: The Gerstner IBM case is instructive — he did reorganize IBM, but around industries/customers rather than products. The reorganization was load-bearing because it aligned the company with how customers bought, not around how IBM manufactured. The key difference: he changed incentives and culture simultaneously. The structure change was one element of a package, not the whole thing.

The nuanced answer: reorgs *can* work when the problem is genuinely structural — when the current structure actively misroutes communication or decision authority in a way that cannot be fixed without changing the boxes. They don't work when the problem is incentive misalignment, cultural drift, or strategic uncertainty, none of which are addressable by moving names on a chart.

### 5.2 Sometimes a Reorg Buys Time to Fix the Real Problem
A secondary counter-argument: even a theatrical reorg sometimes creates a window of disruption during which the real problem can be addressed. The social dynamics reset is real — the reorg clears the political slate temporarily, and a skilled leader can use that window to make the harder changes (incentive structure, performance criteria, cultural norms) before the political calculus re-solidifies.

This is a legitimate use of structural change as a tactical tool — but it requires clear-eyed recognition that the reorg is not the solution, it's the distraction that buys space for the solution. Most organizations that use reorgs this way don't follow through on the second step.

## 6. Data Points and Studies

- **McKinsey Global Survey on Organizational Redesigns**: ~23% of reorgs deemed successful; 44% never completed; ~23% completed but miss objectives. Overall: ~75% failure rate.
- **McKinsey supplemental finding**: 80%+ of restructuring initiatives fail to deliver desired value in the necessary time.
- **Kotter/change management research** (directional): ~70% of major change initiatives fail to achieve intended goals; this has been the approximate figure across research conducted from the mid-1990s through the 2010s.
- **Chronic restructuring cadence**: Some organizations restructure every 12–24 months, a pace at which no structure can demonstrate effectiveness.
- **Change fatigue research**: Organizational change fatigue is well-documented — too-frequent changes cause burnout, cynicism, and employees waiting out initiatives rather than investing in them. The biggest driver of cynicism is perceived loss of control.
- **Structural inertia mortality finding** (Hannan & Freeman): Structural change increases organizational mortality risk during the transition period.
- **Employee cynicism from restructuring**: Organizational restructuring engenders cynicism, described in the research as "one of the most significant obstacles facing contemporary businesses." Cynicism is highest when employees perceive the change as imposed without their input.
- **Homeostatic recovery timeline**: Informal political structures typically re-establish within 12–24 months of a restructuring.

## 7. Suggested Chapter Section Structure

1. **The Reflex** — Why reorgs are the default response (visible, fast, gives management a sense of control, resets political dynamics). The action bias. New manager reflex.

2. **What Survives Any Reorg** — The iceberg model. What actually determines behavior: budget authority, promotion criteria, informal power, communication patterns. Structural inertia theory and homeostasis — the org heals around the change.

3. **Three Historical Case Studies** — IBM's decade of structural churn versus Gerstner's cultural/incentive fix. Kodak's structural hedging that never resolved the incentive conflict. Matrix organization's 60-year cycle of adoption and abandonment.

4. **The Scenarios** — Concrete patterns: the reorg that doesn't move budget authority; the team split that must still coordinate; the center of excellence with no teeth; the decentralization that creates fiefdoms. Each scenario ends with: *same bottleneck, new names on the org chart.*

5. **When Structure Actually Is the Problem** — Conway's Law as a diagnostic: misalignment between communication structure and product architecture is a genuine structural problem. Distinguishing load-bearing reorgs from theatrical ones. The checklist: are you changing incentives? Metrics? Promotion criteria? If not, you're moving boxes.

6. **The Hard Alternative** — What to do instead of reorging. Diagnose which layer the problem lives in. If it's incentives, change the incentives. If it's communication, change the meeting and decision-rights structure directly. If it's culture, know that takes 3–5 years and won't fit in a reorg announcement. Structure is the last lever to pull, not the first.
