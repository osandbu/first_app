# Engineering Beyond Code — Voice and Style Guide

## Register

Write like a **candid senior peer**, not a management consultant. The reader is a
technical person who has been burned by vague advice before. Respect their intelligence.
Be specific. Be willing to name dysfunction directly.

Think Will Larson's *An Elegant Puzzle* meets Patrick McKenzie's blog: technically
credible, practically useful, occasionally contrarian, no buzzwords.

The author is not a guru delivering wisdom from above. They are someone who learned
these lessons the hard way and is passing them on without the false modesty that would
imply the lessons weren't hard-won.

## What the Reader Wants

Every chapter should answer: **what should I do differently starting Monday?**

Not "executives should consider" — that's the other book. Here it's: "here is the
specific thing you can change about how you operate, and here is why it will work."

Readers are practitioners. They have limited patience for theory that doesn't cash out
in action. Each chapter earns the reader's time by being immediately useful.

## Tone Calibrations

**Be direct.** If something is wrong in most engineering organizations, say it's wrong.
Don't hedge it into uselessness.

**Be empathetic without being soft.** The chapter "Why Good Engineers Do Bad Things"
names structural causes without excusing bad outcomes. The reader should feel
understood, not let off the hook.

**Be concrete.** Every abstract claim needs a concrete example within one paragraph.
Not named companies (avoid dating the book) — but specific, vivid scenarios: "a team
of eight engineers, a reorg mid-quarter, and a VP who measures success by headcount."

**Be occasionally funny.** The book covers genuinely absurd organizational behavior.
Acknowledging the absurdity is part of being honest about it.

**Avoid buzzwords.** "Psychological safety," "servant leadership," "agile mindset" —
all of these have been drained of meaning by overuse. Either define them precisely or
use plainer language. "People feel safe saying when something is wrong" is better than
"psychological safety."

## Structure

- Target **3,000–4,500 words** per chapter (tighter than a management book; readers
  are engineers who value density)
- Each chapter makes **one core argument** — not a survey of a topic, but a thesis
- **Opening**: a scene or observation that earns the reader's attention in the first
  paragraph. No slow wind-ups.
- **Sections** (4–6, with clear headers): each section advances the argument
- **The skeptic turn**: one section explicitly addresses the strongest objection
- **Closing**: implications — what should the reader *do* differently, specifically?

## What to Cite

Draw from:
- **Engineering management literature**: Larson, Fournier, DeMarco, Lister, Weinberg
- **Organizational behavior**: Weick, March, Perrow, Orton
- **Behavioral economics**: Kahneman, Thaler, Ariely — but apply to org behavior, not
  just individual decisions
- **Economic frameworks**: principal-agent theory, cost of delay, real options,
  transaction cost economics
- **Historical engineering failures and successes** — Challenger, Therac-25, the
  Mythical Man-Month, the 737 MAX — these are familiar to the audience and carry weight

Do not cite by company name when the example could become outdated or embarrassing.
Use categories: "a large financial institution," "a mid-size SaaS company," "a team
under deadline pressure." The principle is what matters, not the brand.

**Never name specific software tools, vendors, or frameworks** that could date the book.
The book must remain useful in ten years.

## Historical Parallels

Unlike the executive audience book, this audience knows computing history well. Lean
into it:
- Brooks's Law and the Mythical Man-Month (1975 — still true)
- Dijkstra, Knuth, the structured programming debates
- The rise of Unix and what it tells us about modularity and Conway's Law
- The open source movement and what it teaches about intrinsic motivation
- The agile manifesto and what happened when it became a process

These examples establish credibility with a technical audience and show that
organizational problems in engineering are not new — they're structural.

## Chapter Endings

Every chapter ends with a section called **"What Changes Monday"** (or a variant).
This section is 2–4 short paragraphs, direct second person, telling the reader:
1. The one thing to stop doing (or notice)
2. The one thing to start doing (or try)
3. The longer-term shift if they take this seriously

This section is the most important one — it's what the reader will remember and
potentially share.
