Chapter 5: Strategy

Strategy: The Judgment That Remains

The Analyst's Trap

In the spring of 1944, the Allied planners at SHAEF possessed the most sophisticated analytical apparatus in the history of warfare. Operations Research units had calculated optimal tonnage allocations across shipping lanes. Logistics models had mapped every rail junction between the English Channel and the Rhine. Intelligence services had compiled detailed assessments of German division strength, morale, and equipment readiness. The analysis was genuinely impressive—a quantum leap beyond anything Eisenhower's predecessors could have assembled.

None of it answered the question that mattered: Normandy or Calais?

That decision—arguably the most consequential strategic choice of the twentieth century—depended on things no model could represent: a calculated deception operation built on the German High Command's own psychology, political relationships with the Free French that would shape postwar Europe, and a judgment about which coastal geography gave Allied troops the best chance to exploit a successful landing before German reserves could close. The analytical apparatus didn't fail. It succeeded in clarifying exactly where analysis ended and judgment began—and that is what made the decision visible as a decision, rather than a conclusion waiting to be drawn.

Organizations deploying AI systems in their strategy functions are discovering a version of the same dynamic. The quality of competitive intelligence has improved dramatically. Scenario models run at a speed and scale that would have required dedicated analytical teams not long ago. Regulatory environments can be mapped across jurisdictions in hours rather than months. And yet—by the measures that matter—strategic decision quality has not improved at the same rate. Companies with more analysis are not making better bets. In some cases, they are making slower ones.

The problem is not the analysis. The problem is that organizations optimized for better analysis without redesigning the process that turns analysis into decisions. They treated the bottleneck as analytical when it was always something else: the contested questions of direction, identity, and commitment that sit at the center of real strategy and have no analytical answer.

The argument of this chapter is not that AI is inadequate for strategy. It is that AI is clarifying: it removes the analytical undergrowth that has historically obscured the judgment terrain. When analysis is abundant and cheap, what becomes visible—with uncomfortable clarity—is the work that was always at the heart of strategy but was previously buried under the labor of producing the analysis. That is the work that grows more consequential, not less, in an AI-native world. And it is irreducibly human.

This is not a new trap. It is the characteristic mistake of every era that has produced a new analytical technology—and understanding why helps clarify what AI actually changes about strategy, and what it cannot.

What Strategy Actually Is (And Why the Confusion Matters)

There is a concept in organizational life that almost everyone uses and almost no one defines precisely: strategy. The word does enormous work. It describes everything from a five-year plan to a quarterly initiative to a pricing decision. This imprecision would be merely inconvenient if it didn't have practical consequences. In an era when AI accelerates the analytical components of strategy work, the imprecision becomes actively dangerous, because it makes it easy to mistake faster analysis for better strategy.

The distinction that matters most comes from Roger Martin's work on the "choice cascade." Strategy, properly understood, is a set of integrated choices: what does winning look like for us, in which arenas will we compete, and how will we win in those arenas? Each of these is a choice, not a conclusion. It requires judgment about what matters, not just evidence about what is true.

AI systems are exceptionally good at the analytical infrastructure that informs these choices. They can map competitive arenas with granularity. They can stress-test assumptions about how to win. They can surface historical precedents for analogous strategic situations. What they cannot do is resolve the aspiration. They cannot tell you whether your company should care more about market share or margin, whether it should define "winning" at the product level or the ecosystem level, or whether a contested market is worth the disruption its entry would cause to existing relationships. These are value judgments, and value judgments require an objective function—a prior view of what matters—that AI systems can optimize toward but cannot establish.

Henry Mintzberg drew a related distinction that becomes more important, not less, in an AI context: the difference between deliberate strategy, which is planned and executed as intended, and emergent strategy, which arises from patterns in what an organization actually does and what the environment actually rewards. Most strategy literature—and most AI applied to strategy—focuses on the deliberate side: the planning, the modeling, the option generation. But some of the most consequential strategic insights come from emergence: the product line that customers keep buying despite low marketing spend, the market segment that keeps growing while the core business plateaus, the capability that keeps proving useful in unexpected contexts.

Emergent strategy requires noticing—a form of organizational attention that is not accelerated by faster scenario modeling. In fact, the risk of AI-augmented strategy is the opposite: that organizations become so efficient at deliberate planning that they stop leaving space for emergence. They optimize what they can measure and model, and they miss what they can only sense.

The practical implication follows from this distinction: when your strategy process produces an answer, ask whether it is a conclusion drawn from analysis or a choice made in spite of analytical uncertainty. Only the latter is strategy. AI's contribution is to make that distinction unmistakably clear—and in doing so, to raise the stakes of the choice that remains.

The New Division of Labor

When the printing press made books cheap in the fifteenth century, it did not make scholars irrelevant. It made scholarship harder. Before Gutenberg, the scribe's value was partly in the physical production of text—a laborious, skilled act. After Gutenberg, that value collapsed. What remained—and became more consequential—was the interpretive work: what does this text mean, how does it relate to other texts, what implications does it carry for contested theological and political questions? The scholars who adapted understood that their job had shifted from production to interpretation. The ones who did not continued to compete on the dimension that had become a commodity.

The analogy for strategy functions is direct. The work of populating a competitive analysis—gathering pricing signals, tracking new entrants, synthesizing regulatory developments, modeling demand scenarios—is increasingly AI-native work, not because the analysis is unimportant, but because it is a form of production that AI has made abundant. The strategist's value can no longer rest substantially on the ability to gather and synthesize information. It must rest on interpretation: what does this information mean about where we should go?

Consider what this means in practice. A major global retailer discovered it when it invested heavily in AI-powered competitive intelligence. The quality of its analysis improved dramatically. It could track pricing signals in near real time, model demand across markets with more granularity than ever, and flag supplier disruptions before they reached the business. And then something unexpected happened: strategic decision-making slowed. The problem was structural. Better analysis generated more options. More options required more stakeholder review. More stakeholder review required more alignment. The organization had accelerated analysis without redesigning decisions, and the result was a more elegant version of paralysis.

The fix was not analytical. It was organizational: clear decision rights, time-boxed commitments, and a structural separation between the meeting where synthesis happens and the meeting where decisions are made. In practice, this meant the synthesis meeting ended with a summary of the key options and the critical uncertainties attached to each—not a recommendation. Executives were given a set period, typically a week, to absorb the synthesis independently before the decision meeting convened. In the decision meeting, the question was not "what does the analysis say?" but "given what we know, what are we choosing?" The distinction proved decisive. What had been a slow, consensus-seeking process became a faster, more accountable one—not because the analysis changed, but because the organization learned to use it differently.

A major international bank experienced a related dynamic after the global financial crisis. AI systems could map the full regulatory landscape across jurisdictions and flag incompatibilities in the bank's current strategy with emerging compliance requirements. The analysis was technically impressive. What it could not resolve was a prior question: did the bank want to invest in adapting its operating model to serve contested regulatory environments, or did it want to exit certain markets? That decision depended on what kind of institution the bank wanted to be—a judgment about identity that no regulatory model could make.

The new division of labor in strategy functions has a clear shape. AI handles competitive intelligence, scenario modeling, stress-testing of assumptions, and option generation. Human strategists handle question-setting—deciding which questions the analysis should address and which options the organization should seriously consider. They handle synthesis—integrating analytical outputs with organizational context, stakeholder dynamics, and identity. And they handle commitment—making the bet, communicating it, and creating the organizational will to execute.

This is not a more comfortable job. It is a harder one. The analytical work that previously consumed large portions of a strategy team's time was also, in a certain sense, cover—it justified the team's existence, gave it something measurable to produce, and deferred the harder work of actually deciding. When AI handles that work, what remains is the part that strategy functions have always found most difficult: the human conversation about direction.

New Cadences: From Planning to Sensing

The annual strategy cycle is one of the great accidents of corporate life. It is not the natural rhythm of competition—markets don't care about fiscal calendars. It is an artifact of the cost of analysis. Comprehensive competitive assessments, financial models, and scenario analyses were expensive to produce. Producing them once a year was what organizations could afford. The annual cycle persisted not because it was strategically optimal but because more frequent cycles were uneconomical.

AI changes the economics of analysis without changing the underlying need for strategic judgment. Analysis can now be continuous. But the response to continuous analysis is not a continuous decision process—it is a redesigned one.

The decisive step in competitive strategy is rarely observation or action—it is orientation, the integration of new information with existing mental models and organizational judgment. This insight comes from the military strategist John Boyd's framework for combat decision-making, but its application to organizational strategy is direct: the organizations that win are not those with the best information, but those that integrate new information with their understanding of the environment faster and more accurately than their competitors. AI dramatically accelerates the observation step. It does not compress orientation—the step where an executive team integrates new information with its understanding of the organization's capabilities, its stakeholders' constraints, and its own identity.

This has a practical implication for strategy cadences. The shift should not be from annual planning to continuous planning—that way lies planning theater, where the organization is always analyzing and never deciding. The shift should be from annual planning to a two-speed rhythm: continuous sensing, punctuated by periodic decision.

Sensing is a different organizational capability than planning. Planning produces documents and decisions. Sensing produces questions and signals. A sensing-capable organization has people whose job is to notice things that don't fit the current model—market behaviors that the strategy didn't predict, competitive moves that the scenario analysis didn't include, customer patterns that don't appear in the data but are visible to people close to the customer. This is Mintzberg's emergent strategy made into an organizational discipline: not leaving strategy entirely to emergence, but deliberately creating channels for emergence to surface.

The rhythm this suggests is roughly quarterly for strategic sensing—structured conversations about what the organization is noticing that the current strategy doesn't explain—and annual for strategic commitment, where those signals are integrated into explicit choices. This is faster than most organizations currently operate on the commitment side and more disciplined than most operate on the sensing side. Both changes are required. AI makes faster analysis available without making better sensing automatic.

There is a historical precedent for what happens to organizations that confuse faster analysis with better sensing. The scenario planning work produced by American strategic analysts in the 1960s was technically impressive and often strategically beside the point. The capability to model alternative futures had outrun the organizational wisdom to interpret them. The scenarios told decision-makers what was possible in theory; they did not help decision-makers know which signals in the present were pointing toward which futures. The cognitive work of connecting present signals to future outcomes remained stubbornly human.

The practical implication for the executive: don't measure your strategy function only by the quality of its models. Measure it also by the quality of its questions—the signals it notices before they become obvious, the assumptions it challenges before they become constraints.

The Skeptic's Case—And Why It Fails

The strongest version of the argument against this chapter's thesis is worth engaging seriously, because it is not obviously wrong.

Here it is: AI systems will not remain merely analytical. As they improve, they will develop the capacity to recommend strategic directions—not just model options but rank them, weight them against articulated preferences, and produce recommendations that integrate analytical quality with strategic coherence. At that point, the argument goes, the strategist's role is not amplified but replaced. The judgment problem dissolves into a preference specification problem: you tell the system what you care about, and it tells you what to do.

This argument deserves a philosophical answer, not just a technical one.

Strategy is not optimization. Optimization requires a specified objective function—a clear statement of what you are trying to maximize. The most important strategic questions are precisely the questions where the objective function is contested. A media company choosing between defining itself as a content creator or a distribution platform is not optimizing toward a shared objective; it is resolving a disagreement about objectives. A consumer goods company deciding whether to divest heritage brands that represent the company's founding story is not choosing the highest-NPV option; it is deciding who it is willing to become. These decisions require the prior question to be answered: what are we for? That question cannot be delegated to a system that requires an answer to it before it can function.

There is also a second argument, less philosophical but perhaps more practical: the social legitimacy of strategic decisions matters independently of their analytical quality. Organizations follow direction partly because they trust the humans who set it. A strategy that emerges from a rigorous AI-supported process but is announced without the human work of coalition-building, narrative, and commitment is a strategy that will stall in execution. The communicative and political work of strategy—persuading the organization that this direction is worth the sacrifice it requires—is not separable from the analytical work. It is strategy's other half, and it is entirely human.

There is a second objection, less philosophical but structurally important to address: AI may not replace the central strategist, but it might make the central strategy function less necessary by enabling better distributed decision-making. If every business unit manager can access the same quality of competitive intelligence that only the strategy team previously had, why maintain a centralized strategy function? Doesn't AI democratize strategy in a way that argues for its distribution rather than its elevation?

This objection is partially correct and should not be dismissed. AI does enable better distributed decision-making at the operational level. Business unit managers who previously relied on central strategy teams for market intelligence can now access it directly, and they should. The planning assumption, the market size estimate, the competitor capability assessment—these can and should be democratized.

But the problem that the strategy function exists to solve at the top of an organization is not an information problem. It is a coordination and commitment problem: how does a complex organization make integrated choices that sacrifice local optimization for systemic coherence? That problem is not solved by giving everyone better information. It may be made harder, because more people in the organization now have strong analytical views in favor of their local priorities, and someone still has to integrate those views into a direction the whole organization can move in. The more analysis is distributed, the more the center's value lies in synthesis, arbitration, and the commitment to a single direction. That is the essence of what strategy functions do at the top of organizations, and AI does not replace it—it makes it more necessary.

The Long Horizon Problem

There is a category of strategic decision where the limits of AI analysis are sharpest and the stakes of human judgment are highest: decisions with thirty- or forty-year consequences.

An aerospace manufacturer choosing which propulsion technology to develop is making a bet that will define the company's competitive position for decades. The relevant variables—which technologies will mature, which regulatory frameworks will persist, which geopolitical relationships will anchor defense and aviation demand—have no historical analogue close enough to support confident modeling. AI can check internal consistency and stress-test assumptions, but it cannot produce the judgment that these are the right assumptions to stress-test or that the correct range of scenarios has been included.

A consumer goods company deciding whether to invest in next-generation manufacturing capability is making a bet on how consumer preferences, supply chain economics, and regulatory pressures will interact over twenty years. The data that would ground a confident prediction does not exist; it will only exist after the decision has been made.

These long-horizon decisions are qualitatively different from analytical decisions that require better data. They require navigating what strategists call uncertainty rather than risk: situations where the future states of the world cannot be listed, let alone assigned probabilities. The distinction matters because all of AI's analytical power is suited to risk—situations where the relevant outcomes can be enumerated and their likelihoods estimated from historical data. It offers diminishing help with genuine uncertainty—decisions about novel situations where the data, by definition, doesn't reach far enough to be decisive.

The important implication here is not merely that AI cannot help with long-horizon decisions. It is that AI makes the distinction between risk and uncertainty more legible. When you have used AI to model everything that can be modeled, and the answer is still deeply uncertain, you know you have reached judgment territory. That is itself information—valuable information about where to direct executive attention and where to stop waiting for analytical resolution that will not come.

For executives in long-cycle industries, this calls for an explicit discipline: identify which of your major decisions fall into genuine uncertainty rather than calculable risk, and protect those decisions from the pressure to produce analytical justification that the situation does not support. The most dangerous response to AI-accelerated analysis is to demand more analysis when you have already reached the edge of what analysis can resolve.

What the Executive Should Do Differently

The analysis above points to five changes in how executives should approach strategy. These are not technology recommendations. They are process and organizational design changes that become urgent precisely when AI takes over the analytical work.

First: redesign your decision process before your analytical tools. Most organizations deploying AI in strategy functions have done the reverse. They have improved the quality and speed of analysis while leaving the decision process intact. The result is that better analysis flows into an unchanged process and produces the same decisions, faster, with more options to review. The value of AI in strategy comes not from better analysis alone but from redesigning decisions around the assumption that analysis is cheap and fast. That means clearer decision rights, time-boxed commitments, and explicit conversation about which questions require senior judgment and which can be delegated.

Second: separate the synthesis meeting from the decision meeting. When AI accelerates analysis, there is a temptation to collapse synthesis and decision into a single process—to present AI-generated options in a meeting and make the decision in the same session. This is a mistake. Synthesis requires absorption: time for the executive team to integrate new information with their existing understanding of the organization and its context. Decision requires commitment: the willingness to choose among options that are not cleanly superior to each other. These are different cognitive modes. They belong in different conversations, separated by at least enough time for the synthesis to be metabolized.

Third: identify the two or three questions in your strategy that require genuine judgment and protect time to work on them. Every strategy contains analytical questions and judgment questions. Analytical questions can be delegated to AI systems. Judgment questions—the ones about aspiration, identity, and commitment under uncertainty—cannot. Most strategy processes bury the judgment questions under analytical work, which means they get addressed quickly at the end of long planning sessions by exhausted executives. AI's gift to strategy is to surface those questions earlier and more clearly. Find them. Schedule real time to work on them—not as an agenda item at the end of a planning offsite, but as the primary work of the strategy process.

Fourth: build the organization's sensing capability, not just its modeling capability. The investment most organizations are making in AI for strategy is heavily weighted toward modeling: better scenario analysis, better competitive intelligence synthesis, better financial modeling. These are valuable. But they address the deliberate side of strategy, not the emergent side. Organizations also need investment in sensing: the habits and structures that allow signals to surface from the edges of the business before they're obvious enough to appear in the data. This means rewarding people who notice things that don't fit the current model, creating forums for those signals to reach people with the authority to act on them, and measuring not just the quality of the strategy but the speed at which the organization updates its strategic understanding when the environment shifts.

Fifth: be explicit about what you are betting on and why. AI cannot make bets. But it can make implicit bets visible. Most organizations embed strategic assumptions that are never stated as bets—about the stability of the competitive landscape, the behavior of key customers, the trajectory of regulatory environments—without ever acknowledging them as assumptions. AI's analytical capability is well-suited to surfacing these: running the current strategy against scenarios where those assumptions fail, and asking what happens. The output of that analysis is not a decision. It is a forcing mechanism for the conversation that should have happened earlier: what are we assuming, are we willing to make that assumption explicit, and are we prepared for the cost of being wrong?

The Strategist's Future

The printing press did not eliminate the theologian. It changed what the theologian's job required: not the ability to access and copy text, but the ability to interpret it once everyone had access to the same texts. When Luther nailed his theses to the door in 1517—some sixty years after Gutenberg's Bible—he was not making a discovery that the printing press had enabled. He was making an argument that the printing press had made unavoidable. Once information was free, the contested question was never about the information. It was always about what the information meant.

The strategic analyst of the previous era—whose value came substantially from the ability to synthesize competitive intelligence that was laborious and expensive to gather—is facing the same transition. The value of that synthesis is collapsing. What remains, and what grows more consequential as analysis becomes abundant, is the work that was always at the heart of strategy but was previously obscured by the analytical labor surrounding it: the act of choosing direction when the destination is contested, making the bet when the data runs out, and committing the organization to a course of action that sacrifices local optimization for systemic coherence.

This is not a comfortable transition. It is genuinely harder to work at the frontier of judgment than to work at the frontier of analysis. Analysis produces outputs. Judgment produces accountability. When AI takes over the analysis, there is nowhere left to hide behind the work of producing it.

But it is also a clarification long overdue. Strategy has always been, at its core, an act of will—the decision about who we are and what we are trying to win, made under uncertainty, with consequences that will outlast the analysis. AI doesn't change what strategy is. It removes the cover that made it possible to pretend otherwise.

The strategist who grasps this early has a significant advantage. They will stop investing in being the best analyst in the room and start investing in being the best decision-maker—the executive who can hold multiple incommensurable considerations simultaneously, build organizational commitment to a direction that is not analytically certain, and recognize, in the pattern of what is emerging, the future that the models haven't yet predicted. That executive will be more valuable in an AI-native world than in the one that preceded it. The demands will be higher, the accountability more direct, and the quality of the judgment more visible. That is, in the end, what it means for a job to matter.
