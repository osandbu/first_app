Chapter 4: Why Organizations Change More Slowly Than Technology

Why Organizations Change More Slowly Than Technology

The Dynamo Fallacy

In the 1880s, American factories began electrifying. The decision seemed obvious: steam engines were expensive to maintain, dangerous to operate, and tied to geography. Electric motors were cleaner, more flexible, and increasingly affordable. Within two decades, the technology had spread widely through manufacturing. And then — nothing. Measured productivity gains were minimal.

The economist Paul David provided the answer in a landmark paper that has become foundational for thinking about technology transitions. Factory owners had replaced their steam engines with electric motors — but had kept everything else: the long central driveshaft, the leather belts radiating outward to each piece of machinery, the physical layout of workers arranged by proximity to the central power source rather than by the logic of the workflow. They had adopted the technology without redesigning the organization. The result was a marginally cleaner, marginally more reliable version of the same factory.

The transformation came only in the 1920s, when a new generation of factory managers began designing factories from scratch around what electricity made possible: distributed motors at each workstation, workers arranged by function rather than proximity to the engine, natural light through reorganized windows, flexible floor plans that could be reconfigured as products changed. These were not incremental improvements on the steam-era factory. They were a different thing entirely, and they required not just new technology but new imagination about what a factory was for.

The productivity gains from electrification showed up in economic statistics not in the 1890s, when the technology was adopted, but in the 1920s, when the organizational redesign finally occurred. The lag was not a failure of technology adoption. It was a failure of organizational imagination — the inability to see that the new technology required not just replacement of the old mechanism but reinvention of the system around it.

This is the central problem of the current AI transition, and it is being repeated with striking fidelity. Organizations are buying AI systems. They are automating existing workflows. They are producing better, faster versions of the same reports, analyses, and processes they were already running. And they are wondering, with some frustration, why the gains are not more dramatic. The question they are not asking — because it is harder and more disorienting — is the one that eventually unlocked electricity's potential: if we were designing this organization from scratch, with AI's capabilities as a given, what would it look like?

That question is the executive work of the current moment.

Three Sources of Institutional Resistance

Organizations do not resist change because they are irrational. They resist change because they are organizations — because they have incentive systems that reward existing behaviors, identity structures that define what members value about their roles, and inertial forces that make existing processes self-perpetuating long after the rationale for them has disappeared. Understanding resistance requires distinguishing among these three sources, because each responds to a different executive intervention, and treating them as a single undifferentiated "change management problem" is why most change programs fail.

The first source is incentive misalignment. This is the most tractable and the most often confused for the other two. When an organization's performance management system, compensation structure, and promotion criteria reward behaviors that AI disrupts, people behave rationally in resisting adoption. They are not confused about what the technology can do; they understand very clearly what it does to their measured performance.

A global investment bank piloted AI systems for equity research. Analyst productivity, measured in reports per week, doubled in controlled experiments. The rollout stalled for three years — not because of technical failure, but because the performance management system still measured analysts by volume of original research produced. Analysts whose output was augmented by AI were penalized under existing evaluation criteria. The bank eventually adopted the technology, but only after redesigning the performance framework. The incentive redesign, not the technology deployment, was the enabling constraint.

A major retail chain that deployed AI systems for inventory forecasting found regional store managers systematically overriding the AI recommendations. They did not trust predictions they did not understand, and the risk of a stockout fell on them personally while the upside accrued to the corporate supply chain team. The misalignment was structural and specific: the system that bore the cost of AI errors was not the same as the system that captured AI's benefits. No amount of training or communication changed this. The incentive problem required an incentive solution.

The second source of resistance is identity. People in organizations do not just do jobs; they are members of professional communities with traditions, status hierarchies, and self-conceptions. Middle managers are not just people who coordinate work — they are the people who synthesize information and translate it for senior leadership. Partners at professional services firms are not just senior advisers — they are people whose judgment is worth paying for because it is scarce and cultivated through years of experience. When AI systems disrupt the functional basis of these identities without addressing the identity itself, resistance is not irrationality. It is self-protection.

The physician who resists AI diagnostic tools may not believe the tool is less accurate. She may be protecting her conception of what good medicine is: a relationship between a trained clinician and an individual patient, built on trust and clinical presence. That conception may be wrong, or right, or partially both. But it will not be changed by demonstrating the tool's accuracy. It requires an explicit conversation about what the profession is for in a world where some of its most valued capabilities are automatable.

The third source is inertia — the tendency of systems to continue what they were designed to do, independent of whether that behavior is still optimal. Organizations operating in a particular way for decades develop deep routines calibrated for that operating environment. A new technology that generates information faster and in different forms outpaces those routines — not because the organization is stupid, but because its meaning-making system was built for a different environment. People in organizations do not simply process information neutrally; they act first and then interpret their actions to confirm existing beliefs. AI introduces information at a speed and volume that outpaces existing interpretation routines. Organizations fail to adapt not because they are confused about the technology but because their cognitive systems are calibrated for a slower world.

A consumer packaged goods company that ran a successful AI pilot in marketing analytics found scaling fail for a structural reason: data was siloed across business units, each treating its customer data as a proprietary asset underpinning its internal budget negotiations. Sharing data with a centralized AI system would have eroded each unit's informational leverage in resource allocation discussions. Middle managers resisted not because they misunderstood AI but because they understood their own incentives perfectly. This was inertia sustained by incentive — and the identity of the business unit leader as someone whose market knowledge gave them organizational standing lay beneath both.

The Middle Management Problem

The organizational layer most likely to impede AI adoption is not the C-suite and not the frontline workforce. It is middle management. This claim is uncomfortable, and it is important.

Senior executives are typically enthusiastic about AI's strategic possibilities. Frontline workers, despite widespread concern about displacement, often adapt relatively quickly to AI-assisted tools that make their immediate work easier. The structural resistance — where AI initiatives consistently bog down, where pilot successes fail to scale — is in the middle.

The reason is not difficult to find. Middle management derives its organizational value primarily from managing information flows: synthesizing data from below, translating it for leadership above, coordinating across units that lack direct relationships. These functions are precisely what AI systems disrupt. The regional director's value was her superior synthesized picture of multiple markets. The data analyst's value was his ability to build and interpret financial models. When AI systems perform these functions, the organizational rationale for the roles is not eliminated — but it is put under pressure in a way incumbents feel immediately and viscerally.

The responses this pressure produces are not always cynical. Some middle managers resist AI adoption because they are the organizational members most exposed to its unintended consequences — and their resistance sometimes reflects genuine operational knowledge that senior leaders lack. The inventory manager who overrides AI recommendations may be protecting against failure modes the model hasn't encountered. The compliance manager who slows AI deployment in a regulated function may be protecting against liability risks the technology team hasn't modeled.

Executives who cannot distinguish between legitimate operational caution and structural self-protection will find their AI transformations consistently captured by the layer of the organization with the most to lose from them. The practical design implication: structure AI adoption initiatives to separate the team assessing technical viability from the team assessing organizational impact — and ensure the latter includes people who are not incumbents in the roles most affected. When substantive objections are raised, apply a diagnostic question: what specific outcome are you protecting against, and what would constitute sufficient evidence that the risk has been managed? If the answer is specific and falsifiable, the concern is likely genuine. If it is vague and unfalsifiable, the concern is likely structural.

Incentives Are the Architecture

The most durable management insight about organizational change is also the most consistently ignored: incentive systems are not HR concerns. They are the actual architecture of organizational behavior.

The organizational structure must follow the strategy — and the full incentive system, including performance measurement, compensation, and promotion criteria, is as much a part of the structure as the org chart. Organizations that allow their current incentive structures to constrain their AI adoption will consistently underperform those that redesign their incentives around the new strategic intent.

The legal profession's adoption of email in the 1990s provides the clearest recent example. Studies of large law firms found systematic resistance to email that had nothing to do with technical literacy. The resistance was structural: law firm economics depended on billable hours, and email — faster than letter-writing — compressed the time required to complete tasks partners had been billing by the hour for decades. Partners who resisted email were not technophobic; they were protecting a business model. The firms that adopted email fastest were those that simultaneously renegotiated their billing structures, moving toward flat fees and retainer models that rewarded efficiency rather than penalizing it. Technology adoption and business model innovation had to happen together. Neither worked without the other.

This is the principle executives must internalize for AI. Performance management systems that reward the production of analytical work will create organizations that treat AI-assisted production as a threat to their measured output. Compensation structures that reward information control will create organizations that resist AI-enabled transparency. Promotion criteria that value technical expertise over decision quality will create organizations that use AI to produce better technical outputs while atrophying the judgment skills that AI's advance makes more rather than less important.

Redesigning these structures is harder than deploying the technology, takes longer, and generates more organizational friction. But it is the constraint. Executives who have grasped this are redesigning performance systems around outcome quality rather than output volume — measuring the quality of decisions made, not the quantity of analyses produced. They are restructuring compensation to reward organizational transparency and data-sharing rather than data-hoarding. They are rewriting promotion criteria to prize decision quality and the ability to work effectively with AI-generated intelligence, rather than the ability to produce intelligence manually.

Deliberate Caution vs. Structural Avoidance

The argument so far could be read as a brief for moving faster and accepting more disruption. That reading would be wrong, and would give executives a license to do real damage.

Not all organizational slowness is failure. Executives who move carefully on AI are often doing exactly what responsible stewardship requires. They are managing real risks around data privacy, regulatory compliance, liability, reputational damage, and workforce disruption that technically enthusiastic proponents routinely underweight.

A national hospital network that implemented AI-assisted diagnostic tools and found the rollout took six years was not simply being bureaucratic. Physicians who declined to rely on AI diagnostic recommendations in cases where their clinical intuition conflicted were protecting against genuine liability exposure. The legal framework for medical malpractice had not resolved who bears responsibility when an AI-assisted diagnosis is wrong and a physician follows it. Until that framework clarified, caution was rational regardless of the technology's accuracy. The bottleneck was not organizational resistance; it was an institutional environment that had not yet adapted.

The distinction that matters is between deliberate caution and structural avoidance.

Deliberate caution is grounded in specific, articulable risks. It has an identifiable trigger for change — a resolved regulatory question, an updated liability framework, a sufficient evidence base from controlled deployment. It is proportionate: the caution is matched to the magnitude of the risk, not to the comfort of the status quo. And it is visible: the people exercising it can explain exactly what they are waiting for.

Structural avoidance has none of these properties. It invokes risk without specificity. It cannot name the conditions under which adoption would proceed. Its real function is to protect existing arrangements — job security, budget authority, organizational influence — under the cover of prudent risk management.

The diagnostic question executives must apply, to themselves and to the people raising objections, is simply: what would it take to change your mind? If the answer is specific and achievable, the caution is probably genuine. If the answer is vague, shifting, or structurally impossible, the avoidance is probably structural.

There is also a caution on the other side. A regional telecommunications company that adopted AI customer service tools achieved cost reductions — and three years later found customer satisfaction had declined and churn had increased. The AI system had been optimized for resolution speed, which the operations team measured. What customers valued in complex billing disputes was feeling heard — a qualitative outcome no one had measured or encoded in the system's objectives. The company had moved quickly without first asking what problem it was actually trying to solve. Speed is not a substitute for clarity. Moving fast in the wrong direction is not better than moving slowly in the right one.

The Redesign Imperative

The executives who will capture the value of AI are not those who adopt AI systems most aggressively. They are those who use the AI transition as a forcing function to redesign their organizations around the informational and capability environment that AI creates.

This is not comfortable. Organizational redesign is harder, messier, more politically difficult, and more personally threatening than technology adoption. It requires naming structural changes that produce winners and losers. It requires rewriting the implicit contracts that organizational life rests on — the understandings about what roles are for, what expertise is valued, and what career paths lead where. It requires, in short, letting strategy lead and letting structure follow, even when the existing structure has significant institutional momentum.

The honest version of the historical precedent is not reassuring to the executives of large, established organizations. The new organizational forms that unlocked the printing press's potential — the periodical, the scientific journal, the pamphlet, the commercial catalogue — were invented primarily by outsiders to the established institutional order. The monasteries and universities that had controlled information production before Gutenberg did not pioneer the new forms; they were eventually displaced by them. The organizations best positioned to exploit AI are not necessarily the largest or most established. They are the ones with the organizational flexibility to invent new forms rather than improve old ones.

For the senior executive at a large, established firm, this is the specific challenge worth sitting with: the inertia that makes your organization hard to disrupt from outside also makes it hard to redesign from inside. The assets that create organizational stability — culture, relationships, established processes, predictable incentive structures — are the same assets that resist the redesign AI makes strategically urgent. There is no clever move that dissolves this tension. There is only the leadership decision to make the redesign a priority, to absorb the organizational friction it generates, and to build the kind of institution that earns its position in the new environment rather than simply defending its position from the old one.

The established organizations that have navigated prior technological transitions successfully shared one characteristic: a leader willing to question whether the organization's current form was a feature or a historical artifact. The factories that eventually adopted distributed power did not do it because they were braver or smarter than the ones that didn't. They did it because, at the right moment, someone asked the question that the technology had made newly urgent: if we no longer need to organize around the old constraint, what do we want to build instead?

The bottleneck is not technical. It never was. The AI systems are available, effective, and becoming more capable. The bottleneck is human — which is exactly where executive responsibility has always lived.

The window for asking that question, with the combination of urgency and clarity that the current moment provides, will not remain open indefinitely. The organizations that ask it first, with the most seriousness, will set the terms for everyone else. The ones that don't will find themselves, in time, explaining to their boards why competitors who were smaller and less established ten years ago have organizational capabilities that can no longer be matched.

The question is not whether to redesign. It is whether you will do it deliberately or have it done to you.
