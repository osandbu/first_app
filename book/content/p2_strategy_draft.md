# Strategy: The Judgment That Remains

## The Analyst's Trap

In the spring of 1944, the Allied planners at SHAEF possessed the most sophisticated analytical apparatus in the history of warfare. Operations Research units had calculated optimal tonnage allocations across shipping lanes. Logistics models had mapped every rail junction between the English Channel and the Rhine. Intelligence services had compiled detailed assessments of German division strength, morale, and equipment readiness. The analysis was genuinely impressive—a quantum leap beyond anything Eisenhower's predecessors could have assembled.

None of it answered the question that mattered: Normandy or Calais?

That decision—arguably the most consequential strategic choice of the twentieth century—depended on things no model could represent: a calculated deception operation built on the German High Command's own psychology, political relationships with the Free French that would shape postwar Europe, and a judgment about which coastal geography gave Allied troops the best chance to exploit a successful landing before German reserves could close. The analysis informed the decision. It did not make it.

Organizations deploying AI systems in their strategy functions are discovering a version of the same problem. The quality of competitive intelligence has improved dramatically. Scenario models are faster and more comprehensive than anything available five years ago. Regulatory environments can be mapped across jurisdictions in hours rather than months. And yet—by measures that matter—strategic decision quality has not improved at the same rate. Companies with more analysis are not making better bets. In some cases, they are making slower ones.

The problem is not the analysis. The problem is that organizations optimized for better analysis without redesigning the process that turns analysis into decisions. They treated the bottleneck as analytical when it was always something else: the contested questions of direction, identity, and commitment that sit at the center of real strategy and have no analytical answer.

This is not a new trap. It is the characteristic mistake of every era that has produced a new analytical technology—and understanding why helps clarify what AI actually changes about strategy, and what it cannot.

## What Strategy Actually Is (And Why the Confusion Matters)

There is a concept in organizational life that almost everyone uses and almost no one defines precisely: strategy. The word does enormous work. It describes everything from a five-year plan to a quarterly initiative to a pricing decision. This imprecision would be merely inconvenient if it didn't have practical consequences. In an era when AI accelerates the analytical components of strategy work, the imprecision becomes actively dangerous, because it makes it easy to mistake faster analysis for better strategy.

The distinction that matters most comes from Roger Martin's work on what he calls the "choice cascade." Strategy, properly understood, is a set of integrated choices: what does winning look like for us, in which arenas will we compete, and how will we win in those arenas? Each of these is a choice, not a conclusion. It requires judgment about what matters, not just evidence about what is true.

AI systems are exceptionally good at the analytical infrastructure that informs these choices. They can map competitive arenas with granularity. They can stress-test assumptions about how to win. They can surface historical precedents for analogous strategic situations. What they cannot do is resolve the aspiration. They cannot tell you whether your company should care more about market share or margin, whether it should define "winning" at the product level or the ecosystem level, or whether a contested market is worth the disruption its entry would cause to existing relationships. These are value judgments, and value judgments require an objective function—a prior view of what matters—that AI systems can optimize toward but cannot establish.

Henry Mintzberg drew a related distinction that becomes more important, not less, in an AI context: the difference between deliberate strategy, which is planned and executed as intended, and emergent strategy, which arises from patterns in what an organization actually does and what the environment actually rewards. Most strategy literature—and most AI tools applied to strategy—focuses on the deliberate side: the planning, the modeling, the option generation. But some of the most consequential strategic insights come from emergence: the product line that customers keep buying despite low marketing spend, the market segment that keeps growing while the core business plateaus, the capability that keeps proving useful in unexpected contexts.

Emergent strategy requires noticing—a form of organizational attention that is not accelerated by faster scenario modeling. In fact, the risk of AI-augmented strategy is the opposite: that organizations become so efficient at deliberate planning that they stop leaving space for emergence. They optimize what they can measure and model, and they miss what they can only sense.

Understanding this distinction is the starting point for using AI well in strategy. The aspiration and the bet are human work. The analysis is increasingly AI-native. Conflating them produces organizations that are analytically impressive and strategically confused.

## The New Division of Labor

When the printing press made books cheap in the fifteenth century, it did not make scholars irrelevant. It made scholarship harder. Before Gutenberg, the scribe's value was partly in the physical production of text—a laborious, skilled act. After Gutenberg, that value collapsed. What remained—and became more consequential—was the interpretive work: what does this text mean, how does it relate to other texts, what implications does it carry for contested theological and political questions? The scholars who adapted quickly understood that their job had shifted from production to interpretation. The ones who did not continued to compete on the dimension that had become a commodity.

The analogy for strategy functions is direct. The work of populating a competitive analysis—gathering pricing signals, tracking new entrants, synthesizing regulatory developments, modeling demand scenarios—is increasingly AI-native work. Not because the analysis is unimportant, but because it is a form of production, and AI has made production cheap. The strategist's value can no longer rest substantially on the ability to gather and synthesize information. It must rest on interpretation: what does this information mean about where we should go?

Consider what this means in practice. A major global retailer discovered it when it invested heavily in AI-powered competitive intelligence. The quality of its analysis improved dramatically. It could track pricing signals in near real time, model demand across markets with more granularity than ever, and flag supplier disruptions before they reached the business. And then something unexpected happened: strategic decision-making slowed. The problem was structural. Better analysis generated more options. More options required more stakeholder review. More stakeholder review required more alignment. The organization had accelerated analysis without redesigning decisions, and the result was a more elegant version of paralysis.

The fix was not analytical. It was organizational: clear decision rights, time-boxed commitments, and a structural separation between the meeting where synthesis happens and the meeting where decisions are made. These are not AI problems. They are governance problems that AI exposed.

A major international bank experienced a related dynamic after the global financial crisis. AI systems could map the full regulatory landscape across jurisdictions and flag incompatibilities in the bank's current strategy with emerging compliance requirements. What the analysis could not resolve was a prior question: did the bank want to invest in adapting its operating model to serve contested regulatory environments, or did it want to exit certain markets? That decision depended on what kind of institution the bank wanted to be—a judgment about identity that no regulatory model could make.

The new division of labor in strategy functions has a clear shape. AI handles competitive intelligence, scenario modeling, stress-testing of assumptions, and option generation. Human strategists handle question-setting—deciding which questions the analysis should address and which options the organization should seriously consider. They handle synthesis—integrating analytical outputs with organizational context, stakeholder dynamics, and identity. And they handle commitment—making the bet, communicating it, and creating the organizational will to execute.

This is not a more comfortable job. It is a harder one. The analytical work that previously consumed large portions of a strategy team's time was also, in a certain sense, cover—it justified the team's existence, gave it something measurable to produce, and deferred the harder work of actually deciding. When AI handles that work, what remains is the part that strategy functions have always found most difficult: the human conversation about direction.

## New Cadences: From Planning to Sensing

The annual strategy cycle is one of the great accidents of corporate life. It is not the natural rhythm of competition—markets don't care about fiscal calendars. It is an artifact of the cost of analysis. Comprehensive competitive assessments, financial models, and scenario analyses were expensive to produce. Producing them once a year was what organizations could afford. The annual cycle persisted not because it was strategically optimal but because more frequent cycles were uneconomical.

AI changes the economics of analysis without changing the underlying need for strategic judgment. Analysis can now be continuous. But the response to continuous analysis is not a continuous decision process—it is a redesigned one.

The military strategist John Boyd developed what he called the OODA loop—Observe, Orient, Decide, Act—as a framework for understanding how competitive advantage is won in fast-moving environments. His insight, developed from studying air combat, was that the loop's most consequential step is the least obvious one: Orient. Observation is the collection of data. Action is execution. But Orientation—the step where new information is integrated with existing mental models, organizational experience, and strategic intent—is where competitive advantage is actually created. Organizations that Orient faster than their competitors don't just have better information; they have better understanding.

AI dramatically accelerates Observe. It can collect and synthesize competitive signals at a rate no human team can match. It can support Act through scenario modeling of execution options. What it cannot compress is Orient—the step where an executive team integrates new information with its understanding of the organization's capabilities, its stakeholders' constraints, its own identity, and the genuinely unpredictable dynamics of its competitive environment.

This has a practical implication for strategy cadences. The shift should not be from annual planning to continuous planning—that way lies planning theater, where the organization is always analyzing and never deciding. The shift should be from annual planning to a two-speed rhythm: continuous sensing, punctuated by periodic decision.

Sensing is a different organizational capability than planning. Planning produces documents and decisions. Sensing produces questions and signals. A sensing-capable organization has people whose job is to notice things that don't fit the current model—market behaviors that the strategy didn't predict, competitive moves that the scenario analysis didn't include, customer patterns that don't appear in the data but are visible to people close to the customer. This is Mintzberg's emergent strategy made into an organizational discipline: not leaving strategy entirely to emergence, but deliberately creating channels for emergence to surface.

The rhythm that this suggests is roughly quarterly for strategic sensing—structured conversations about what the organization is noticing that the current strategy doesn't explain—and annual for strategic commitment, where those signals are integrated into explicit choices. This is faster than most organizations currently operate on the commitment side and more disciplined than most operate on the sensing side. Both changes are required. AI makes faster analysis available without making better sensing automatic.

There is a historical precedent for what happens to organizations that confuse faster analysis with better sensing. The RAND Corporation's scenario planning work in the 1960s—some of the most sophisticated strategic analysis ever produced—was technically impressive and often strategically beside the point. The capability to model scenarios had outrun the organizational wisdom to interpret them. The scenarios told decision-makers what was possible in theory; they did not help decision-makers know which signals in the present were pointing toward which futures. The cognitive work of connecting present signals to future outcomes remained stubbornly human.

## The Skeptic's Case—And Why It Fails

The strongest version of the argument against this chapter's thesis is worth engaging seriously, because it is not obviously wrong.

Here it is: AI systems will not remain merely analytical. As they improve, they will develop the capacity to recommend strategic directions—not just model options but rank them, weight them against articulated preferences, and produce recommendations that integrate analytical quality with strategic coherence. At that point, the argument goes, the strategist's role is not amplified but replaced. The judgment problem dissolves into a preference specification problem: you tell the system what you care about, and it tells you what to do.

This argument deserves a philosophical answer, not just a technical one.

Strategy is not optimization. Optimization requires a specified objective function—a clear statement of what you are trying to maximize. The most important strategic questions are precisely the questions where the objective function is contested. A media company choosing between defining itself as a content creator or a distribution platform is not optimizing toward a shared objective; it is resolving a disagreement about objectives. A consumer goods company deciding whether to divest heritage brands is not choosing the highest-NPV option; it is deciding who it is willing to become. These decisions require the prior question to be answered: what are we for? That question cannot be delegated to a system that requires an answer to it before it can function.

There is also a second argument, less philosophical but perhaps more practical: the social legitimacy of strategic decisions matters independently of their analytical quality. Organizations follow direction partly because they trust the humans who set it. A strategy that emerges from a rigorous AI-supported process but is announced without the human work of coalition-building, narrative, and commitment is a strategy that will stall in execution. The communicative and political work of strategy—persuading the organization that this direction is worth the sacrifice it requires—is not separable from the analytical work. It is strategy's other half, and it is entirely human.

A second objection deserves acknowledgment: AI may not replace the central strategist, but it might make the central strategy function less necessary by enabling better distributed decision-making. If every business unit manager can access the same quality of competitive intelligence that only the strategy team previously had, why maintain a centralized strategy function at all? Doesn't AI democratize strategy in a way that argues for its distribution rather than its elevation?

This objection is partially correct and should not be dismissed. AI does enable better distributed decision-making at the operational level. Business unit managers who previously relied on central strategy teams for market intelligence can now access it directly, and they should. The planning assumption, the market size estimate, the competitor capability assessment—these can be democratized, and they should be.

But the problem that the strategy function exists to solve at the top of an organization is not an information problem. It is a coordination and commitment problem: how does a complex organization make integrated choices that sacrifice local optimization for systemic coherence? That problem is not solved by giving everyone better information. It may be made harder, because more people in the organization now have strong analytical views in favor of their local priorities, and someone still has to integrate those views into a direction the whole organization can move in. The more analysis is distributed, the more the center's value lies in synthesis, arbitration, and the commitment to a single direction. That is the essence of what strategy functions do at the top of organizations, and AI does not replace it.

## The Long Horizon Problem

There is a category of strategic decision where the limits of AI analysis are sharpest and the stakes of human judgment are highest: decisions with thirty- or forty-year consequences.

An aerospace manufacturer choosing which propulsion technology to develop is making a bet that will define the company's competitive position for decades. The relevant variables—which technologies will mature, which regulatory frameworks will persist, which geopolitical relationships will anchor defense and aviation demand—have no historical analogue close enough to support confident modeling. AI can check internal consistency and stress-test assumptions, but it cannot produce the judgment that these assumptions are the right ones to stress-test or that the correct range of scenarios has been included.

A consumer goods company deciding whether to invest in next-generation manufacturing capability is making a bet on how consumer preferences, supply chain economics, and regulatory pressures will interact over twenty years. The data that would ground a confident prediction does not exist; it will only exist after the decision has been made.

These long-horizon decisions are not only more consequential than routine strategic analysis—they are qualitatively different. They require what the philosopher Frank Knight called "uncertainty" rather than "risk": a situation where the future states of the world cannot be listed, let alone assigned probabilities. Knight's distinction matters because all of AI's analytical power is suited to risk—to situations where the relevant outcomes can be enumerated and their likelihoods estimated from data. It is of limited help with uncertainty—with decisions about genuinely novel situations where the data, by definition, doesn't reach.

This is where the strategist's irreplaceability is most clearly not a gap that AI will eventually close. The limit is not computational. It is ontological: the future doesn't exist yet, and no amount of historical data resolves what happens in genuinely novel situations. The executive who makes a thirty-year bet is not producing an analytical output. They are making an act of will—committing the organization to a direction under conditions where the commitment itself is part of what shapes the outcome. That is not an AI function. It is the oldest function of leadership.

## What the Executive Should Do Differently

The analysis above points to five changes in how executives should approach strategy. These are not technology recommendations. They are process and organizational design changes that become urgent when AI takes over the analytical work.

**First: redesign your decision process before your analytical tools.** Most organizations deploying AI in strategy functions have done the reverse. They have improved the quality and speed of analysis while leaving the decision process intact. The result is that better analysis flows into an unchanged process and produces the same decisions, faster. The value of AI in strategy comes not from better analysis but from redesigning decisions around the assumption that analysis is cheap and fast. That means clearer decision rights, time-boxed commitments, and explicit conversation about which questions require senior judgment and which can be delegated.

**Second: separate the synthesis meeting from the decision meeting.** When AI accelerates analysis, there is a temptation to collapse synthesis and decision into a single process—to present the AI-generated options in a meeting and make the decision in the same meeting. This is a mistake. Synthesis requires absorption: time for the executive team to integrate new information with their existing understanding of the organization and its context. Decision requires commitment: the willingness to choose among options that are not cleanly superior to each other. These are different cognitive modes. They belong in different conversations, separated by at least enough time for the synthesis to be metabolized.

**Third: identify the two or three questions in your strategy that require genuine judgment and protect time to work on them.** Every strategy contains analytical questions and judgment questions. Analytical questions can be delegated to AI systems. Judgment questions—the ones about aspiration, identity, and commitment under uncertainty—cannot. Most strategy processes bury the judgment questions under analytical work, which means they get addressed quickly at the end of long planning sessions by exhausted executives. AI's gift to strategy is to surface those questions earlier and more clearly. Use it. Find the questions that can't be answered analytically and schedule real time to work on them—not as an agenda item at the end of a planning offsite, but as the primary work of the strategy process.

**Fourth: build the organization's sensing capability, not just its modeling capability.** The investment most organizations are making in AI for strategy is heavily weighted toward modeling: better scenario analysis, better competitive intelligence synthesis, better financial modeling. These are valuable. But they address the deliberate side of strategy, not the emergent side. Organizations also need investment in sensing: the organizational habits and structures that allow signals to surface from the edges of the business, before they're obvious enough to appear in the data. This means rewarding people who notice things that don't fit the current model, creating forums for those signals to be heard by people with the authority to act on them, and measuring not just the quality of the strategy but the speed at which the organization updates its strategic understanding when the environment shifts.

**Fifth: be explicit about what you are betting on and why.** AI cannot make bets. But it can make implicit bets visible. Most organizations have strategic assumptions that are never stated as bets—assumptions about the stability of the competitive landscape, the behavior of key customers, the trajectory of regulatory environments—that are embedded in the strategy without being acknowledged as assumptions. AI's analytical capability is well-suited to surfacing these: running the strategy against scenarios where those assumptions fail, and asking what happens. The output of that analysis is not a decision. It is a forcing mechanism for the conversation that should have happened earlier: what are we assuming, are we willing to make that assumption explicit, and are we prepared for the cost of being wrong?

## The Strategist's Future

The printing press did not eliminate the theologian. It changed what the theologian's job required: not the ability to access and copy text, but the ability to interpret it when everyone had access to the same texts. The strategic analyst of the previous era—whose value came from the ability to synthesize competitive intelligence that was laborious and expensive to gather—is facing the same transition. The value of that synthesis is collapsing. What remains, and what grows more consequential as analysis becomes abundant, is the work that was always at the heart of strategy but was previously obscured by the analytical labor surrounding it: the act of choosing direction when the destination is contested, making the bet when the data runs out, and committing the organization to a course of action that sacrifices the local for the systemic.

This is not a comfortable transition. It is genuinely harder to work at the frontier of judgment than to work at the frontier of analysis. Analysis produces outputs; judgment produces accountability. When AI takes over the analysis, there is nowhere to hide.

But it is also a clarification. Strategy has always been, at its core, a human act of will—the decision about who we are and what we are trying to win, made under uncertainty, with consequences that will outlast the analysis. AI doesn't change what strategy is. It removes the cover that made it possible to pretend otherwise.

The strategist who grasps this early has a significant advantage. They will stop investing in being the best analyst in the room and start investing in being the best decision-maker—the executive who can hold multiple incommensurable considerations simultaneously, who can build organizational commitment to a direction that is not analytically certain, and who can recognize, in the pattern of what is emerging, the future that the models haven't yet predicted. That executive will be more valuable in an AI-native world, not less. The work will just look different than it did before.
