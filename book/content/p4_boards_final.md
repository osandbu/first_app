# What Boards Will Expect

In the early months of 2002, Congress was still absorbing the wreckage of Enron and WorldCom when it passed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. The legislation was widely described as the most significant expansion of corporate governance requirements since the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. Among its provisions: CEOs and CFOs now faced personal criminal liability for certifying false financial statements. Audit committees were required to include financially literate independent directors. Material weaknesses in internal controls had to be disclosed publicly.

What changed was not the underlying importance of financial integrity—that had always mattered. What changed was the expectation of board engagement. Boards could no longer treat financial oversight as a formality—a function they nominally supervised but effectively delegated to management and auditors. After Sarbanes-Oxley, they were required to understand the control environment well enough to certify it, or face personal legal exposure for the failure. The result was structural: audit committees were reconstituted with specific expertise requirements, questioning frameworks were developed and institutionalized, and the relationship between boards and management on financial matters was permanently altered.

AI is following a similar arc. The governance apparatus has not yet formalized—there is no AI equivalent of Sarbanes-Oxley, and it may not arrive for several years. But the underlying dynamic is identical: a strategically material capability is being deployed across organizations at scale, and the governance structures responsible for oversight have not yet developed the rigor to match the deployment. The question is not whether that rigor will arrive—history is clear that it will—but whether boards and executives will build it before it is forced on them, or after. This chapter argues for before, and is specific about what that means.

## The Governance Gap

There is a consistent pattern across every major technology transition of the past century: the gap between the pace of deployment and the development of governance structures to manage it. Nuclear technology was deployed before radiation protection standards were adequate. The internet proliferated before data privacy frameworks were coherent. Algorithmic trading expanded before market structure regulations caught up.

AI is inside this same pattern, but the governance gap is larger and the stakes higher than in most previous transitions. An AI system making consequential decisions at organizational scale—lending approvals, hiring screens, medical triage, supply chain routing—introduces risk categories that existing governance frameworks were not designed to assess: model drift, bias amplification, novel failure modes with no historical precedent, and the specific challenge of accountability when a decision was made by a system rather than a person.

The gap between organizations deploying AI at scale and organizations with board-level AI governance processes is wider than the deployment gap itself. This is not unusual for an early-stage technology—the same survey taken of cybersecurity oversight in 2010 would have shown a comparable gap. Research on cybersecurity committee formation at major public companies shows an average of approximately eight years from first identifying cybersecurity as a material risk to establishing formal board-level oversight structures. AI risk may compress this timeline given its higher visibility and faster pace of change—but the compression will come from pressure, not from boards acting voluntarily at the frontier.

The boards that build AI governance infrastructure in advance will have a structural advantage over those that wait for a forcing event. They will identify concentration risks, accountability gaps, and competitive vulnerabilities before they become crises. They will send clearer signals to management about what is expected, creating better-aligned investment decisions. And they will be better positioned when the regulatory moment arrives—able to certify their oversight structures because they built them ahead of the requirement.

There is a practical objection to this forward-looking posture that deserves direct acknowledgment: most boards will not prioritize AI governance investment without a forcing event. This is historically accurate. Sarbanes-Oxley was triggered by Enron. Cybersecurity committee formation accelerated after specific high-profile breaches became public. The argument for building governance infrastructure in advance of the forcing event is not that boards are unusually virtuous. It is that the forcing event for AI governance will be expensive and reputationally damaging in ways that differ from prior technology crises—because AI errors often operate at scale before they surface, affecting many decisions before any single failure triggers scrutiny. The institution that has thought through accountability before the error responds quickly and credibly. The one that has not improvises under pressure, in public.

The governance gap is not a technical problem. It is a board competency and process problem. Boards are capable of developing rigorous oversight in domains they do not fully understand technically—they have done it for financial controls, cybersecurity, and environmental risk. AI requires the same approach: not technical literacy, but the ability to ask substantive questions about a domain that has become strategically material.

## What Boards Are Currently Asking—and What They Should Ask Instead

In most boardrooms today, the AI question takes a recognizable form: management presents a strategy document or investment case for AI deployment, the board asks whether the company has a coherent AI strategy, management presents a roadmap, and the board accepts or adjusts the investment allocation.

This is insufficient. Not because the opportunity framing is wrong—AI does represent significant opportunity—but because it mirrors the posture boards took toward financial reporting before Sarbanes-Oxley: treating a materially consequential function as primarily management's domain, with board engagement limited to resource allocation decisions.

A more rigorous board engagement with AI strategy organizes around three dimensions, each of which generates a different set of questions.

**Competitive position:** Is the organization building AI capability that will be strategically material, or is it deploying AI tactically without a coherent theory of how it changes the competitive position? The board's questions here are not operational. They are strategic: How does AI change the basis of competition in this industry? Who are the AI-native competitors or entrants who could threaten our business model? If a competitor deployed AI more effectively than we have over the next three years, where specifically would we lose position? A credible answer to these questions requires real strategic engagement—not a deployment roadmap but a competitive theory.

For executives preparing for this conversation: the competitive theory must exist and must be defensible before the board asks for it. It is not a PowerPoint slide; it is a clear articulation of where AI changes who wins in your market and what your organization is doing specifically about that.

**Risk architecture:** Who is accountable for AI decisions, and how are errors surfaced and remediated? This is the governance question most often absent from board discussions. When an AI system contributes to a consequential error—a lending decision that violates fair lending law, a hiring screen that produces systematic bias, a clinical support system that generates an incorrect recommendation at scale—the board needs a clear accountability structure. Who was the human accountable for the decision the AI informed? What is the audit trail? How was the error identified, escalated, and remediated?

A large healthcare system's board made this question concrete by requiring management to define—before deployment—the accountability architecture for every AI system touching clinical decisions: which human holds final decision authority, what the audit trail looks like, and how errors surface. When a diagnostic support system later produced a systematic error, this pre-established structure allowed the organization to respond quickly and credibly. Boards that had not done this work faced the same clinical AI systems with no clear accountability architecture when something went wrong.

**Organizational readiness:** Does the organization have the talent, culture, and infrastructure to execute on its AI strategy, or is it investing in technology while underinvesting in the organizational capability to use it? What percentage of senior leaders can articulate how AI is changing their functional area and what they are doing differently as a result? How is the organization measuring learning and adaptation, not just deployment? What is the talent strategy for the combination of judgment and AI fluency that AI-native execution requires?

A major retailer's audit committee, in its annual enterprise risk review, identified a concentration risk the management team had not framed as such: the company's pricing, inventory, and customer personalization systems had all migrated to AI systems from a single vendor ecosystem. The audit committee challenged management using its standard vendor concentration lens—the same questions it would ask about supply chain concentration. Management had not framed it as a risk. The board's institutional discipline in applying existing frameworks to new technology contexts surfaced a genuine vulnerability. The lesson is not that boards need new frameworks for AI. They need to apply their existing frameworks with discipline and specificity.

## The CEO Capability Signal

Board responsibility for CEO selection and evaluation is one of the clearest governance functions. The AI era has changed the competency profile of effective CEO leadership in ways that most nominating committees have not yet updated.

The question is not whether a CEO candidate has deployed AI systems. Many have, in roles where deployment was technically straightforward. The question is whether the candidate demonstrates genuine strategic capability in the AI context.

Three signals distinguish AI-strategic capability from AI-operational experience.

**A coherent competitive theory.** The AI-capable CEO can articulate—without a presentation deck, in conversation—where AI is changing the competitive dynamics of their industry, what that means for the company's current advantages and vulnerabilities, and how the company's AI investments connect to a specific theory of competitive differentiation. The executive who responds with deployment statistics—number of AI pilots, AI investment as a percentage of IT budget—has demonstrated operational engagement, not strategic thinking.

**Demonstrated willingness to experiment at organizational cost.** AI strategy requires accepting failure. The experiments that produce the most organizational learning are often those that produce unsuccessful outcomes in the short term. The CEO with a track record of authorizing genuine experiments—not pilots designed to succeed, but actual tests of hypotheses that might prove wrong—and who has extracted learning from the failures, demonstrates the disposition that AI-native strategy requires. Nominating committees evaluating candidates should ask specifically about AI experiments that failed, what was learned, and what changed.

**A clear accountability architecture.** The AI-capable CEO can explain, specifically, who is accountable for which AI decisions in their organization, how errors are surfaced and escalated, and what the governance structure looks like. This is not the same as having deployed AI systems responsibly in the past. It is the capacity to design and maintain accountability structures for AI deployment—a governance capability distinct from technical deployment experience.

A private equity sponsor conducting a CEO succession review for a portfolio company added AI strategic capability as an evaluation criterion. Two leading candidates who scored well on traditional dimensions—operational track record, financial sophistication, team leadership—scored poorly on the AI capability dimension. The sponsor extended the search rather than compromise. The CEO hired from an adjacent industry demonstrated a clear competitive theory in the interview, naming specifically where AI would change the industry's competitive dynamics and what the portfolio company's response should be. Within eighteen months, the company's operational improvement pace had outpaced comparable portfolio companies. The capability signal was predictive.

## Governance Architecture: What the Board Itself Needs

If AI strategy is a board-level responsibility, boards must be structured to exercise it. This requires both board composition changes and board process changes.

**Board composition.** The nominating committee's annual board composition review should include an explicit assessment of AI strategic literacy. The criterion is not deep technical expertise but the demonstrated ability to engage substantively with AI strategy questions: Has this director operated at senior levels in an AI-native environment? Has she governed AI deployment with adequate risk architecture? Does he understand, from experience, what distinguishes AI strategy from AI theater?

A consumer goods company's nominating committee made this a systematic priority over three annual cycles, adding two directors: one with operational experience running AI-intensive businesses, one with experience in AI risk governance in financial services. The board's AI-related discussions shifted. Questions became more specific. Management presentations became more rigorous in anticipation. The board began receiving forward-looking AI risk assessments rather than backward-looking deployment reports.

**Board process.** The more important question is not a new committee—it is integration into existing structure.

The temptation, when a new topic arrives at the board level, is to create a standalone committee for it. Early AI governance discussions have produced this instinct in many boardrooms. Resist it. Early ESG governance committees provide the cautionary parallel: by isolating sustainability in a specialized committee, organizations treated it as a separate domain outside the core business strategy. Information flowed to the committee rather than through it. Board members not on the committee felt exempt from engaging with the topic. The committee became a mechanism for the main board to feel comfortable without actually engaging—sustainability as a governance checkbox rather than a strategic question. The mature ESG governance models that actually work—those that drive real business adaptation—are those that integrated sustainability into audit and compensation committee charters and into full board strategic discussions, rather than creating a separate venue for it.

AI governance belongs in the existing committee structure: risk architecture and error accountability in the audit committee; executive incentives tied to AI capability-building in the compensation committee; board composition with AI strategic literacy in the nominating/governance committee. AI topics belong in full board strategic discussions, not in a committee that other directors can ignore.

A technology company's compensation committee confronted this integration challenge directly. Designing the CEO's long-term incentive structure, it faced the novel question of how to incentivize AI capability-building when outcomes are three to five years out. The committee developed a scorecard mixing lagging indicators—AI-driven revenue contribution, cost reduction—with leading indicators: internal AI adoption rates, AI-related talent retention, number of AI systems in production. Imperfect by the committee's own admission, but it sent a clear organizational signal: AI investment moved from a budget line to a strategic agenda item at every board meeting.

For senior functional executives reading this chapter: if your board is starting to tie executive compensation to AI capability-building metrics, that is a signal to build visibility into your function's contribution to those metrics. Don't wait to be asked. The AI capability accountability conversation will reach your function whether you initiate it or not.

## The Skeptic's Turn: When Board Oversight Becomes Overreach

The objection most commonly raised against the governance posture this chapter describes is that boards engaging too deeply in AI strategy create confusion and damage execution speed. The oversight/management distinction is real and important. Boards that attempt to select AI vendors, evaluate specific model architectures, or prescribe technical approaches will create dependency and confusion.

But this concern misidentifies the ask. The governance framework this chapter describes does not require boards to select systems or supervise pilots. It requires boards to ask rigorous questions about strategy, risk, and accountability—the same level of engagement boards now apply to financial controls and cybersecurity, neither of which required boards to understand double-entry bookkeeping or network protocols.

The "too fast to govern" objection is also frequently raised: AI is evolving so quickly that board-level expertise will be obsolete before it is acquired. This is a real concern about the wrong level of abstraction. Boards do not need to understand specific AI systems—those will indeed change. They need to understand the governance principles that apply regardless of which capabilities are in use: Who is accountable for AI decisions? How are errors surfaced and remediated? What is our competitive exposure to AI-enabled disruption? How does our AI capability map to our strategic priorities? What is our risk concentration in AI dependencies?

These questions remain stable even as specific systems evolve. The board that in 1999 asked whether the company had a coherent internet strategy did not need to know whether it was using Java or Perl. The governance question and the strategic question are durable; the technical specifics are not the board's domain.

Industry context matters. A financial services board faces a different AI risk surface than a consumer products board. The risk of model-driven lending discrimination, the regulatory exposure from algorithmic credit decisions, the accountability demands in customer-facing AI applications—these are specific to the industry context. Effective AI governance acknowledges this specificity rather than applying generic templates. The principles are universal; their application is industry-specific.

## What Exceptional AI Governance Looks Like

Project forward five years. The organizations with exceptional AI governance will have boards that treat AI oversight with the same systematic rigor they apply to financial controls.

Their boards will include directors who have operated AI-native businesses and who can distinguish AI strategy from AI theater in a management presentation. Their audit committees will receive quarterly risk updates on AI-specific vulnerabilities—model drift, concentration risk, accountability gaps, regulatory developments—with the same discipline applied to financial risk. Their compensation structures will include long-horizon incentive components tied to AI capability-building, creating alignment between executive decision-making and the three-to-five-year payoff windows that AI investments typically require.

Most importantly, their accountability architecture will be explicit and pre-established. They will know—before an AI system makes a consequential error, not after—who is accountable, how errors surface, and what the remediation process looks like.

For executives reading this chapter, the twelve-month agenda is specific: Define the accountability architecture for every AI system touching consequential decisions in your business—who the human accountable party is, what the audit trail looks like, and how errors are surfaced. Build a measurement framework that connects AI investment to organizational capability development, not just to deployment metrics. Present both to your audit committee as a governance matter, framed explicitly that way. Then work with your nominating committee to assess whether your board composition includes the AI strategic experience it needs to ask the right questions.

Boards that receive this kind of proactive engagement—executives thinking clearly about AI accountability before problems arrive—become more effective partners in the AI transformation than boards that first encounter AI governance in the context of a crisis. The investment in governance structure pays dividends well before the regulatory moment this chapter's opening analogy projects. It pays dividends immediately, in the quality of the board-management partnership on the most consequential strategic investment most organizations will make in this decade.
