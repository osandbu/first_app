# The Counsel That Remains
## What Artificial Intelligence Cannot Own in the Legal Function

---

The partner who ran a global investment bank's trading desk legal team for twenty years described the moment of reckoning with characteristic precision: "We went from reviewing two thousand contracts a quarter to twenty thousand. Headcount barely moved. We thought we had solved the problem." He paused. "Then we discovered what the problem actually was."

What the bank had solved was throughput. What surfaced in its place was judgment.

The bottleneck in the legal function has not been eliminated by AI systems — it has been relocated. What AI systems absorb is information processing; what they expose is the judgment layer that was always the function's true core. For most of the history of large organizations, the legal department functioned as an information bottleneck as much as an expertise bottleneck. Attorneys were expensive because legal knowledge was scarce and document processing was labor-intensive. The two things — expertise and throughput — were bundled together by economic necessity. AI systems are unbundling them, and the unbundling reveals, with uncomfortable clarity, that a substantial proportion of what passed for legal value was information processing dressed in professional clothing. The remaining core — the judgment layer — turns out to be both smaller than most legal departments claimed and more important than most executives realized.

The General Counsel's job is not ending. It is being clarified. The executive's responsibility is to understand what it is being clarified *into* — and to stop measuring the legal function's value by the volume of documents it processes.

---

## The Bottleneck That Is Moving

Before the printing press, legal knowledge was hand-copied, scarce, and concentrated in institutions that controlled access to documents. The scribes who reproduced legal texts held genuine power: they determined what was available, introduced interpretive glosses, and shaped what precedent was retrievable. When Gutenberg's press reduced the cost of reproducing text by two orders of magnitude, the scribal class was not eliminated — it was forced to compete on something other than document reproduction. The power shifted from those who could copy legal texts to those who could interpret them. The same dynamic now plays out across every corporate legal department in the world.

Liberating half of legal department capacity concentrates the remaining work in the domain that matters most. Studies of large in-house legal functions consistently find that between forty and sixty percent of attorney time is consumed by document-intensive work: contract review, due diligence, compliance monitoring, basic research. This is not an accident of poor management. It is the inevitable consequence of having built legal departments in a world where human attention was the only available tool for processing legal information. The cost structure of the profession reflected this constraint, and so did the career structure: junior attorneys learned the law by doing document review, built expertise through supervised repetition, and eventually earned the right to apply that expertise to higher-order problems.

AI systems can now handle the bulk of this document work faster, with greater consistency, and at a fraction of the cost. A major pharmaceutical company found that regulatory submission documents — the literature reviews, clinical summaries, and benefit-risk analyses required for drug approval filings — could be produced substantially faster and at lower cost. A multinational operating across forty-seven jurisdictions found that monitoring regulatory changes across all markets simultaneously, a task that would have required a much larger in-house team, was now tractable.

The bottleneck is moving. What executive leadership needs to understand — and most has not yet fully absorbed — is that this does not mean the legal function is getting smaller in importance. It means the part of the legal function that remains is getting larger in consequence. The bottleneck is relocating to exactly the territory where legal expertise has always been most genuinely valuable and most difficult to scale: judgment about acceptable risk, accountability for decisions that cannot be delegated to a system, and navigation of legal territory where no established precedent exists.

The executive who measures this transition by headcount reduction is asking the wrong question. The right question is: what is the legal function's judgment now being applied to, and is the answer commensurate with the organization's actual risk profile?

## What the Machine Cannot Own

The question of who is responsible when an AI system advises on a decision that goes wrong is both simpler and more complicated than most executives assume.

The simple part: the machine owns nothing. Under every existing legal framework in every jurisdiction, professional responsibility rests with the human professional who relied on the tool, reviewed its output, and allowed it to inform a decision. A legal team that submits an AI-drafted regulatory filing and receives a rejection due to an interpretation error in the AI's benefit-risk analysis will find that the relevant bar association, the relevant regulator, and the relevant plaintiff's attorney are all interested in the same question: which human signed this? The AI system's role is legally invisible. The attorneys who reviewed, approved, and submitted the document bear full professional responsibility, exactly as they would if a junior associate had drafted the same flawed analysis.

Bar associations across major jurisdictions have issued guidance on precisely this point. The pattern of conclusions is consistent: attorneys who use AI tools in producing work product have the same professional obligations as attorneys who use any other tool, including the obligation to exercise independent professional judgment about the accuracy and quality of that work product. The technology does not transfer the duty of competence. It changes the form in which that duty must be exercised.

This is not a new principle. It is the same principle that governed the actuary whose firm introduced spreadsheets. When computational tools became available, the actuary who relied on them remained professionally responsible for the accuracy of the analysis they produced. The spreadsheet did not absorb liability; it changed what the actuary was expected to verify and understand. The profession responded by raising its standards of numerical verification, not by concluding that the arrival of the spreadsheet had resolved questions of actuarial accountability.

But here is where the complexity enters — and where executives who have absorbed the simple answer stop thinking too early.

What is genuinely new is not the question of individual professional liability, which transfers cleanly from familiar doctrine. What is new is the question of *systemic* liability — when AI systems produce errors at scale across thousands of decisions simultaneously, the individual professional liability framework, while intact, becomes insufficient as an accountability mechanism.

A large property and casualty insurer that uses AI systems to adjudicate straightforward claims automatically and route complex claims to human reviewers faces a fundamentally different risk profile than one that routes every claim through human review. When a pattern of low-severity claims — each individually below the threshold that triggers human review — proves to be connected to a coordinated fraud scheme, the insurer has not suffered a single professional's error. It has suffered the consequence of a governance choice about where to draw the boundary between automated and human decision-making. The liability is not professional; it is organizational. The General Counsel who helped design that governance framework, or who failed to question it, owns a portion of that organizational liability in a way that no individual professional liability doctrine fully captures.

The private equity firm whose AI system correctly identified every disclosed material contract in a due diligence review but failed to identify an undisclosed oral agreement referenced obliquely in board minutes provides a related illustration. The system had no mechanism for flagging the absence of expected documentation — only for analyzing present documentation. The deal closed; the liability materialized. The attorney who led the due diligence process would correctly point out that no AI system was designed to catch what was not there. The client would correctly point out that due diligence was supposed to catch what was not there. The gap between those two positions is exactly where organizational accountability lives.

For the General Counsel, this means two things that must be held simultaneously. First: individual professional liability for AI-assisted work product functions exactly as traditional doctrine describes, and attorneys who do not understand this are taking risks they cannot see. Second: the GC's broader organizational responsibility extends to the architecture of AI-assisted legal processes — where automation boundaries are drawn, what failure modes they create, and what human oversight mechanisms exist to catch systematic errors before they propagate to scale.

The machine cannot own this. The GC must. And the executive team that treats the legal function as a processing cost center, rather than as the organization's institutional risk owner, will discover this distinction at the moment it can least afford to.

## The Expertise Pipeline Problem

There is a consequence of AI-assisted legal work that most General Counsels have not yet confronted directly, and that the legal profession is not well-equipped to discuss honestly, because it implicates the profession's own long-term health.

Junior attorneys have historically learned to practice law by doing the document-intensive work that AI systems now perform. Contract markup, document review, basic research, first-pass due diligence — these were the apprenticeship tasks through which associates developed the pattern recognition, the attention to detail, and the sense of what matters in a legal document that eventually qualified them to exercise independent judgment. The work was not glamorous, and most partners would not have described it as pedagogically designed. But it functioned as a training system nonetheless, and its outputs — experienced attorneys who had internalized legal standards through thousands of hours of supervised repetition — were the profession's raw material.

What happens when AI systems absorb this work?

A mid-size regional law firm that deployed AI systems for all first-pass document review and contract drafting found out within two years. Partners began noticing that associates on partnership track had substantially less developed independent drafting and analytical skills than previous cohorts at the same career stage. The associates had learned to review and correct AI output rather than to produce original analysis. They could identify when an AI-generated clause was wrong; they were less able to construct the right clause from first principles. They had become sophisticated editors of machine output — a valuable skill, but not the same skill as being a lawyer who has internalized what the clause is trying to accomplish.

Cognitive science research on expertise development provides the theoretical foundation: skills not practiced degrade, and the degradation is most severe in exactly the skills that are hardest to observe from the outside. An associate who reviews a hundred AI-generated contracts without independently drafting appears, from a performance metrics standpoint, to be doing the same job as an associate who drafted those contracts. The skill atrophy is invisible until it is needed.

Aviation has recognized this failure mode under the rubric of automation dependency. Highly automated flight control systems handle the vast majority of routine flight operations with greater precision than human pilots. But the accident investigation record has documented cases where pilots, having become dependent on automation for normal operations, lost proficiency in the manual control skills required precisely when the automation failed. The automation handled ninety-nine percent of cases well; the remaining one percent required exactly the deep skill that automation had caused to atrophy. The consequence was catastrophic failure in the novel situations that most required human expertise.

Applied to legal: an AI system that handles ninety-five percent of contract review with high accuracy makes the legal team progressively less practiced at the detailed contract analysis required when the system encounters a document pattern outside its training — precisely the cases where manual review should be most rigorous and where the system's confidence signals may be least reliable.

The General Counsel who deploys AI aggressively today without redesigning the expertise development pipeline is making a bet that the legal situations the organization faces in five years will not require capabilities the organization has stopped developing. Given that the most significant legal risks tend to arise from novel fact patterns, new regulatory frameworks, and unprecedented commercial structures, this is a bet that should not be made casually.

The design response is not to slow AI adoption. It is to deliberately preserve the expertise-building experiences that AI adoption would otherwise eliminate. Structured deliberate practice outside the AI workflow. Mandatory independent drafting for associates, even when AI output is available. Supervision structures that require attorneys to demonstrate underlying judgment, not merely the ability to evaluate machine output. The GC must treat this not as an HR program but as strategic risk management: preserving institutional capability in the domains where the organization will need it most when the unexpected occurs.

## Governance for Machine-Speed Error

Individual errors have always been part of legal practice. The framework for managing them — professional liability, malpractice insurance, reputational consequences — has developed over centuries and broadly works. The framework breaks down when errors propagate at machine speed across an entire decision class before anyone notices they have occurred.

The investment bank that deployed AI-assisted contract review at scale, seeing its review volume increase by an order of magnitude, was not running a risk management operation designed for machine-speed error propagation. The oversight mechanisms in place — attorney review of flagged items, periodic audits of a sample of reviewed contracts — were designed for a world where errors occurred one at a time, were distributed randomly across the review population, and could be caught by reviewing a statistically representative sample. When an AI system makes a systematic error on a particular clause type rather than a random error across all clause types, reviewing a random sample may miss the systematic error entirely. The bank's legal exposure did not decrease proportionally to the volume increase because the new risk profile was different in kind, not just in scale.

Reliability engineering in high-consequence industries has developed responses to this problem. The core insight is that highly automated systems require different oversight architectures than partially automated ones. Automation does not simply reduce the volume of human review required; it changes the *nature* of the review that catches the errors that matter. Sampling strategies must be redesigned to detect systematic errors rather than random ones. Exception reporting must be redesigned to surface what the system was not designed to flag, not only what it flagged. And the humans who provide oversight must maintain enough operational familiarity with the underlying task to recognize the difference between a system performing well and a system performing confidently in a domain where its training is thin.

For the General Counsel, this is a mandate to become an architect of oversight systems, not simply a consumer of AI outputs. The legal function's traditional value was expert judgment on individual matters. The AI-native legal function's additional value is the design and governance of systems that ensure individual expert judgment is applied to the right matters — and that catch systematic problems before they accumulate to exposure.

This requires new operational infrastructure. Decision audit trails that allow the legal team to understand, after the fact, which decisions were AI-assisted, what inputs the AI system processed, and what the human reviewer was or was not shown. Systematic failure mode analysis, borrowed from engineering disciplines, that maps what categories of error the AI system might make that would not be caught by the existing review process. Independent testing of AI system performance against the actual document population the organization encounters, not the standard market documentation against which the system was trained. And governance structures — at the department level, at the legal leadership level, and potentially at the board level — that ensure accountability for AI system design choices rests at an appropriate level of seniority.

The General Counsel who does not build this infrastructure is delegating more than document review. They are delegating the capacity to know, with confidence, what their legal function is actually doing.

## Novel Territory and the Irreplaceable Judgment Layer

AI systems learn from precedent. This is their fundamental architecture: they recognize patterns in large bodies of prior decisions, contracts, rulings, and regulatory guidance, and they apply those patterns to new inputs. They are, by design, backward-looking. They excel when the territory is familiar and the training corpus is dense. They provide the least reliable guidance precisely where reliable guidance is most needed: at the frontier.

A multinational corporation operating across forty-seven jurisdictions found this distinction sharp. Its AI monitoring system performed well on jurisdiction-specific compliance alerts — flagging when a particular country's data protection regulation required a specific disclosure, or when a new employment law created an obligation in a specific market. Where it failed was in identifying the *interactions* between jurisdictions: cases where compliance with one country's data localization requirement created a conflict with another country's data transfer obligation. The failure was not a performance failure — it was an architectural one. Cross-jurisdictional conflicts of this kind are, by definition, underrepresented in any training corpus because their novelty is what creates the conflict. The AI system could not surface what it had not seen, and what it had not seen was precisely the territory that mattered most.

This points to what may be the most important remaining function of the General Counsel in an AI-native legal environment: serving as the organization's early warning system for legal risk categories that do not yet have established precedent, which AI systems are structurally unable to identify.

The law has always developed through the encounter of established doctrine with novel facts. Gutenberg's press created intellectual property questions that the common law of the fifteenth century was entirely unprepared to address. The electrification of industry created workplace liability questions that required decades of case law development. The internet created privacy, jurisdiction, and liability questions that courts are still resolving. In each case, the most valuable legal work — and the most consequential mistakes — occurred at the frontier: where the existing framework was being applied to situations it was not designed for.

AI systems can tell you what the existing framework says. They can research every reported decision on point, identify the most relevant authorities, and synthesize the current state of doctrine with speed and accuracy that no human attorney can match. What they cannot do is tell you when the existing framework is inadequate — when the fact pattern in front of you is genuinely novel enough that applying established doctrine will produce wrong results, and that the organization should be preparing for a legal environment that does not yet exist.

The General Counsel's job in novel territory is not to know the answer. It is to recognize that the existing answers are likely to be wrong, and to bring that recognition into the organization's strategic planning before it becomes a legal crisis. This requires a form of legal imagination — the ability to construct plausible futures in which current conduct creates liability under frameworks that do not yet exist — that is not a research function and cannot be substituted for by improved research capability, however powerful.

Executives should measure this capacity directly: Is the legal function surfacing legal risk categories in advance of established precedent? Or is it responding to the legal environment as it already exists? An AI-assisted legal function that only identifies known risks is not providing adequate legal coverage for an organization operating in a rapidly evolving regulatory landscape. The judgment layer the GC provides is at its most valuable — and most irreplaceable — precisely when there is nothing in the training data to draw on.

## The Skeptic's Turn: Will AI Simply Eliminate the General Counsel?

The argument presented in this chapter has a strong version against itself, and it deserves a direct response.

The strong objection is not that AI will fail to transform legal work. It is that AI will transform legal work so thoroughly that the General Counsel role as currently constituted will not survive. If AI systems can perform contract review, due diligence, regulatory monitoring, and basic litigation support at or above human accuracy and at a fraction of the cost, the economic case for a large in-house legal function weakens considerably. This chapter might be offering a comforting narrative to a profession that is actually facing something closer to structural displacement.

The objection deserves concession in its strongest partial form: yes, the legal function will almost certainly be smaller in headcount for routine work. The law firm model built on leveraging junior associate hours for document-intensive tasks will be significantly disrupted. Legal departments that have grown by adding attorneys to process increasing volumes of contracts and compliance documentation will not need to grow that way in the future. The employment economics of large legal organizations are genuinely changing.

But the objection conflates two different things: the legal function as an information-processing operation, and the legal function as an accountability-bearing institution.

The history of finance when market data became universally accessible is instructive. When information that had been the proprietary advantage of large institutions became broadly available, the argument could have been made — and in some quarters was — that the information advantage investment banks had built their businesses around was about to be eliminated. What actually happened was that the demand for high-quality judgment about that information increased dramatically. When data became cheap, the scarce resource was not data; it was the ability to make good decisions about what the data meant. The economics of financial expertise were reorganized, not eliminated.

The parallel holds for legal expertise, with one additional element that the financial analogy does not fully capture. Legal risk is irreducibly about accountability, and accountability requires a human who can be held responsible. Organizations do not operate in a legal vacuum; they operate within legal systems designed for human actors who can be sued, prosecuted, sanctioned, and licensed. As long as this remains true — and there is no serious proposal to change it — the legal function cannot be automated away, because what it ultimately provides is not information, but the institutional capacity to own risk and bear responsibility.

What will be displaced is the labor-intensive information processing that has historically surrounded that function. The General Counsel who understands this will reorganize accordingly: fewer attorneys processing documents, more attorneys exercising judgment, and the entire function positioned as a strategic risk owner rather than an administrative cost center. For executive leadership, this means resisting the reflex to measure AI's impact in the legal function purely through headcount reduction — and asking instead how the judgment the function retains is being deployed against the organization's highest-consequence risks.

## Redesigning the Legal Function

The AI-native legal department will look different from its predecessor in ways that can be described with some precision.

It will skew more senior in composition. When AI systems handle the work that junior attorneys have historically performed, the economic argument for large associate classes weakens. But the talent model required for a senior-heavy department is fundamentally different from a pyramid-shaped one: recruiting strategies, development paths, and retention structures all require redesign. A legal department that attempts to maintain a traditional pyramid structure while automating its base will not simply become more efficient — it will create a hollowed-out organization in which neither the junior nor the senior layers are doing work calibrated to their actual function.

It will be more explicitly focused on risk ownership than on throughput. The legal department that has historically justified its existence through volume — how many contracts reviewed, how many matters closed, how many compliance checks completed — will need different metrics. The relevant measures will be: what legal risks did the organization avoid, what novel situations did the legal function identify before they became crises, and what governance structures is the legal function maintaining over the AI systems it deploys? If a function's value can be fully quantified through throughput metrics, it is performing work that can eventually be automated. The unmeasurability of judgment work is not a deficiency in the metrics; it is the signal that genuine judgment is being exercised.

It will be more strategically central in organizational position, not less. This seems counterintuitive until you consider the mechanism. When the legal department's volume output is largely automated, every decision the legal team makes is visibly a judgment call, not a processing task. The General Counsel's advice on whether to enter a new jurisdiction, how to structure a novel transaction, or how aggressively to contest a regulatory interpretation carries more organizational weight when it is clearly a product of expertise rather than a byproduct of throughput. The function becomes less like a factory and more like a consultancy — and consultancies that provide rare judgment command different economics than factories that provide interchangeable processing.

The executive team that restructures the legal function with this understanding will find that it has acquired a genuinely different capability: not a faster contract review operation, but an institutional risk intelligence function capable of operating at the frontier of the organization's legal environment.

## What the General Counsel Must Do Differently

The chapter's argument resolves to four concrete imperatives for the General Counsel navigating this transition.

**Redesign the oversight architecture before deploying at scale.** AI systems create the capacity for machine-speed error propagation across entire decision classes. The oversight mechanisms appropriate for human-paced legal work are not appropriate for AI-assisted legal work. Before expanding AI deployment, the GC must design oversight systems that can detect systematic errors, not just random ones — sampling strategies calibrated to the error modes of AI systems rather than the error modes of human reviewers, exception reporting that surfaces what the system was not designed to flag, and decision audit trails that make AI-assisted processes transparent and reviewable after the fact.

**Treat expertise development as strategic risk management.** The expertise pipeline that sustains the legal function's long-term capability will not maintain itself if AI systems absorb all the apprenticeship work. The GC must explicitly preserve deliberate practice opportunities — mandatory independent analysis, supervised drafting without AI assistance, structured exposure to the novel and complex cases that cannot be handled by the AI system — and must understand the cost of this investment as the cost of maintaining institutional capability in the situations that matter most. This is not a training program. It is insurance against the moment when the AI system's confidence exceeds its competence.

**Assign the legal function's resources to frontier risk, not to confirming what AI already knows.** The General Counsel's most important contribution in an AI-rich environment is not reviewing AI-generated contract analysis. It is identifying the legal risk categories that the organization faces in territory where AI systems have no reliable precedent to draw on: novel regulatory frameworks, unprecedented commercial structures, cross-jurisdictional conflicts, and the emerging liability doctrines that will define the organization's legal environment a decade from now. This is the judgment work that AI systems are structurally unable to perform — and the work that justifies the legal function's claim on senior executive attention.

**Own the governance of AI-assisted legal processes as a professional obligation, not just an organizational choice.** The professional responsibility framework makes clear that attorneys who deploy AI tools retain full accountability for the work product those tools contribute to. The standard of care for AI-assisted legal practice is still being established by bar associations, courts, and regulators. The General Counsel who participates actively in that process — engaging with bar association working groups on AI ethics and professional responsibility, maintaining rigorous documentation of AI governance decisions as evidence of the standard of care being applied, and consulting proactively with regulators about AI-assisted compliance processes before a dispute arises — is better positioned than the GC who discovers the emerging standard reactively, when a dispute has already made it relevant. Shaping the framework is always preferable to being judged by a framework shaped by others.

---

The printing press did not eliminate the need for legal expertise. It eliminated the need for legal scribes and elevated the attorney who could interpret what the documents meant. The current transition will eliminate the need for attorneys as document processors and will elevate the attorney who can bear responsibility for what the documents commit the organization to.

This is not a comfortable transition. It requires intellectual honesty about how much of the legal function's historical headcount was, in economic terms, document processing with a law degree. It requires courage to redesign expertise pipelines that the legal profession has relied on for a century. And it requires a GC willing to insist to a cost-focused executive team that the judgment layer being retained is more valuable — not less — precisely because AI has made everything around it cheaper.

But it is an intelligible transition, and it is the General Counsel's to navigate. The function that emerges — smaller in headcount for routine work, senior in composition, strategically positioned as the organization's risk intelligence function — will be more genuinely legal, not less. What it loses in volume, it gains in consequence. And consequence, ultimately, is what legal expertise has always been for.
