# What Boards Will Expect

In the early months of 2002, Congress was still absorbing the wreckage of Enron and WorldCom when it passed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. The legislation was widely described as the most significant expansion of corporate governance requirements since the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. Among its provisions: CEOs and CFOs now faced personal criminal liability for certifying false financial statements. Audit committees were required to include financially literate independent directors. Material weaknesses in internal controls had to be disclosed publicly.

What changed was not the underlying importance of financial integrity—that had always mattered. What changed was the expectation of board engagement. Boards could no longer treat financial oversight as a formality—a function they nominally supervised but effectively delegated to management and auditors. After Sarbanes-Oxley, they were required to understand the control environment well enough to certify it, or face personal legal exposure for the failure. The result was structural: audit committees were reconstituted with specific expertise requirements, questioning frameworks were developed and institutionalized, and the relationship between boards and management on financial matters was permanently altered.

AI is following a similar arc. The governance apparatus has not yet formalized—there is no AI equivalent of Sarbanes-Oxley, and it may not arrive for several years. But the underlying dynamic is identical: a strategically material capability is being deployed across organizations at scale, and the governance structures responsible for oversight have not yet developed the rigor to match the deployment. The question is not whether that rigor will arrive—history is clear that it will—but whether boards and executives will build it before it is forced on them, or after.

This chapter is about what that governance looks like, why it is a board-level responsibility, and what executives should be doing now to be prepared for the expectations their boards are starting to form.

## The Governance Gap

There is a consistent pattern across every major technology transition of the past century: the gap between the pace of deployment and the development of governance structures to manage it. Nuclear technology was deployed before radiation protection standards were adequate. The internet proliferated before data privacy frameworks were coherent. Algorithmic trading expanded before market structure regulations caught up.

AI is inside this same pattern, but the gap is wider and the stakes are higher than most previous transitions. An AI system making consequential decisions at organizational scale—lending approvals, hiring screens, medical triage, supply chain routing—introduces risk categories that existing governance frameworks were not designed to assess: model drift, bias amplification, novel failure modes that have no historical precedent, and the specific challenge of accountability when a decision was made by a system rather than a person.

A McKinsey global survey on AI adoption found something striking: the gap between organizations reporting AI at scale and organizations reporting board-level AI governance processes was larger than the deployment gap itself. The majority of organizations deploying AI at scale had not developed board-level oversight structures for that deployment. This is not unusual for an early-stage technology—the same survey taken of cybersecurity in 2010 would have shown a comparable gap. Research on cybersecurity committee formation timelines shows the average S&P 500 company took approximately eight years from first identifying cybersecurity as a material risk to establishing formal board-level oversight structures.

The boards that move faster on AI governance than the average will have a structural advantage. They will identify concentration risks, accountability gaps, and competitive vulnerabilities before they become crises. They will send clearer signals to management about what is expected, creating better-aligned investment decisions. And they will be better positioned when the regulatory equivalent of Sarbanes-Oxley arrives for AI—better able to certify their oversight structures because they built them in advance of the requirement.

The governance gap is not a technical problem. It is a board competency and process problem. Boards are capable of developing rigorous oversight in domains they do not fully understand technically—they have done it for environmental risk, cybersecurity, human capital, and financial controls. AI requires the same approach: not technical literacy, but the ability to ask substantive questions about a domain that has become strategically material.

## What Boards Are Currently Asking—and What They Should Ask Instead

In most boardrooms today, the AI question takes a recognizable form: management presents a strategy document or an investment case for AI deployment, the board asks whether the company has a coherent AI strategy, management presents a roadmap, and the board accepts or adjusts the investment allocation. The conversation is framed around opportunity—where AI can create value—with risk as a secondary consideration.

This is insufficient. Not because the opportunity framing is wrong—AI does represent significant opportunity—but because it mirrors the posture boards took toward financial reporting before Sarbanes-Oxley: treating a materially consequential function as primarily management's domain, with board engagement limited to resource allocation decisions. The limitation became visible when the function went wrong.

A more rigorous board engagement with AI strategy organizes around three dimensions, each of which generates a different set of questions.

**Competitive position:** Is the organization building AI capability that will be strategically material, or is it deploying AI tactically without a coherent theory of how it changes the competitive position? The board's questions here are not operational: Which vendors are you using? How many pilots are running? They are strategic: How does AI change the basis of competition in this industry? Who are the AI-native competitors or entrants who could threaten the business model, and what is management's assessment of their capabilities? If a competitor deployed AI more effectively than we have over the next three years, where specifically would we lose market position? A credible answer to these questions requires real strategic engagement—not a deployment roadmap but a competitive theory.

**Risk architecture:** Who is accountable for AI decisions, and how are errors surfaced and remediated? This is the governance question most often absent from board discussions. When an AI system contributes to a consequential error—a lending decision that violates fair lending law, a hiring screen that produces systematic bias, a clinical support system that generates an incorrect recommendation at scale—the board needs a clear accountability structure. Who was the human accountable for the decision the AI informed? What is the audit trail? How was the error identified, how was it escalated, and how was it remediated?

A large healthcare system's board made this question concrete. Facing the deployment of AI systems for diagnostic support, the board required management to define in advance—before deployment—the accountability architecture for every AI system touching clinical decisions: which human clinician holds final decision authority, what the audit trail looks like, and how errors surface and get remediated. When a diagnostic support system later produced a systematic error, this pre-established accountability structure allowed the organization to respond quickly and credibly. Boards that had not done this work faced the same AI systems with no clear accountability architecture when something went wrong.

**Organizational readiness:** Does the organization have the talent, culture, and infrastructure to execute on its AI strategy, or is it investing in technology while underinvesting in the organizational capability to use it? This question addresses the most common failure mode in AI deployment: organizations that purchase AI capability and cannot extract value from it because they have not built the organizational muscles to direct it. The board's questions here include: What percentage of senior leaders can articulate how AI is changing their functional area and what they are doing differently as a result? How is the organization measuring learning and adaptation, not just deployment? What is the talent strategy for the specific combination of judgment and AI fluency that AI-native execution requires?

A Fortune 500 retailer's audit committee provided a model for how this scrutiny plays out. In its annual enterprise risk review, it identified a concentration risk the management team had not framed as such: the company's pricing, inventory, and customer personalization systems had all migrated to AI systems from a single vendor ecosystem. The audit committee challenged management using its standard vendor concentration lens—the same questions it would ask about supply chain concentration. Management had not framed it as a risk. The board's institutional discipline in applying existing frameworks to new technology contexts surfaced a genuine vulnerability.

## The CEO Capability Signal

Board responsibility for CEO selection and evaluation is one of the clearest governance functions. And the AI era has changed the competency profile of effective CEO leadership in ways that most nominating committees have not yet updated.

The question is not whether a CEO candidate has deployed AI systems. Many have, in roles where deployment was technically straightforward. The question is whether the candidate demonstrates genuine strategic capability in the AI context—which looks different from operational AI deployment.

Three signals distinguish AI-strategic capability from AI-operational experience.

**A coherent competitive theory.** The AI-capable CEO can articulate—without a presentation deck, in conversation—where AI is changing the competitive dynamics of their industry, what that means for the company's current advantages and vulnerabilities, and how the company's AI investments connect to a specific theory of competitive differentiation. The executive who responds to this question with deployment statistics—number of AI pilots, AI investment as a percentage of IT budget—has demonstrated operational engagement, not strategic thinking. The one who can explain why AI changes the source of competitive advantage in their specific market context has demonstrated the thinking boards need.

**Demonstrated willingness to experiment at organizational cost.** AI strategy requires accepting failure. The experiments that produce the most organizational learning are often the ones that produce unsuccessful outcomes in the short term. The CEO who has a track record of authorizing genuine experiments—not pilots designed to succeed, but actual tests of hypotheses that might prove wrong—and who has extracted learning from the failures, demonstrates the disposition that AI-native strategy requires. Boards evaluating CEO candidates should ask specifically about AI experiments that failed, what was learned, and what changed as a result.

**A clear accountability architecture.** The AI-capable CEO understands that AI deployment without clear accountability is a liability. They can explain—specifically—who is accountable for which AI decisions in their organization, how errors are surfaced and escalated, and what the governance structure looks like above the operational layer. This is not the same as having deployed AI systems responsibly. It is the capacity to design and maintain accountability structures for AI deployment, which is a governance capability distinct from technical deployment experience.

A private equity sponsor confronted the CEO capability question directly in a succession review for a portfolio company. The sponsor added AI strategic capability as an evaluation criterion—and found that two leading candidates who scored well on traditional dimensions scored poorly on this dimension. The sponsor extended the search. The CEO hired from an adjacent industry with demonstrated AI transformation experience was asked specifically to explain his theory of how AI would change the competitive dynamics of the portfolio company's industry. His answer was specific, nuanced, and challenged some of the board's assumptions. Within eighteen months, the company's operational improvement pace outpaced comparable portfolio companies. The capability signal was predictive.

## Governance Architecture: What the Board Itself Needs

If AI strategy is a board-level responsibility, boards must be structured to exercise it. This requires two changes: board composition and board process.

**Board composition.** The nominating committee's annual board composition review should include an assessment of AI strategic literacy: not deep technical expertise in building AI systems, but the demonstrated ability to engage substantively with AI strategy questions. The questions that matter are not "can this director code?" but "has this director operated at senior levels in an AI-native environment? Has she governed AI deployment with adequate risk architecture? Does he understand, from experience, what distinguishes AI strategy from AI theater?"

A consumer goods company's nominating committee made this a systematic priority over three annual cycles, adding two directors with relevant backgrounds: one with operational experience running AI-intensive businesses, one with experience in AI risk governance in financial services. The board's AI-related discussions shifted: questions became more specific, management presentations became more rigorous in anticipation, and the board began receiving forward-looking AI risk assessments rather than backward-looking deployment reports.

The composition question should be integrated into existing framework rather than creating a separate "AI committee." Creating a standalone AI committee risks the same failure that early ESG committees produced: isolating AI governance from core business strategy, treating it as a specialized domain rather than an integrated strategic question. AI is not a specialized domain—it is a general-purpose capability that changes every function. The governance architecture should match: AI oversight belongs in the audit committee (for risk), the compensation committee (for executive incentives tied to AI capability-building), and the nominating/governance committee (for board composition), with integration into full-board strategic discussions.

**Board process.** The AI governance calendar should include, at minimum: an annual strategic AI review (not a deployment report, but a forward-looking assessment of competitive position and capability gaps); quarterly risk committee updates on AI-specific risks (model drift, concentration, accountability gaps, regulatory developments); and specific questions on AI in every audit committee cycle. The compensation committee should evaluate whether executive incentive structures are adequately aligned with AI capability-building time horizons—which are typically three to five years, creating misalignment with annual bonus structures.

A technology company's compensation committee confronted this directly. Designing the CEO's long-term incentive structure, it faced the novel question of how to incentivize AI capability-building when outcomes are three to five years out. The committee developed a scorecard mixing lagging indicators—AI-driven revenue contribution, cost reduction—with leading indicators: internal AI adoption rates, AI-related talent retention, number of AI systems in production. Imperfect by the committee's own admission, but it sent a clear organizational signal. AI investment moved from a budget line to a strategic agenda item at every board meeting.

## The Skeptic's Turn: When Board Oversight Becomes Overreach

The objection most commonly raised against the governance posture this chapter describes is that boards that engage too deeply in AI strategy create confusion and damage execution speed. The oversight/management distinction is real and important. The goal is governance—setting expectations and holding management accountable—not management itself.

This concern is legitimate. Boards that attempt to select AI vendors, evaluate specific model architectures, or prescribe technical approaches to AI deployment will create dependency and confusion. The governance principle applies here as directly as elsewhere: boards set expectations, demand measurement, and hold management accountable. They do not operate.

But the objection misidentifies the ask. The governance framework this chapter describes does not require boards to select systems or supervise pilots. It requires boards to ask rigorous questions about strategy, risk, and accountability—the same level of engagement boards now apply to financial controls and cybersecurity, neither of which required boards to understand double-entry bookkeeping or network protocols.

The "too fast to govern" objection is also frequently raised: AI is evolving so quickly that by the time boards develop expertise, the landscape will have changed. This is a real concern about the wrong level of abstraction. Boards do not need to understand specific AI systems—those will indeed change. They need to understand the governance principles that apply regardless of which capabilities are in use: accountability, risk concentration, competitive exposure, organizational readiness. These principles are durable. The board that in 1999 asked whether the company had a coherent internet strategy did not need to know whether it was using Java or Perl. The governance question and the strategic question are durable; the technical specifics are not the board's domain.

Industry-specificity matters. A financial services board faces a different AI risk surface than a consumer products board. The risk of model-driven lending discrimination, the regulatory exposure from algorithmic credit decisions, the accountability demands in customer-facing AI applications—these are specific to the industry context. Effective AI governance frameworks acknowledge this specificity rather than applying generic templates. The governance principles are universal; their application is industry-specific. Boards that are applying a one-size-fits-all AI governance checklist are doing better than those applying no framework at all, but worse than those that have invested in understanding the specific AI risk profile of their business context.

## What Exceptional AI Governance Looks Like

Project forward five years. The companies with exceptional AI governance will look different from those that treated AI as a management matter delegated upward only when problems emerged.

Their boards will include directors who have operated AI-native businesses and who understand the difference between AI deployment and AI strategy. Their audit committees will receive quarterly risk updates on AI-specific vulnerabilities—model drift, concentration risk, accountability gaps—with the same discipline they apply to financial risk. Their compensation structures will include long-horizon incentive components tied to AI capability-building, creating alignment between executive decision-making and the three-to-five-year payoff windows that AI investments typically require.

Most importantly, their accountability architecture will be explicit and pre-established. They will know—before an AI system makes a consequential error, not after—who is accountable, how errors surface, and what the remediation process looks like. This is not primarily a legal or compliance consideration. It is an organizational design consideration. The organization that has thought through accountability before a failure can respond quickly and credibly. The organization that has not will improvise under pressure—with predictable consequences for both the outcome and the trust of all stakeholders watching.

For executives reading this chapter, the practical question is not what the board will eventually demand. It is what you should be building now, ahead of that demand.

The answer is specific. Start with accountability architecture: define, for every AI system making consequential decisions, the human accountability structure above it. Then build a measurement framework for AI capability—not deployment metrics, but outcome metrics that connect AI investment to organizational learning and competitive capability. Present this framework to your audit committee in the next cycle, framed explicitly as a governance question. Then work with the nominating committee to assess whether your board composition includes the AI strategic experience it needs.

Boards that receive this kind of proactive governance engagement from management—executives who are thinking clearly about AI accountability before problems arrive—will be more supportive partners in the AI transformation than those who first hear about AI governance in the context of a crisis. The investment in governance structure pays dividends well before the regulatory moment this chapter's opening Sarbanes-Oxley analogy suggests. It pays dividends immediately, in the quality of the board-management partnership on the most strategically consequential investment most organizations will make in this decade.
