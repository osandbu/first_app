# Why Organizations Change More Slowly Than Technology

---

## The Dynamo Fallacy

In the 1880s, American factories began electrifying. The decision seemed obvious: steam engines were expensive to maintain, dangerous to operate, and tied to geography. Electric motors were cleaner, more flexible, and increasingly affordable. Within two decades, the technology had spread widely through manufacturing. And then — nothing. Measured productivity gains were minimal. Historians of technology puzzled over this for decades. The machines were better. Why wasn't the output?

The economist Paul David provided the answer in a 1990 paper that has become foundational for thinking about technology transitions. Factory owners had replaced their steam engines with electric motors. But they had kept everything else: the long central driveshaft, the leather belts radiating outward to each piece of machinery, the physical layout of workers arranged by proximity to the central power source rather than by the logic of the workflow. They had adopted the technology without redesigning the organization. The result was a marginally cleaner, marginally more reliable version of the same factory.

The transformation came only in the 1920s, when a new generation of factory managers — who had grown up with electricity rather than having learned it as a late addition to an existing system — began designing factories from scratch around what electricity made possible. Distributed motors at each workstation. Workers arranged by function rather than proximity to the engine. Natural light through reorganized windows. Flexible floor plans that could be reconfigured as products changed. These were not incremental improvements on the steam-era factory. They were a different thing entirely, and they required not just new technology but new imagination about what a factory was for.

The productivity gains from electrification showed up in economic statistics not in the 1890s, when the technology was adopted, but in the 1920s, when the organizational redesign finally occurred. The lag was not a failure of technology adoption. It was a failure of organizational imagination — the inability to see that the new technology required not just replacement of the old mechanism but reinvention of the system around it.

This is the central problem of the current AI transition, and it is being repeated with striking fidelity. Organizations are buying AI systems. They are automating existing workflows. They are producing better, faster versions of the same reports, analyses, and processes they were already running. And they are wondering, with some frustration, why the gains are not more dramatic. The question they are not asking — because it is harder and more disorienting — is the one that eventually unlocked electricity's potential: if we were designing this organization from scratch, with AI's capabilities as a given, what would it look like?

That question is the executive work of the current moment. Almost no organization is doing it well.

## Three Sources of Institutional Resistance

Organizations do not resist change because they are irrational. They resist change because they are organizations — because they have incentive systems that reward existing behaviors, identity structures that define what members value about their roles, and inertial forces that make existing processes self-perpetuating long after the rationale for them has disappeared. Understanding resistance requires distinguishing among these three sources, because each responds to a different executive intervention and treating them as a single undifferentiated "change management problem" is why most change programs fail.

The first source is incentive misalignment. This is the most tractable and the most often confused for the other two. When an organization's performance management system, compensation structure, and promotion criteria reward behaviors that the new technology disrupts, people behave rationally in resisting adoption. They are not confused about what the technology can do; they understand very clearly what it does to their measured performance.

A global investment bank piloted AI systems for equity research in the early 2010s. The technology worked. Analyst productivity, measured in reports-per-week, doubled in controlled experiments. The rollout stalled for three years. Not because of technical failure. Because the performance management system still measured analysts by volume of original research produced — the very metric that AI-assisted production undermined. Analysts whose output was augmented by AI were penalized under existing evaluation criteria. The technology was ready; the incentive architecture was not. The bank eventually adopted the technology, but only after redesigning the performance framework. The incentive redesign, not the technology deployment, was the enabling constraint.

A major retail chain provides a more mundane but equally instructive example. The firm deployed AI systems for inventory forecasting and achieved measurably better outcomes in warehouse pilots. Regional store managers, whose bonuses were tied to local inventory metrics, systematically overrode the AI recommendations. They did not trust predictions they did not understand, and the risk of a stockout fell on them personally while the upside accrued to the corporate supply chain team. The misalignment was structural and specific: the system that bore the cost of AI errors was not the same as the system that captured AI's benefits. No amount of training or communication changed this. The incentive problem required an incentive solution.

The second source of resistance is identity — the organizational equivalent of what Freud called the narcissism of small differences. People in organizations do not just do jobs; they are members of professional communities with traditions, status hierarchies, and self-conceptions. Middle managers are not just people who coordinate work; they are the people who synthesize information and translate it for senior leadership. Partners at professional services firms are not just senior advisers; they are people whose judgment is worth paying for because it is scarce and cultivated through years of experience. Physicians are not just diagnosticians; they are members of a profession whose authority rests on a particular relationship between patient trust and clinical expertise.

When AI systems disrupt the functional basis of these identities without addressing the identity itself, resistance is not irrationality — it is self-protection. The physician who resists AI diagnostic tools may not believe the tool is less accurate. She may be protecting her conception of what good medicine is: a relationship between a trained clinician and an individual patient, built on trust and clinical presence, not on algorithmic synthesis. That conception may be wrong, or it may be right, or it may be partially both. But it will not be changed by demonstrating the tool's accuracy. It requires an explicit conversation about what the profession is for in a world where some of its most valued capabilities are automatable.

The third source is inertia — the tendency of systems to continue what they were designed to do, independent of whether that behavior is still optimal. Karl Weick's concept of organizational sensemaking explains this at a cognitive level: people in organizations don't simply process information neutrally; they enact their environments, acting first and then interpreting their actions retrospectively to confirm existing beliefs. Organizations that have been operating in a particular way for decades have deep sensemaking routines calibrated for that operating environment. A new technology that generates information faster and in different forms outpaces those routines — not because the organization is stupid, but because its meaning-making system was built for a different environment.

A consumer packaged goods company that ran a successful AI pilot in marketing analytics and sought to scale it enterprise-wide encountered this in a specific form. Data was siloed across business units, each of which treated its customer data as a proprietary asset underpinning its internal budget negotiations. Sharing data with a centralized AI system would have eroded each unit's informational leverage in resource allocation discussions. Middle managers resisted not because they misunderstood AI but because they understood their own incentives perfectly. This was inertia sustained by incentive — a combination of the first and third sources of resistance, with the identity dimension lurking beneath it: the business unit leaders who controlled proprietary data also derived status from that control.

## The Middle Management Problem

The organizational layer most likely to impede AI adoption is not the C-suite and not the frontline workforce. It is middle management.

This claim is uncomfortable, and it is important. Senior executives are typically enthusiastic about AI's strategic possibilities, even if they lack the operational knowledge to deploy it well. Frontline workers, despite widespread concern about displacement, often adapt relatively quickly to AI-assisted tools that make their immediate work easier. The structural resistance — the place where AI initiatives consistently bog down, where information doesn't flow, where pilot successes fail to scale — is in the middle.

The reason is not difficult to find. Middle management derives its organizational value primarily from managing information flows: synthesizing data from below, translating it for leadership above, coordinating across units that don't have direct relationships with each other. These functions are precisely what AI systems disrupt. The regional director's value was her superior synthesized picture of multiple markets. The data analyst's value was his ability to build and interpret financial models. The operations coordinator's value was her synthesis of logistics, staffing, and customer data into a coherent daily picture. When AI systems perform these functions, the organizational rationale for the roles is not eliminated — but it is put under pressure in a way that incumbents feel immediately and viscerally.

The responses this pressure produces are not always cynical. Some middle managers resist AI adoption because they are the organizational members most exposed to its unintended consequences — and their resistance sometimes reflects genuine operational knowledge that senior leaders lack. The inventory manager who overrides AI recommendations may be protecting against failure modes the model hasn't encountered. The compliance manager who slows AI deployment in a regulated function may be protecting against liability risks the technology team hasn't modeled.

But executives who cannot distinguish between legitimate operational caution and structural self-protection will find their AI transformations consistently captured by the layer of the organization with the most to lose from them. This requires directness that most corporate cultures resist: naming the dynamic clearly, separating the people who are raising substantive objections from those who are raising substantive-sounding objections that serve other purposes, and designing adoption strategies that do not give veto power to the most threatened stakeholders.

The data-siloing example from the consumer packaged goods company illustrates this precisely. The unit leaders who controlled customer data were not wrong to understand that sharing it would reduce their leverage. They were wrong — strategically wrong, as stewards of the enterprise — to use that leverage to block organizational capability that would have benefited the whole. But in the absence of executive intervention that explicitly reshaped the incentive, that was exactly the rational choice.

## Incentives Are the Architecture

The most durable management insight about organizational change is also the most consistently ignored: incentive systems are not HR concerns. They are the actual architecture of organizational behavior.

Alfred Chandler's foundational work on the relationship between strategy and structure established that structural change must follow strategic intent — and that organizations that allow their current structure to constrain their strategic choices will consistently underperform those that redesign their structures around the strategy. What Chandler called "structure," expanded to include the full incentive system, is precisely what executives must redesign if AI adoption is to generate the value it promises.

The law firm's experience with email in the 1990s provides the clearest recent example. Studies of large law firms found systematic resistance to email that had nothing to do with technical literacy. The resistance was structural. Law firm economics depended on billable hours, and email — faster than letter-writing — compressed the time required to complete tasks partners had been billing by the hour for decades. Partners who resisted email were not technophobic; they were protecting a business model. The firms that adopted email fastest were not the most technically sophisticated. They were the ones that simultaneously renegotiated their billing structures, moving toward flat fees and retainer models that rewarded efficiency rather than penalizing it. Technology adoption and business model innovation had to happen together. Neither worked without the other.

This is the principle executives must internalize for AI. Performance management systems that reward the production of analytical work will create organizations that treat AI-assisted production as a threat to their measured output. Compensation structures that reward information control will create organizations that resist AI-enabled transparency. Promotion criteria that value technical expertise over decision quality will create organizations that use AI to produce better technical outputs while atrophying the judgment skills that AI's advent makes more rather than less important.

Redesigning these structures is harder than deploying the technology, it takes longer, and it generates more organizational friction. But it is the constraint. Executives who have grasped this are redesigning performance systems around outcome quality rather than output volume — measuring the quality of decisions made, not the quantity of analyses produced. They are restructuring compensation to reward organizational transparency and data-sharing rather than data-hoarding. They are rewriting promotion criteria that prize the ability to work effectively with AI systems and to make good judgments using AI-generated intelligence, rather than the ability to produce intelligence manually.

## Deliberate Caution vs. Structural Avoidance

The argument so far could be read as an indictment of organizational resistance to AI adoption — a brief for moving faster and accepting more disruption. That reading would be wrong, and it would give executives a license to do real damage.

Not all organizational slowness is failure. The counter-argument deserves genuine engagement: executives who move carefully on AI are often doing exactly what responsible stewardship requires. They are managing real risks around data privacy, regulatory compliance, liability, reputational damage, and workforce disruption that technically enthusiastic proponents routinely underweight.

A national hospital network that implemented AI-assisted diagnostic tools and found the rollout took six years was not simply being bureaucratic. Physicians who declined to rely on AI diagnostic recommendations in cases where their clinical intuition conflicted were protecting against genuine liability exposure. The legal framework for medical malpractice had not resolved who bears responsibility when an AI-assisted diagnosis is wrong and a physician follows it. Until that framework clarified, a rational physician would be cautious regardless of the technology's accuracy. The bottleneck was not organizational resistance; it was institutional environment that had not yet adapted. The hospital's job was to navigate that environment carefully, not to override it.

The distinction that matters is between deliberate caution and structural avoidance.

Deliberate caution is grounded in specific, articulable risks. It has an identifiable trigger for change — a resolved regulatory question, an updated liability framework, a sufficient evidence base from controlled deployment. It is proportionate: the caution is matched to the magnitude of the risk, not to the comfort of the status quo. It is visible: the people exercising it can explain exactly what they are waiting for.

Structural avoidance has none of these properties. It invokes risk without specificity. It cannot name the conditions under which adoption would proceed. Its real function is to protect existing arrangements — job security, budget authority, organizational influence — under the cover of prudent risk management.

The diagnostic question executives must ask, of themselves and of the people raising objections to AI initiatives, is: what would it take to change your mind? If the answer is a specific thing — a resolved liability question, a demonstrated accuracy threshold, a pilot success in a particular context — the caution is probably genuine. If the answer is vague, shifting, or structurally impossible ("we need more certainty than the technology can provide"), the avoidance is probably structural.

The regional telecom company that deployed AI customer service tools and achieved cost reductions, then found three years later that customer satisfaction and retention had declined, is a cautionary example of a different kind. The company had moved quickly and had not asked the right questions before it did. The AI system had been optimized for resolution speed — what the operations team measured. What customers actually valued, in complex billing disputes, was feeling heard. No one had measured this; no one had encoded it in the system's objectives. The fast adopter paid a price for moving quickly without thinking clearly about what the right objective was. This is not an argument for slowness; it is an argument for clarity — about what problem you are actually trying to solve and what success genuinely looks like.

## The Redesign Imperative

The executives who will capture the value of AI are not those who adopt AI systems most aggressively. They are those who use the AI transition as a forcing function to redesign their organizations around the informational and capability environment that AI creates.

This is not a comfortable framing. Organizational redesign is harder, messier, more politically difficult, and more personally threatening than technology adoption. It requires naming structural changes that produce winners and losers. It requires rewriting the implicit contracts that organizational life rests on — the understandings about what roles are for, what expertise is valued, and what career paths lead to what destinations. It requires, in Chandler's terms, letting strategy lead and letting structure follow, even when the existing structure has significant institutional momentum.

The printing press analogy provides the honest version of this dynamic. The institutional custodians of Europe's pre-Gutenberg information environment — the monasteries that controlled manuscripts, the Church hierarchy that controlled interpretation, the universities that controlled scholarly authority — did not lose their monopolies gradually or gracefully. The new organizational forms that unlocked the press's potential — the periodical, the scientific journal, the pamphlet, the commercial catalogue — were invented primarily by outsiders to the established order, not by its incumbent institutions adapting in time. The organizations best positioned to exploit AI are not necessarily the largest or most established. They are the ones with the organizational flexibility — which is partly structural, partly cultural, and substantially about leadership will — to invent new forms rather than improve old ones.

The bottleneck is not technical. The AI systems are available, effective, and becoming more so. The bottleneck is human — which means it is exactly where executive responsibility lies.

The productive version of this chapter's argument is not a call to move recklessly or to dismiss the legitimate concerns of people whose roles are genuinely threatened by AI's advance. It is a call to diagnostic honesty: to look clearly at where organizational resistance is grounded in legitimate risk and where it is grounded in structural self-protection, to design incentive systems that align the organization's behavior with its strategic intent, and to ask, with genuine seriousness, what the right organizational design is for a world in which the informational constraints that shaped the current design no longer apply.

The factory owners who redesigned around electricity, rather than simply replacing the steam engine, did not do it because they were braver or smarter than the ones who didn't. They did it because they grasped, at the right moment, that the technology had changed what was possible — and that the possible only becomes actual when the organization is designed to realize it. They asked the question that the technology made newly urgent: if we no longer need to organize around the old constraint, what do we want to build instead?

That question is now available to executives in every industry. The constraint that shaped the modern corporation — the scarcity and cost of information synthesis, the need for hierarchical layers to move intelligence up through the organization, the organizational overhead required to sustain analytical capability — has been disrupted. The window for redesign is open.

The question is whether the organizations with the most to gain from redesign will move before the ones with the most to lose from it succeed in keeping things the way they are.

That question has always been, at its core, an executive question. It is now the most important one on the agenda.
