# Critique: p2_strategy — Strategy Draft

## Criterion 1: Thesis Alignment

**Core thesis to test against:** AI makes analytical work cheap and fast, which does not reduce the strategist's importance—it increases it, because the decisions that require human judgment are now exposed rather than buried under analysis.

---

**Issue 1.1 — The Long Horizon Problem section partially drifts from the thesis.**

> "These long-horizon decisions are not only more consequential than routine strategic analysis—they are qualitatively different. They require what the philosopher Frank Knight called 'uncertainty' rather than 'risk': a situation where the future states of the world cannot be listed, let alone assigned probabilities."

**Problem:** This section is strong and interesting, but it argues a somewhat different claim than the chapter's core thesis. The thesis is that AI *exposes* judgment decisions by clearing away analytical work. The long horizon section argues that AI simply *can't help* with long-horizon decisions—a subtly different point about AI's limits rather than AI's revealing function. The section doesn't land back on the core argument: that AI makes the judgment problem clearer. It ends on the philosophical point about uncertainty, which is compelling but leaves the "so what for the strategist" implicit.

**Fix:** Add a paragraph at the end of this section that reconnects to the thesis: long-horizon decisions are where the judgment problem is most exposed, not just most severe. AI's very inability to model genuine uncertainty makes it a clarifying tool—if you've used AI to model everything it can model and the answer remains uncertain, you know you are in judgment territory. That's information.

---

**Issue 1.2 — The opening section could tie the Eisenhower story more tightly to the thesis before proceeding.**

> "None of it answered the question that mattered: Normandy or Calais?"

**Problem:** The opening is vivid and well-constructed, but the paragraph that follows ("Organizations deploying AI systems...") moves quickly to contemporary application without explicitly naming what principle the Eisenhower story is illustrating. A reader unfamiliar with the chapter's thesis could read the opening as an argument that AI is insufficient for strategy rather than that AI *reveals* the judgment problem. The distinction matters: one is a limitation argument, one is an amplification argument.

**Fix:** Add one sentence after the Eisenhower paragraph that names the pattern explicitly before applying it to contemporary organizations. Something like: "The analytical apparatus didn't fail. It succeeded in clarifying exactly where analysis ended and judgment began—and that is what made the decision visible as a decision, rather than a conclusion waiting to be drawn."

## Criterion 2: Aging Risk

**Issue 2.1 — No specific AI tool or model names appear in the draft.** This criterion is satisfied. The draft uses "AI systems" consistently and avoids product names, company names, and model references. The examples use "a major global retailer," "a major international bank," and "an aerospace manufacturer"—all correctly anonymized. No fix needed.

**Issue 2.2 — The phrase "five years ago" in the opening creates a mild dating risk.**

> "Scenario models are faster and more comprehensive than anything available five years ago."

**Problem:** This creates a relative time anchor that will feel stale as the book ages. The claim also doesn't add analytical weight—readers already know AI has improved.

**Fix:** Delete the phrase "than anything available five years ago" or replace with an enduring framing: "Scenario models run at a speed and scale that would have required dedicated analytical teams not long ago." Better yet: cut the sentence and let the Eisenhower parallel do that work—the contrast between 1944 and now is implicit in the structure.

---

## Criterion 3: Argument Quality

**Issue 3.1 — The core argument is clearly stated, but the chapter's thesis sentence doesn't appear explicitly until the final section.**

The chapter builds toward the thesis rather than announcing it early. This is a reasonable structural choice—the opening establishes the problem before the argument—but for a business book, where executives may be reading nonlinearly, the thesis should be visible earlier.

**Fix:** Consider adding a single "thesis sentence" to the end of the opening section, before "What Strategy Actually Is." Something that explicitly names the argument: the goal is not to frame AI as inadequate but to name what it does—clears away the analytical undergrowth and exposes the judgment terrain.

---

**Issue 3.2 — The retailer example is strong but slightly thin on the resolution.**

> "The fix required redesigning the decision process, not the analysis tools—establishing clear decision rights, forcing time-boxed commitments, and separating the strategy synthesis meeting from the strategy decision meeting."

**Problem:** This is the right fix, but the draft names it in a sentence that reads like a list. The retailer example is the most concrete business case in the chapter and deserves slightly more space on the resolution—what specifically did redesigning the decision process look like? The reader can visualize the problem but can't quite visualize the solution.

**Fix:** Expand by two or three sentences: specifically, what happened differently in the "strategy synthesis meeting" versus the "strategy decision meeting"? What was gained? This makes the organizational design implication vivid rather than abstract.

---

**Issue 3.3 — The skeptic's case section handles two objections, but the transition between them is abrupt.**

> "A second objection deserves acknowledgment: AI may not replace the central strategist, but it might make the central strategy function less necessary by enabling better distributed decision-making."

**Problem:** The section opens by announcing it will address "the strongest version" of the argument, handles it well, then pivots to a second objection without signaling the transition. A reader following the argument may not realize a new objection has been introduced.

**Fix:** Add a brief transitional sentence: "There is a second objection, less philosophical but structurally important to address." This is minor but improves the section's navigability.

---

**Issue 3.4 — The Frank Knight reference is philosophically interesting but may feel like an academic detour to a business reader.**

> "They require what the philosopher Frank Knight called 'uncertainty' rather than 'risk': a situation where the future states of the world cannot be listed, let alone assigned probabilities."

**Problem:** Knight's risk/uncertainty distinction is correct and useful, but introducing a philosopher by name in a chapter that has otherwise avoided academic jargon creates a register shift. A senior executive reading this chapter may find it intellectually interesting but feel slightly distanced from the practical argument.

**Fix:** Keep the distinction but de-emphasize the attribution: "...decisions where the future states of the world cannot be listed, let alone assigned probabilities—what strategists call uncertainty rather than risk, and a domain where no amount of historical data produces confident guidance." The distinction survives; the academic register softens.

---

## Criterion 4: Voice Check

**Issue 4.1 — The closing paragraph's final sentence approaches breathlessness.**

> "The work will just look different than it did before."

**Problem:** This ending is a slight anticlimax after a strong penultimate paragraph. "The work will just look different" undersells the argument. The chapter has built a case that the strategist's job is not merely different but more demanding, more exposed, and more consequential. The final sentence should carry that weight.

**Fix:** Replace with a sentence that names what is genuinely new and hard: something about the strategist who can thrive when there is no analytical cover, when the only thing left to do is decide.

---

**Issue 4.2 — One passage in "The New Division of Labor" edges toward tech journalism tone.**

> "Not because the analysis is unimportant, but because it is a form of production, and AI has made production cheap."

**Problem:** The phrase "AI has made production cheap" is accurate but sounds like a technology briefing rather than management thinking. The Gutenberg analogy makes the same point more elegantly in the next paragraph. This sentence is slightly redundant with what follows.

**Fix:** Cut or fold into the Gutenberg paragraph, which makes the point with more authority: "The scholars who adapted quickly understood that their job had shifted from production to interpretation." That sentence does the work; the preceding sentence weakens it.

---

**Issue 4.3 — The OODA Loop section is well-executed but the framing could be slightly tighter.**

> "Military strategist John Boyd developed what he called the OODA loop—Observe, Orient, Decide, Act—as a framework for understanding how competitive advantage is won in fast-moving environments."

**Problem:** This introductory sentence is mildly expository—it reads like an explainer before the actual point. The reader deserves to be brought into the argument faster.

**Fix:** Lead with the insight, then name the framework: "The decisive step in competitive strategy is rarely Observation or Action—it is Orientation, the integration of new information with existing mental models and organizational judgment. This insight, from the military strategist John Boyd's combat decision framework, has a direct application to AI-augmented strategy..."

---

## Criterion 5: Executive Utility

**Issue 5.1 — "What Strategy Actually Is" section ends with an implication that is conceptual rather than actionable.**

> "Understanding this distinction is the starting point for using AI well in strategy. The aspiration and the bet are human work. The analysis is increasingly AI-native. Conflating them produces organizations that are analytically impressive and strategically confused."

**Problem:** This is well-stated as a conceptual point but doesn't tell the executive what to do. After reading this section, what should the reader change on Monday morning?

**Fix:** Add one sentence at the end: "The practical implication: when your strategy process produces an answer, ask whether it is a conclusion drawn from analysis or a choice made in spite of analytical uncertainty. Only the latter is strategy."

---

**Issue 5.2 — The Long Horizon Problem section has no explicit implication for executives.**

The section ends on a philosophical observation about acts of will and leadership without translating to an actionable suggestion.

**Fix:** Add a closing implication: "For executives in long-cycle industries, this is a discipline to cultivate explicitly: identify which of your major decisions fall into genuine uncertainty rather than calculable risk, and protect those decisions from the pressure to produce analytical justification that the situation does not support."

---

## Summary

### Three Things That Work Well

1. **The Eisenhower opening.** Vivid, historically grounded, and directly illustrative of the chapter's argument. Sets the tone for the entire chapter and earns the reader's attention immediately. The parallel between Operations Research in 1944 and AI in strategy functions today is the kind of historical analogy that makes management thinking memorable and durable.

2. **The skeptic's case section.** The philosophical answer to the "AI will eventually automate strategy" argument is genuinely strong—the point that strategy requires establishing the objective function, not just optimizing toward one, is the most intellectually rigorous moment in the chapter. The second counter-argument (democratization of analysis) is also handled well, and the acknowledgment that AI *does* enable better distributed decision-making at the operational level gives the argument credibility.

3. **The cadences section.** The OODA loop application is original and practical. The two-speed rhythm argument—continuous sensing, punctuated by periodic decision—is the most actionable structural idea in the chapter and addresses a gap in most strategy literature. The distinction between planning and sensing is a genuine contribution to how executives think about AI in strategy processes.

### Top 3 Priority Fixes

1. **Strengthen the thesis statement placement and clarity.** The chapter's core argument—that AI exposes judgment rather than eliminating the need for it—should be named explicitly and early, not just demonstrated through the structure. Add a thesis-anchoring sentence at the end of the opening section and at the end of the "Long Horizon Problem" section. (Criteria 1 and 3)

2. **Sharpen the closing.** The final paragraph is strong but the final sentence undersells the chapter's argument. Replace "The work will just look different than it did before" with a sentence that captures what is genuinely demanding about the post-AI strategist's role—the exposure, the accountability, the absence of analytical cover. (Criterion 4)

3. **Add implication sentences to "What Strategy Actually Is" and "The Long Horizon Problem."** Both sections deliver strong analytical content but leave the reader without a clear action. The book's promise to executives is utility: after each section, the reader should know what to do differently. Both sections need one closing sentence that translates insight to implication. (Criterion 5)
