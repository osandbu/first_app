# Critique: p4_obsolete — What Skills Become Obsolete

---

## 1. Thesis Alignment

The chapter's central argument—that many executive skills are depreciating assets, and that honest reckoning with this is the prerequisite for adaptation—holds together well across most of the chapter. The navigator opening serves the thesis directly. The three-category structure in Section 2 is well-anchored in the book's core argument about information scarcity giving way to judgment scarcity.

**Flag:** The closing section ("The Skill Portfolio Reset") drifts slightly from the thesis in its final three paragraphs. The Britannica and navigator conclusions repeat observations made earlier in the chapter without adding new argument. The chapter's thesis is about giving executives a framework for distinguishing appreciating from depreciating skills—but the close returns to historical parallels rather than reinforcing the forward-looking framework. The chapter's last line is strong ("before the market makes it for you") but the two paragraphs preceding it are less focused.

**Fix:** Tighten the close to make it more sharply about the skill portfolio reallocation—what to invest less in, what to invest more in—rather than returning to the historical parallels already visited.

---

## 2. Aging Risk

The chapter is largely clean of aging references. No specific AI products, models, or company names appear. The historical references (GPS, mechanical tabulating machines, Britannica) are well-chosen because they are historical rather than contemporary.

**Flag 1:** The phrase "AI systems" appears throughout, which is the intended style and creates no aging risk.

**Flag 2:** The McKinsey Global Institute research reference in Section 1 ("Research from McKinsey Global Institute...30–40%...") is appropriate—it attributes the finding to type rather than naming a specific report with a date. This is fine.

**Flag 3:** The reference to "HBR research on how much of managerial time is spent in meetings" follows the same pattern. No aging risk.

**No significant changes required on aging grounds.** The chapter is clean.

---

## 3. Argument Quality

**What works:** The three-category structure (information gathering, operational coordination, analytical team management) is analytically rigorous and gives the argument a spine that holds across the chapter's length. The concrete business examples (insurance company, retail conglomerate, media company, logistics company) are specific enough to be convincing and general enough to avoid dating.

**Weakness 1:** The treatment of the Ghoshal/Bartlett framework in Section 3 ("The Organizational Archaeology") is underdeveloped. The chapter names the framework but does not give it enough room to do real work. The insight—that management layers were justified by the information-integration function—is powerful and supports the argument that the obsolescence is structural rather than individual. But the section moves too quickly through this point and into the Britannica example.

**Suggested fix:** Expand the Ghoshal/Bartlett application by one paragraph—show specifically how the "integration" role maps onto the three categories identified in Section 2, reinforcing the structural argument.

**Weakness 2:** The counter-argument section (Section 5, "The Skeptic's Case") is weaker than the rest of the chapter. The pattern recognition objection is raised but the response is somewhat dismissive: "The implicit claim...is not supported by the evidence." This is asserted rather than demonstrated. The reader who holds this view will find the response unsatisfying.

**Suggested fix:** Replace the assertion with a more specific engagement: acknowledge the genuine cases where experienced human pattern recognition is irreplaceable (novel situations, ethical complexity, reading specific individuals), then tighten the argument that this concession does not rescue the general claim. Give the objector something, then explain why that something does not change the conclusion.

**Weakness 3:** The skill atrophy point (pilot/autopilot analogy in Section 5) is compelling but brief. It deserves more development because it is one of the most counterintuitive and important points in the chapter—the idea that the executives who use AI most may be building the most risk into their capabilities long-term.

---

## 4. Voice Check

The chapter generally maintains the right voice—management thinker, not tech journalist. The opening navigator passage is well-paced. The three-category section is appropriately analytical without being dry.

**Flag 1:** The phrase "an honest framework for telling the difference between appreciating assets and depreciating liabilities" (opening section) uses financial language that is effective but should be reinforced later—the chapter never quite completes the "portfolio" metaphor it introduces. The "Skill Portfolio Reset" title in the final section picks it up, but the body of that section doesn't use portfolio language to structure its argument. This is a minor inconsistency of framing.

**Flag 2:** The sentence "Research consistently finds that 30 to 40 percent of senior leadership time is consumed by activities whose primary function is information gathering and synthesis" in the audit section is slightly passive and data-heavy in a way that interrupts the chapter's otherwise direct voice. The audit section would benefit from a single illustrative example rather than the statistical citation.

**Flag 3:** The final section occasionally edges toward the preachy tone the book style guide cautions against: "The most generous thing this book can do is help you make that accounting clearly." This is too self-referential and slightly condescending. Cut this sentence.

---

## 5. Executive Utility

The three audit questions in Section 4 are the chapter's strongest contribution to executive utility—they are specific, actionable, and unsentimental. These work.

**Flag 1:** Section 2 (the three skill categories) is analytically strong but does not close with an implication for executives. After identifying that information gathering, operational coordination, and analytical team management are the three categories at risk, the section ends without telling the reader what to do with this taxonomy. Add a closing sentence or two that makes the actionable implication clear: look at your current week and assign time to each of these categories. The share in categories one through three is the size of your exposure.

**Flag 2:** Section 3 ("The Organizational Archaeology") explains why skills accumulated but doesn't convert this understanding into an executive implication. Knowing that the obsolescence is structural rather than personal is psychologically useful but doesn't tell the reader what to do differently. Add a closing implication: if the displacement is structural, the response must be organizational as well as personal—restructuring the team's functions, not just the individual's time allocation.

**Flag 3:** Section 6 ("The Skill Portfolio Reset") promises more than it delivers. It raises the question of what to invest in but answers it at a high level of abstraction ("judgment, trust, organizational design, ethical reasoning") without connecting back to what the executive should stop spending time on. The utility would be higher if the section closed with a concrete reallocation logic: for every hour currently spent on synthesis activities, invest one hour in judgment-intensive decisions or trust-building activities.

---

## What Works Well

1. **The three-category structure** (information gathering, operational coordination, analytical team management) is the chapter's analytical backbone and one of the book's more rigorous frameworks. It gives executives a precise taxonomy for auditing their own exposure, which is the chapter's core function.

2. **The three audit questions in Section 4** are excellent—specific, uncomfortable, and genuinely diagnostic. They are the most actionable element of the chapter and demonstrate the book's commitment to executive utility over abstract observation.

3. **The historical parallels** (navigator, bookkeeper, encyclopedia editor) are well-chosen and well-deployed. They establish the structural argument without being repetitive, and they reinforce the key insight that this displacement is structural rather than personal.

---

## Top 3 Priority Fixes

1. **Strengthen the counter-argument response in Section 5.** The pattern recognition objection is the strongest one the chapter faces, and the current response dismisses it rather than engaging it. Acknowledge where experienced human pattern recognition genuinely adds value, then explain why the concession does not rescue the general claim. This is essential for the chapter to be credible to skeptical readers.

2. **Add executive implications to Sections 2 and 3.** Both sections analyze the situation well but don't close with what the executive should do differently. The book's style requires that every section end with an implication. Adding one sentence to each section would bring both into compliance with this standard.

3. **Tighten and refocus the closing section.** The final section returns to historical parallels already visited rather than completing the portfolio reallocation logic introduced at the chapter's opening. Revise the close to deliver a specific reallocation framework—what to invest less in, what to invest more in—and cut the self-referential final observation about the book's intentions.
