# Research Notes: p2_strategy — Strategy

## Chapter Argument in One Sentence

AI makes the analytical work of strategy cheap and fast, which does not reduce the
strategist's importance—it increases it, because the decisions that require human
judgment are now exposed rather than buried under the work of analysis.

---

## 1. Historical Parallels and Analogies

### The Printing Press and the Theologian's Job

Before Gutenberg, the Church's interpretive authority rested partly on its monopoly
over text. Scribes copied scripture; clergy interpreted it; ordinary people could not
access either. The printing press destroyed that information monopoly in a generation.
Did it make theologians obsolete? No—it made theology harder. Once everyone could read
the Bible, the contested questions of interpretation became unavoidable. Luther's theses
were nailed to a door in 1517; Gutenberg had printed his Bible around 1455. When
information becomes freely available, the argument about what it means becomes the
central task. The analogy to strategy is direct: AI systems that synthesize competitive
intelligence don't simplify strategy—they force the hard question into the open. What
does all this information mean about where we should go?

### The Rise of the Consigliori and the Fall of the Secretary of State

In Renaissance Florence, the humanist movement produced the first systematic "secretaries
of state"—advisors whose value came from their ability to gather, synthesize, and
translate intelligence for princes. Machiavelli was one. As intelligence networks
improved and information flowed more freely, these roles did not wither—they became more
specialized and more powerful, because the premium shifted from finding information to
making sense of it politically. The prince's advisor who could synthesize and recommend
was worth far more than the one who could only report. AI replicates this dynamic:
synthesis becomes table stakes; the scarce skill is political and organizational
judgment—knowing which coalition supports which direction, which stakeholders will
resist, what the organization can actually execute.

### Operations Research and the D-Day Problem

In World War II, military planners faced a combinatorial problem: how to allocate
scarce shipping tonnage, air cover, and manpower across multiple theaters with
incomplete information. The British and Americans built Operations Research units—
the first systematic use of analytical science to support strategic decisions. What OR
revealed, rather than resolved, was the deeper strategic question: once you know the
efficient frontier of options, which do you choose? The OR analysts at SHAEF could tell
Eisenhower the optimal logistics sequence for the Channel crossing. They could not tell
him to choose Normandy over Calais, a decision that depended on German psychology,
political relationships with the Free French, and a calculated deception operation that
no model could fully represent. AI systems are a quantum leap in OR capability. The
Eisenhower problem remains.

## 2. Key Frameworks from Management Literature

### Porter's Five Forces — as Sensing Tool, Not Blueprint

Porter's Five Forces framework remains valuable as a perceptual structure for strategy,
but AI transforms how it is populated. The work of scanning competitive dynamics—
tracking new entrants, monitoring supplier consolidation, assessing buyer power shifts—
can now happen continuously rather than annually. What changes is not the framework but
the cadence. The strategic insight still requires human judgment about which signals
matter, which competitive moves are credible versus noise, and how your own organization's
capabilities match the landscape. AI populates the Five Forces faster; it cannot weight
them for you.

### Roger Martin's Choice Cascade (Playing to Win)

Martin's framework—that strategy is a set of integrated choices about winning aspiration,
where to play, and how to win—is precisely what AI cannot make. AI can help you
understand what "winning" has looked like in analogous competitive contexts. It cannot
tell you what you aspire to win. It can model outcomes across multiple "where to play"
options with more rigor than ever before. It cannot resolve value disagreements in the
executive team about which markets matter more. The Choice Cascade becomes a useful
frame for identifying exactly where human judgment is irreplaceable: the aspiration and
the bet on how to win are judgment calls; the analysis of which arenas are more or less
attractive is increasingly AI-native work.

### Mintzberg's Emergent vs. Deliberate Strategy

Henry Mintzberg's distinction between deliberate strategy (planned, executed as intended)
and emergent strategy (arising from patterns in action and adaptation) becomes more
important in an AI context, not less. AI systems excel at deliberate strategy work:
modeling scenarios, stress-testing plans, evaluating alternatives against stated
criteria. But emergent strategy—recognizing the pattern in what's actually working,
sensing when the environment has shifted—requires human perception and organizational
attention. One implication is that AI-native strategy processes must preserve space for
emergence; the risk is that AI makes deliberate planning so efficient that organizations
over-plan and under-sense.

### The OODA Loop (Boyd)

Military strategist John Boyd's Observe-Orient-Decide-Act loop, developed for air combat,
has an important insight for AI-augmented strategy: the Orient step—where perception is
shaped by mental models, cultural traditions, and prior analyses—is where decisions are
actually made, and it is the hardest step to compress. AI dramatically accelerates
Observe (data collection and synthesis) and can support Act (scenario modeling for
action planning). It does not replace Orient: the step where an executive integrates
new information with organizational identity, stakeholder relationships, and strategic
intent. Organizations that mistake faster observation for better orientation will move
quickly in the wrong direction.

### Horizon Planning (McKinsey's Three Horizons)

The classic horizon framework—managing core business (H1), building emerging businesses
(H2), and seeding future options (H3)—becomes more dynamic in an AI context. AI
compresses the H1 analytical cycle. But the framework's value is in allocating executive
attention across time horizons, and that allocation is a judgment call. One structural
suggestion for the chapter: AI makes H1 more efficiently manageable, which means
executives should be reallocating more attention to H2 and H3—but most won't, because
the visible, quantifiable work of H1 will always feel more urgent.

## 3. Concrete Business Examples

### A Major Retailer and the Real-Time Intelligence Trap

A major global retailer invested heavily in AI-powered competitive intelligence—pricing
signals, demand forecasting, supplier risk—and found that the quality of its strategic
analysis improved dramatically while its strategic decision-making slowed. The problem:
more information generated more meetings to review it, more options to analyze, more
stakeholder alignment to achieve. The lesson is that AI-accelerated analysis without
AI-accelerated decision processes creates a new kind of analysis paralysis. The fix
required redesigning the decision process, not the analysis tools—establishing clear
decision rights, forcing time-boxed commitments, and separating the strategy synthesis
meeting from the strategy decision meeting.

### A Global Bank Repositioning Across Regulatory Environments

A major international bank used AI systems to model regulatory scenarios across
jurisdictions as the global compliance environment shifted after 2008. The AI output was
technically impressive: it could map the full regulatory landscape and flag
incompatibilities in the bank's current strategy across markets. What it could not do
was tell the bank whether to invest in changing its operating model to serve a contested
regulatory environment or to exit certain markets strategically. That decision required
judgments about the bank's identity, its relationships with regulators, and what kind of
institution it wanted to be over a decade—factors not reducible to regulatory compliance
modeling.

### An Aerospace Manufacturer and the Thirty-Year Bet

Decisions in industries with decades-long product cycles—defense, aviation, heavy
infrastructure—expose what AI cannot do in strategy. A major aerospace manufacturer
faces product development commitments that will shape the company's competitive position
for thirty or forty years. AI can model demand scenarios, supply chain options, and
technology development curves with more sophistication than ever. But the fundamental
bet—which technologies will mature, which regulatory regimes will hold, which geopolitical
relationships will anchor demand—requires human judgment operating under radical
uncertainty, with no historical analogue and no data that reaches far enough. In these
long-horizon decisions, AI is genuinely useful for checking internal consistency and
stress-testing assumptions; it cannot replace the bet.

### A Consumer Goods Company and the Portfolio Dilemma

A global consumer goods company used AI-driven market analysis to make a portfolio
decision: which of its brands to double down on as consumer preferences shifted. The AI
synthesis was clear that three of its heritage brands faced structural headwinds. What
it could not resolve was the organizational and identity question: these brands were
the company's founding story. Divesting them was analytically obvious and organizationally
wrenching. The strategic question was not "what does the data say" but "who are we
willing to become." That is not an AI question.

### A Technology Company Entering a New Market

A technology company considering entering an adjacent hardware market used AI to run
scenario analysis on regulatory, competitive, and supply chain dimensions. The analysis
identified a narrow but viable path. The decision to enter required a judgment call
about whether the company's culture—built on software—could make the organizational
transformation required to compete in hardware. AI could not assess organizational
capability to change, the specific executive team's likelihood of success, or the
cultural capital required to navigate a manufacturing-intensive business. The
recommendation was analytically supportable; the judgment about whether this team could
execute it was not.

### A Media Company and the Platform Shift

A major media company faced the classic platform transition: its core business—print and
broadcast—was in structural decline, and digital adjacencies were available but required
fundamental business model change. AI systems could model each scenario's economics
in detail. What they could not do was resolve the values disagreement at the top of the
organization: some executives saw the company as a content creator, others as a
distribution platform. These were not analytical disagreements. They were disagreements
about what the company was for. The strategic choice depended on resolving that
identity question first—and that conversation had to be human.

## 4. Counter-Arguments and Responses

### Counter-Argument 1: "AI will eventually automate strategic decisions too."

The objection: as AI systems improve, they will not merely analyze options but recommend
among them with increasing confidence and accuracy. Autonomous strategy is the logical
endpoint. Why believe the strategist's judgment is permanently irreplaceable rather than
temporarily ahead of the technology?

Response: The strongest version of this argument deserves a serious answer. AI systems
can optimize toward specified objectives across known solution spaces. Strategy is
precisely the act of defining the objectives and establishing that the relevant solution
space has been correctly identified—both of which require normative judgment, not
optimization. The question "what are we trying to win, and does winning mean what we
think it means?" cannot be answered by a system that requires an objective function.
Additionally, the social and political legitimacy of strategic decisions matters
independently of their analytical quality. Organizations follow directions partly because
they trust the humans who set them; autonomous AI strategy faces a legitimacy deficit
that is structural, not temporary. A decision made by an AI system, even an accurate
one, does not carry the organizational commitment that a human-led decision process
generates. This may be the most durable argument: strategy is partly a social act of
commitment, not just an analytical act of optimization.

### Counter-Argument 2: "AI actually reduces the strategist's importance—good analysis
democratizes strategy."

The objection: if AI makes strategic analysis available to anyone in the organization,
then every manager can now see the competitive landscape clearly and make locally
optimal strategic decisions. You don't need a centralized strategy function anymore.
Strategy becomes distributed, not elevated.

Response: This argument is partially correct—AI does enable better distributed
decision-making at the operational level, and the book should acknowledge this.
But it misunderstands what the strategy function does at the top of the organization.
The problem of organizational direction—of choosing among incommensurable goods, making
bets under genuine uncertainty, and committing the organization to a course of action
that requires sacrifice—is not a problem that better local information solves. In fact,
wider access to good analysis may increase the centrifugal forces in organizations: if
every unit can see the competitive dynamics in its own arena clearly, coordination
and direction become harder, not easier. The strategist's job gets more important
precisely because more people in the organization now have strong analytical views.
Someone still has to integrate them and make the call.

## 5. Data Points and Studies to Reference

- A McKinsey Global Survey on strategy processes finding that less than 30% of
  executives report being satisfied with their company's strategic planning process—
  useful to establish that strategy has always been a broken process, not just a
  pre-AI one. (McKinsey Quarterly, Strategy Practice surveys, ~2018-2022 vintage.)

- Research from MIT Sloan Management Review on AI and managerial decision-making
  suggesting that AI augmentation improves decision quality in structured domains but
  shows diminishing or negative returns in novel, ambiguous decisions—directly supports
  the chapter's thesis about where the boundary lies.

- A HBR study on strategic planning cycle length finding that companies with faster
  planning cycles (quarterly vs. annual) outperform in dynamic markets—supports the
  argument for new strategy cadences.

- The original RAND Corporation work on scenario planning (Herman Kahn, 1960s)—
  useful for establishing that the tools of strategic analysis have always been ahead
  of the wisdom to use them well.

- Research on "decision fatigue" in organizational contexts: more options, even
  high-quality options, reduce decision quality—supports the argument that AI-generated
  analytical abundance can harm strategic decision-making without the right process
  design.

- A Deloitte or BCG survey on AI adoption in strategy functions showing high tool
  adoption but low reported impact on strategic outcomes—use to establish the gap
  between AI deployment and actual strategic benefit.

- Gary Klein's research on Naturalistic Decision Making (NDM)—the observation that
  expert decision-makers in complex, time-pressured environments rely on pattern
  recognition rather than analysis, and that AI may complement but cannot replicate
  this form of expertise.

## 6. Structural Suggestion: Chapter Sections

### Section 1: The Analyst's Trap (Opening)

Establish the paradox: AI is being deployed extensively in strategy functions to
accelerate analysis. Organizations are getting better analysis faster than ever and
are not making better strategic decisions. Why? Because the bottleneck was never
analysis. Open with the historical parallel—printing press, or the OR example—to show
this is a pattern, not a new problem.

### Section 2: What Strategy Actually Is

Distinguish strategy from strategic analysis. Draw on Martin's Choice Cascade and
Mintzberg's emergent/deliberate distinction to establish that strategy is an act of
will, not a conclusion from evidence. What AI does well (populating the analysis) and
what it cannot do (resolving the aspiration, the identity, the bet). This section
should feel like it clarifies a concept that most executives use imprecisely.

### Section 3: The New Division of Labor in Strategy

What the strategy function looks like when AI handles competitive intelligence, scenario
modeling, and stress-testing. The human strategist's job is not eliminated—it shifts.
From analysis to synthesis. From data gathering to question setting. From annual planning
to continuous sensing and periodic decision. Use the retailer and bank examples here.
Be specific about which roles are automated and which are amplified.

### Section 4: New Cadences—From Planning to Sensing

The annual strategy cycle is an artifact of the cost of analysis, not the pace of
competition. When analysis is cheap, planning cadences can compress. But sensing—
recognizing emergent patterns before they're obvious—requires different organizational
habits. Introduce the OODA loop framing and the Mintzberg emergent strategy lens.
The implication: organizations need to redesign not just how they analyze but how they
pay attention. What does a monthly or quarterly strategic sensing rhythm look like?

### Section 5: The Strategist's New Irreplaceability (The Skeptic Turn)

Address the counter-arguments directly. Acknowledge what AI will genuinely do to
democratize analysis and support distributed decision-making. Then make the affirmative
case: the decisions that define an organization's future—the identity questions, the
long-horizon bets, the commitment under uncertainty—become more visible and more
consequential when AI removes the noise of analytical work. The strategist's job is not
safe because AI can't do it yet; it is safe because it is definitionally human work.
Use the aerospace and media examples here.

### Section 6: Implications for Executives — What to Do Differently

Concrete recommendations:
1. Redesign your decision process before your analytical tools. Most strategy
   transformations get this backwards.
2. Identify the two or three questions in your strategy that require genuine judgment
   and protect time to work on them.
3. Separate the synthesis meeting from the decision meeting. Don't let faster analysis
   create the illusion of faster decision-making.
4. Develop the organization's sensing capability, not just its modeling capability. Who
   is watching for signals that don't fit the current model?
5. Be explicit about what you are betting on and why. AI cannot make bets for you, but
   it can make your implicit bets visible.

## Tone and Emphasis Notes

- The chapter should avoid sounding defensive about strategy as a profession.
  The argument is not that AI is less powerful than advertised; it is that more
  powerful analysis creates a clearer view of the judgment problem at the center of
  strategy, which is genuinely hard.
- The printing press analogy is the strongest opening move—use it early to establish
  the historical pattern.
- The section on new cadences is an opportunity to be practical and original; most
  strategy literature doesn't address rhythm and cadence seriously.
- Don't let the counter-argument section feel defensive. The strongest version of the
  argument—that AI will eventually automate strategy—deserves a serious philosophical
  answer, not just a dismissal.
