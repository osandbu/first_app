# Product: The Curation Machine

## How AI Reshapes the Product Management Role

---

Imagine a product team in 1998 tasked with designing a new feature for a consumer software application. The sequence was fixed and slow: someone drafted a brief, a researcher spent three weeks conducting interviews and focus groups, a designer translated findings into mockups over several more weeks, an engineer estimated the work and wrote a technical specification, and a prototype — crude, static, often paper-based — finally landed in front of stakeholders months after the original idea was spoken aloud. The feedback loop from concept to testable artifact ran six months on a good day. The scarcity was obvious: building was expensive, and so the whole organization organized itself around reducing the cost of building wrong.

That world no longer exists. The cost of building a prototype has collapsed so far that a capable product team can now move from a verbal description of a feature to a functional, testable artifact in hours rather than months. AI systems synthesize patterns across thousands of user conversations, support tickets, and behavioral signals in the time a researcher once spent scheduling her first interview. The prototyping phase — once the center of gravity in the entire development process — has become nearly free.

When a bottleneck disappears, the system does not simply speed up. It reorganizes around a new bottleneck. And in product development, the new bottleneck is something organizations have never had to manage as a discrete resource before: the quality of human judgment about what is worth building.

This chapter's argument is simple but its implications are far-reaching: AI compresses the distance between idea and artifact, and in doing so it shifts the entire productive constraint of product development from generation to curation. The PM who thrives in this environment is not the one who can write the sharpest specification or synthesize the most user research. It is the one who can look at fifty credible options and know — with conviction, clarity, and the organizational authority to carry it — which one is worth building.

## I. The Compression

Thomas Edison did not merely invent things. He industrialized the process of invention itself. At Menlo Park, he built a machine designed to generate and test ideas at scale: a team, a physical lab, a process, a supply of raw materials for rapid experimentation. The feedback loop from hypothesis to prototype to verdict shrank from years to weeks. The result, however, was not an automatic flood of finished products. It was a flood of *decisions*: which experiments to kill, which to extend, which to patent and pursue commercially. Edison's genius was not just creative — it was curatorial. He understood that the productive capacity of his invention factory would only be realized if the decision process about what to pursue could keep pace with the generation process.

Product teams working with AI systems now face structurally the same challenge. The generation capacity — idea to prototype, data to insight, concept to variant — has expanded dramatically. The decision capacity has not. And this asymmetry is not a technical problem that will solve itself; it is an organizational design problem that requires deliberate response.

Consider what happens in concrete terms. A product team at a consumer packaged goods company deployed AI systems to synthesize customer feedback across retail, social, and support channels. The time-to-insight for product teams dropped from six weeks — the typical cycle time for a research report — to forty-eight hours. The team expected the bottleneck to dissolve. Instead, it moved. Teams began scheduling more decision meetings per month than they could productively run, and the quality of individual decisions degraded under the volume. The company ultimately had to restructure its product review cadence, treating decision capacity as a managed resource the way manufacturing plants manage line capacity. The technology accelerated generation. The organization had to redesign itself to handle the surplus.

The insight here echoes what happened to finance departments when spreadsheets arrived in the early 1980s. When VisiCalc and then Lotus 1-2-3 made financial modeling cheap, many expected finance teams to shrink. The opposite happened: the cost of building a financial model fell so far that companies could afford to model far more scenarios, ask far more strategic questions, and explore far more options than before. Finance headcount grew in most major organizations. The demand for analysis expanded to fill the new analytical capacity — and then some. Product organizations will traverse the same curve: AI creates more decisions, not fewer, and the skill premium shifts entirely to the quality of those decisions, not the speed of the work that precedes them.

This is what it means to say that building has become cheap and deciding has become expensive. It is not a metaphor. It is a structural reorganization of where organizational value lives.

## II. The New Shape of Discovery

For decades, the discovery phase of product development had a particular texture. User researchers conducted interviews and focus groups. Analysts synthesized behavioral data from product usage. PMs synthesized the synthesis, trying to distill latent customer needs from a jumble of what users said, what they did, and what they might want if given the option. The work was slow not because people worked slowly but because the data collection and pattern-recognition steps were genuinely labor-intensive. A team might spend weeks processing research before the first real product insight emerged.

AI systems have fundamentally altered this texture. Behavioral signals from thousands of users can now be synthesized in hours. Support transcripts, survey responses, and usage patterns can be processed at a scale that would have required an army of analysts a decade ago. A healthcare technology company deployed AI to automate the synthesis of clinical workflow observations that had previously required weeks of ethnographic research to process — and produced insights that traditional research pipelines had consistently missed.

But the healthcare example contains a cautionary note that product leaders should read carefully. The PMs who used the tool most effectively were those with deep clinical domain knowledge. They could identify which AI-synthesized patterns reflected genuine workflow problems versus artifacts of the data collection method itself. PMs without that domain background tended to pursue the most statistically prominent signals — which often represented minor irritants rather than high-stakes problems worth solving. The AI lowered the search cost for patterns. It did not perform the act of recognition — the judgment about which patterns *mattered*.

This finding points directly at Clayton Christensen's most important contribution to product thinking. The Jobs-to-Be-Done framework holds that customers do not buy products; they hire them to accomplish something in their lives. Understanding the *job* a customer is trying to do requires interpretation of why people behave as they do — not just observation of what they do. And AI systems, for all their facility at surfacing behavioral patterns, cannot perform that interpretive act. AI surfaces what users do. It cannot surface why. The gap between behavior and motivation is precisely where product insight lives, and it remains a distinctly human function to close it.

This has an important implication for how executives should think about building their product discovery function. Domain expertise — genuine, deep understanding of the customer's world — becomes more valuable when AI handles the information processing layer, not less. The research skill that remains scarce is not the ability to design a survey or code an interview transcript. It is the ability to look at an AI-generated pattern and know whether it represents a meaningful customer reality or a statistical artifact. That skill is not teachable by training sessions on AI tools. It is built through years of close engagement with the customer's actual situation.

## III. From Specification to Curation

The printing press did not eliminate the author. It elevated the editor to gatekeeper. Before Gutenberg, the scarcity in publishing was the production of text itself — scribes were the bottleneck, and the question of what was worth reproducing almost answered itself: if it was worth the cost of a scribe, it was worth preserving. After the press, text became cheap to reproduce, and a new scarcity emerged: the editor who could judge what was worth printing and for whom. The editor did not generate the ideas. The editor chose among them, shaped them, killed the weaker ones, and positioned the stronger ones for their audience. The editor's role was curatorial.

AI systems are doing something structurally identical in product development. The scarcity was never really in writing specifications, generating wireframes, or running surveys — those were always just the most visible labor. The real scarcity was always in judgment about what problem deserved solving. AI compresses the production side. The editorial function of the product manager becomes the constraint.

A mid-sized enterprise software company grasped this early and acted on it. After deploying AI systems for specification writing and user-story generation, it eliminated two layers of junior PM roles that had primarily served as information processors and specification writers. It doubled its investment in senior PMs with deep domain expertise. Revenue per PM headcount rose substantially within eighteen months. The company's Chief Product Officer described the shift as "moving from a translation business to a judgment business."

That phrase deserves unpacking. The traditional PM role was, in substantial part, a translation business: translating customer needs into engineering-legible specifications, translating engineering constraints into customer-legible roadmaps, translating executive strategy into team-legible priorities. AI systems now handle much of that translation work. What remains is the upstream judgment about what to translate in the first place, and the downstream work of building organizational conviction around the answer.

This means that the new metrics of product management performance cannot be the old ones. Time-to-prototype, specification quality, and survey completion rates measure the translation function. The new metrics should measure the curatorial function. Time-to-insight — how quickly a team moves from data to a confident decision about what to build — reflects the quality of the discovery and synthesis process. Decision quality per sprint — whether the things the team decided to build actually served customers and advanced strategy — reflects the quality of the curation itself. These metrics are harder to capture than traditional PM metrics, which is precisely why most organizations have not built measurement systems around them. That gap represents a significant organizational vulnerability.

The PM who thrives in the AI era is not defined by mastery of product tooling or proficiency with AI systems, though functional competence matters. She is defined by three things: deep understanding of the customer, sharp clarity about what her organization is trying to be, and the political and relational skill to build a coalition around a product decision and carry it to execution. The first two are epistemic virtues. The third is fundamentally interpersonal. None of them are automated by AI.

## IV. The Governance Problem

Speed without governance is not acceleration. It is drift. The retail e-commerce division that tripled its feature velocity after deploying AI systems discovered this in uncomfortable post-mortems: customer satisfaction scores did not improve proportionally, because the speed of iteration had outpaced the team's ability to evaluate whether the features being shipped actually mattered. The team had optimized for production when it needed to optimize for selection. The solution was not to slow down. It was to insert a structured human judgment layer before any AI-generated prototype was approved for development — a "decision quality" review that operated outside the generation process.

This points to a fundamental organizational design question that executives must answer deliberately: what governance structure is appropriate when the generation process is fast and cheap, and the decision process is slow and expensive?

Traditional stage-gate product development was built for the opposite world — one in which building was expensive and decisions were relatively fast. The stage gates existed to prevent expensive building decisions from being made on thin evidence. When building becomes cheap, the logic of stage-gate inverts. You want to generate quickly and decide carefully — not generate slowly and decide at a checkpoint.

But the alternative frameworks in circulation — continuous discovery, lean startup, agile delivery — were not designed for the specific condition that AI creates either. Teresa Torres's continuous discovery model, the most sophisticated current framework for high-frequency learning, compresses feedback cycles between discovery and delivery. AI does not merely accelerate continuous discovery; it structurally dissolves the boundary between discovery and delivery. When AI can generate a functional prototype from a brief conversation with a user, "testing an idea" and "building the thing" begin to collapse into a single activity. The governance model must evolve accordingly.

What this points toward is something that might be called curatorial governance: a lightweight, fast decision layer that is structurally distinct from the generation process, focused not on preventing bad builds but on actively choosing among good options. The financial services firm that used AI systems to generate dozens of product variants for a retail savings product offers a useful precedent. The design and build process compressed from nine months to eleven weeks. But the time senior leaders spent deciding which variants to advance barely changed — and those leaders acknowledged the decision was harder, not easier, because they had more credible options to choose between. The governance function didn't need to be slower. It needed to be more disciplined: clearer about the criteria for selection before the generation process started, so that decision-making could operate at the speed the generation now demanded.

The practical implication is that product organizations need to separate and explicitly design two processes that have historically been tangled together: the generation process, which AI now accelerates dramatically, and the selection process, which requires human judgment and must be deliberately resourced and structured. Treating decision capacity as a managed resource — as the consumer packaged goods company eventually did — is not a nice-to-have. It is the organizational prerequisite for realizing the value of accelerated generation.

## V. The Skeptic's Turn: Won't AI Eventually Curate Too?

The strongest objection to everything argued above is not that AI cannot currently perform the curatorial judgment function. It is that this is a temporary limitation — that curation is simply a future AI capability not yet arrived. If AI systems can already generate product variants, synthesize user research, and rank options by predicted outcome, why shouldn't the judgment layer eventually be automated as well?

This objection deserves a serious answer, not a dismissal. The answer rests on two foundations that are structural rather than technological.

First, product decisions are not optimization problems with known objective functions. They require choosing the objective function itself, which is an act of organizational and strategic identity. A product team deciding whether to build for power users or casual users, for enterprise buyers or individual consumers, for short-term engagement or long-term retention, is not computing an answer from existing data. It is choosing who the company wants to be and whose problems it considers worth solving. That choice involves accountability, stakeholder alignment, and the kind of legitimate authority that organizations deliberately confer on humans. It is not a decision that can be outsourced to a system without changing what the decision *is* — because part of what makes the decision real is that a human being with skin in the game made it and is answerable for it.

Second, product success depends heavily on internal organizational adoption: engineering teams must believe in what they are building, sales teams must be able to explain why it matters, support teams must understand it well enough to explain it to customers. That adoption is partly a political and relational process. A PM who builds the internal coalition to execute a product decision is performing a function that is, at its core, interpersonal — and interpersonal legitimacy remains a distinctly human domain. An AI-generated product recommendation that no one inside the organization can get behind has failed, regardless of its predicted outcome. The product decision is not finished when the analysis is complete. It is finished when the organization is aligned around it. That alignment is earned through human relationships, not computed.

The second common objection is economic rather than philosophical: that AI's productivity gains will simply reduce headcount rather than improve product quality. This argument has historical precedent on its side — many automation waves have reduced employment in the functions they automated. But the spreadsheet/finance parallel cuts the other way. The cost of financial modeling fell so sharply that organizations expanded the scope of financial questions they asked, not contracted it. The relevant question for product organizations is not whether AI reduces the labor required for current product work, but whether it expands the scope of product problems companies can economically attempt. There is strong reason to believe it does. Faster feedback loops make it economically viable to address smaller, more targeted customer problems. The PM role that survives and grows is the one reoriented toward curation and strategy, not the one anchored to specification and synthesis.

## VI. The Hedgehog Problem at Scale

There is a strategic risk in all of this that sits above the organizational design questions, and it is the most important thing an executive can take from this chapter.

Jim Collins, in his analysis of what distinguished great companies from merely good ones, identified a concept he called the Hedgehog — the intersection of three questions: What can you be the best in the world at? What drives your economic engine? What are you deeply passionate about? The companies that achieved sustained greatness were those that could hold to the answer with discipline, resisting the temptation to diffuse their energy across everything they could do.

AI makes it possible to build more. It does not make it wise to do so. In fact, it makes the Hedgehog Concept harder to maintain, not easier. An unfocused product organization can now prototype a hundred mediocre ideas in the time it once took to build one good one. Speed of generation without clarity of strategy is not a productivity gain. It is an acceleration of drift. The consumer electronics manufacturer that used AI-generated synthetic user research to identify a latent customer need succeeded not because the AI identified the insight but because a product leader recognized which signal in AI-generated output pointed toward a genuine opportunity. The AI lowered the search cost. The judgment — that this particular signal, out of hundreds, was worth pursuing — was human. And it was grounded in a clear strategic understanding of what the company was trying to be.

This reframes the Chief Product Officer's job more sharply than any other development in recent years. The CPO's role has always included strategy, but in a world where the team's generative capacity was limited, strategy often expressed itself through roadmap prioritization: a human-scale list of features organized by estimated value and effort. When AI systems expand the team's generative capacity dramatically, the CPO's strategic function must expand to match. The job becomes less about managing a roadmap and more about maintaining the organization's clarity about what it is trying to be — and holding that clarity against the constant gravitational pull of feasible-but-wrong options.

This means the CPO of an AI-native product organization must invest, deliberately and continuously, in the three things that hold strategic focus together: a well-articulated theory of the customer (who matters most and why), a clear understanding of the organizational capabilities that create distinctive value, and a governance structure that channels the team's generative energy toward the options that sit at the intersection of both. Without those three anchors, AI simply accelerates the production of strategic noise.

## VII. What Survives

The most durable insight from every major technology transition in the past two centuries is this: the introduction of a powerful tool does not eliminate the function it accelerates. It concentrates the function in the individuals and organizations best equipped to use the tool well.

When the printing press made text cheap to reproduce, the function of literary judgment did not disappear. It concentrated in the hands of editors, publishers, and critics who could distinguish what was worth reproducing from what was not. When electricity made mechanical power cheap, the function of industrial design did not disappear. It concentrated in the engineers and designers who understood what forms were worth manufacturing at scale. When computing made calculation cheap, the analytical function did not disappear from finance. It concentrated in the people who knew which calculations were worth running and what the results meant.

AI is compressing the work of building products in precisely the same way. The PM function does not disappear. It concentrates in the individuals who can perform the curatorial act — who can look at a hundred AI-generated options and know which one is true to the customer, feasible for the team, and aligned with strategy — and who can bring the organization to believe it and build it.

This is a higher standard than the PM role historically demanded. The specification writer could be good at the function without the strategic clarity or organizational authority to be good at the curatorial act. Those are different competencies. Organizations that recognize the distinction and build their product functions around it will realize the value that AI generation creates. Organizations that do not will find that AI simply lets them make more mistakes faster.

What should executives do differently, concretely?

First, audit the structure of your product organization against the distinction between generation and curation. How much of your PM capacity is still organized around information processing and specification writing — functions AI now performs? How much is organized around decision quality and customer insight — the functions that AI amplifies but cannot perform? The ratio tells you where the restructuring opportunity lies.

Second, invest in domain expertise as a deliberate strategic choice. The healthcare example is not an anomaly. When AI handles the information processing layer, the scarcest and most valuable input to product discovery is not research method or tool proficiency — it is genuine deep understanding of the customer's situation. That understanding is built through proximity, not through better analytics. Build it deliberately.

Third, design your governance structure to separate generation from selection. Give your team's generation capacity the freedom to run fast. Give your selection process the structure it needs to run well. Treat decision capacity as a managed resource with explicit limits, review cadences, and quality criteria — just as you would treat manufacturing capacity or financial capital.

Fourth, make the Hedgehog Concept a living organizational practice, not a framing exercise. AI's expansion of what your team can build makes the discipline of deciding what you will build more important, not less. The CPO's primary job is to hold that line — to maintain organizational clarity about what you are trying to be, and to channel the generative power of AI toward that end rather than away from it.

The compression of building does not diminish the importance of deciding. It makes deciding the whole game. And the organizations that win are not the ones with the most sophisticated AI systems. They are the ones with the clearest judgment about what to build with them.
