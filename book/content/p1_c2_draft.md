# The End of Information Scarcity

---

## The Hierarchy Was an Information Machine

Walk into the headquarters of almost any large corporation built in the twentieth century and you will find, behind the org chart on the wall, a theory of information. The theory is never stated explicitly because it was never a theory — it was just how things worked. Information traveled upward through layers because the layers were where intelligence lived. Regional managers knew more than store managers. Division heads knew more than regional managers. The executive suite synthesized a picture of the enterprise that no individual below it could assemble. The hierarchy was not primarily a coordination mechanism. It was a compression algorithm, designed to extract usable signal from the noise of a complex organization and deliver it, concentrated, to the people who needed to act on it.

This was a rational response to a specific informational environment — one in which generating, transmitting, and synthesizing information was expensive, slow, and geographically constrained. A retailer with three hundred stores in fifteen states could not afford to give every department head a real-time picture of the competitive landscape. That picture existed only at headquarters, and only in fragmented form, assembled by teams of analysts over days and weeks. The layers of management were the infrastructure through which partial pictures were combined into a coherent whole.

Jay Galbraith, writing in 1974, gave this a formal name: information processing theory of organizations. Galbraith's insight was that organizational structure exists primarily to manage uncertainty — and that uncertainty is, at its core, an information problem. Build more hierarchy when information is scarce and decisions require integration. Build flatter structures when information flows freely and local judgment can be trusted. The shape of the organization, in Galbraith's model, is a function of its information environment.

The information environment has changed. Not gradually and not partially. The synthesis that forty analysts produced over three weeks can now be generated in hours. The market intelligence that required a dedicated research team can now be surfaced on demand. The executive briefing that passed through five editorial filters before reaching the C-suite can now be assembled from raw data in real time, without the filters. The information cost structure that justified the twentieth-century hierarchy — and that Galbraith assumed as a fixed condition — has been disrupted.

This is not a minor efficiency improvement. It is a structural shift in the premise on which most large organizations are built.

## The Bloomberg Moment

In the 1980s, financial markets ran on scarcity. Real-time price data — which security had just moved, what the bid-ask spread was across markets, how a bond was trading in London while New York slept — was available only to firms with the resources to subscribe to expensive terminal systems. The firms with the terminals had a genuine information advantage. They could see the market; their counterparts could not.

The Bloomberg terminal changed that. Within a decade, real-time market data had become a commodity. Any firm of meaningful size could see the same information simultaneously. The information advantage that had sustained certain financial firms' competitive positions evaporated. The edge in financial markets shifted, almost overnight, from access to interpretation — from who had the data to who could reason about it most effectively.

AI systems are the Bloomberg terminal for corporate intelligence. This shift is not approaching; it is underway. The CEO whose competitive advantage rested on having a better-synthesized picture of her industry than her rivals is now in the position of the 1985 bond trader whose edge was a Reuters feed. The data is becoming available to everyone. The question is what you do with it.

A major global retailer experienced this in a form that was more disorienting than clarifying. After deploying AI systems to synthesize customer data, supply chain signals, and competitor pricing in real time, the firm found that its store managers were receiving better-informed briefs than their regional directors. The power of the regional director role — historically justified by the director's superior information about multiple markets — was quietly hollowed out. The company faced a structural question it had not expected to answer so soon: what do regional directors do when they are no longer the primary conduit of market intelligence upward through the organization?

A global investment bank faced the same question from a different angle. Analysts at the firm spent roughly sixty percent of their time aggregating and formatting data for managing directors. When AI systems handled this work, an uncomfortable question surfaced: the analysis that managing directors had been requesting was largely backward-looking and confirmatory rather than genuinely forward-looking. The synthesis function had been masking a deeper deficit in the quality of judgment being applied. The bank's challenge became not efficiency but deciding what better analysis should actually look like. They had fixed the production problem and discovered the judgment problem waiting behind it.

## What Scarcity Moves To

When a resource stops being scarce, the value it was generating doesn't disappear. It migrates. The relevant question after any abundance transition is not "what did we lose?" but "what is now the binding constraint?"

Three things become scarce as information becomes cheap.

The first is the judgment to know which information matters and which does not. This is not a trivial distinction. In the pre-AI world, the volume of available information was the primary constraint; organizations processed what they could and discarded the rest. In a world where AI systems can synthesize any available signal at low marginal cost, the binding constraint becomes selective attention. Which markets are worth monitoring? Which customer signals are predictive of behavior change? Which competitive intelligence is genuinely strategic versus merely interesting? The OODA loop — the Observe-Orient-Decide-Act cycle that defines competitive advantage in dynamic environments — is compressed dramatically at the Observe stage by AI systems. The bottleneck shifts to Orient: interpreting what the observations mean, and to Decide: acting on that interpretation before the opportunity closes.

A major health system illustrated this dynamic when it deployed AI systems to surface integrated data across physician and administrative domains simultaneously. Rather than enabling faster decisions, the initial result was conflict. Executives and clinicians were looking at the same data and disagreeing about what it meant — which signals indicated a quality problem requiring immediate intervention, which indicated a staffing problem requiring a different kind of response, which indicated a patient population shift requiring a strategic decision. Information abundance exposed a judgment deficit that had been hidden inside an information scarcity. They had not previously disagreed about what the data meant, because they had each been looking at different subsets of it.

The second scarce resource is prioritization capacity. Information abundance without prioritization discipline is not an advantage — it is a new kind of cognitive trap. Organizations that deploy AI systems to generate more information, faster, without redesigning how they decide what to act on, find that they have traded one bottleneck for a more pernicious one. The executive whose inbox now contains AI-synthesized intelligence from fifty sources has a curation problem, not an information problem. The meetings that were slow because participants hadn't been briefed are now slow because participants were briefed on too many things and can't agree on which matter most.

A major automotive manufacturer illustrated this with uncomfortable precision. The firm's executive committee historically met weekly to review a consolidated briefing prepared by a team of forty analysts over several days. When AI systems began producing equivalent briefings in hours, the committee's meeting agenda did not change. The bottleneck, it turned out, was not information gathering — it was the committee's capacity to reach decisions. The AI system had accelerated the input. The decision-making architecture had not changed to match.

The third scarcity is trust. The willingness to act on information — especially information that arrives faster than full organizational buy-in can be constructed — requires trust in the source, in the framework, and in the judgment of the decision-maker. F.A. Hayek observed in 1945 that the knowledge required for good economic decisions is dispersed across individuals, none of whom can fully communicate what they know in explicit form. Price signals in markets were his solution for aggregating tacit, distributed knowledge into actionable signal. The analogous problem inside organizations has been partially solved by AI systems, which can aggregate explicit information at scale — but Hayek's deeper point survives. Tacit knowledge, contextual judgment, and trust relationships remain beyond what any system can fully capture. When an AI synthesis tells a regional director that a particular market is showing decline, and the regional director's thirty years of experience with that market says otherwise, the question is not whose analysis is right. It is which form of knowledge — the synthesized explicit or the cultivated tacit — is more reliable in this context. That is a judgment call. And it requires trusting someone's judgment enough to act on it.

## The Politics of Symmetry

Executives who expect AI to make their organizations more rational are in for a specific kind of disappointment. Information asymmetry has never been merely informational.

Consider what information control has historically provided inside organizations. The regional manager who synthesized market intelligence for the division head had genuine informational value to offer — but also structural power. Her position in the reporting chain was partially justified by her role as a relay node. The analyst who built the financial model was not just producing information; he was the only person who understood the model's assumptions, which gave him a form of leverage over the conclusions it would yield. The manager who controlled the customer data that determined budget allocations had organizational influence that was not solely a function of her formal authority.

When AI systems reduce information asymmetry, they do not eliminate organizational politics. They expose the underlying power structures those asymmetries were sustaining.

A large consumer goods company discovered this when AI systems gave frontline marketing managers access to the same synthesized market data previously available only to the CMO. Within eighteen months, several brand managers had made product decisions that outperformed centrally-directed campaigns. The response from senior leadership was ambivalent: the results were welcomed; the erosion of central authority was not. The episode revealed that information asymmetry had been serving a political function — not merely an informational one.

This is the strongest counter-argument to the optimistic reading of AI's organizational implications, and it deserves honest engagement. AI systems do not create neutral information environments. Someone decides which data feeds the AI receives. Someone designs the questions it is asked. Someone sets the parameters that determine which signals it surfaces and which it filters. The executive who controls those design decisions holds new information advantages — ones that are potentially less visible and therefore harder to challenge than the old ones.

The response to this concern is not to deny it but to design against it. Transparency in AI system design — documenting which data sources are used, which assumptions are encoded in the synthesis framework, which outputs are surfaced and which suppressed — is not a technical afterthought. It is the governance infrastructure of the new information environment. Executives who build this transparency into AI deployment create organizations capable of genuine distributed decision-making. Those who allow it to remain opaque simply recreate information asymmetry at a new level — one that is harder for their organizations to see and harder for their competitors to counter, but also harder for themselves to correct when it fails.

## Decision Architecture, Redesigned

If the information architecture that justified the twentieth-century hierarchy no longer exists, the hierarchy built on that architecture is in need of redesign.

This is not an argument for eliminating organizational structure. It is an argument for ensuring that the structure matches the informational environment it operates in — which is what Galbraith's theory has always implied. The practical implication is that executives must now ask explicitly what each layer of management is for, in a world where information no longer needs to travel through it.

Some functions that organizational layers historically performed are already being automated: compiling reports, translating field data upward, filtering and summarizing for executives who couldn't see everything. These functions disappear with the information cost that created them. What remains is what organizational layers always also did but rarely made explicit: making judgment calls about what matters, bearing accountability for decisions, building the coalitions that turn decisions into actions.

Agency theory — Jensen and Meckling's framework for understanding the misalignments between principals and agents — suggests a related implication. Principal-agent problems are sustained by information gaps. When the agent knows things the principal does not, that asymmetry creates space for misaligned interests to operate undetected. Much of the overhead of large organizations — audit functions, compliance requirements, supervisory layers — exists to close those gaps. When AI systems make information more symmetric, some of that overhead becomes redundant.

But new agency questions emerge. The agent who once had an information advantage now faces a different strategic choice: how to use AI system design as a mechanism for maintaining influence. The principal's new accountability is not just to ask better questions but to ensure that the AI systems being used to answer them are designed with appropriate transparency and governed with appropriate oversight. The information environment has changed. The political environment has not.

## Judgment as the New Scarcity

There is a version of the future that looks like this: every executive has access to the same synthesized picture of their competitive environment. Information flows freely, both inside organizations and between them. The barriers to market intelligence that previously protected established positions are gone. In that world, what does the executive offer that cannot be replicated by any competitor with the same AI tools?

The answer is judgment.

The multinational professional services firm that discovered AI systems allowed its junior staff to produce comparable synthesis work faced this question directly. Partners who had justified high billing rates partly on the basis of pattern recognition built from years of engagements found their informational advantage under pressure. The firm arrived at an answer that had always been true but had never been tested so directly: the professional's distinctive contribution was relationship trust, contextual judgment, and accountability for outcomes. AI systems could produce the analysis. They could not sit in the room with a client, read the political dynamics of the organization, make the call about which recommendation the client was actually capable of implementing, and stake their professional reputation on the outcome.

This is the enduring principle that AI's advance reveals rather than challenges. Peter Drucker identified the knowledge worker's distinctive contribution in 1959 as the application of specialized knowledge to the firm's problems. The knowledge that AI systems commoditize is retrievable and explicit — the kind that can be trained on and reproduced. What cannot be commoditized is the knowledge embedded in judgment: the capacity to read a situation's context accurately, to know when the model is missing something, to make the call when the data runs out, and to stand behind the outcome.

The executive who grasped what the Bloomberg terminal meant was not the one who subscribed to it first. It was the one who understood that the terminal had changed what the game was — that the edge was no longer in the data but in the reasoning applied to it. The same realization is available now to executives who are willing to look clearly at what the AI transition is actually changing. Information is no longer the scarcity. Judgment, prioritization, and trust are.

The organizations that internalize this and redesign around it — building decision architectures that assume cheap information and invest in the human capacities that AI cannot replicate — will establish the competitive positions that matter. The ones that use AI to produce better versions of the same reporting they were already generating, in organizations still structured around the information hierarchy that no longer makes sense, will find that they have made themselves more efficient at a game that is no longer being played.

The hierarchy was an information machine. The information problem it was built to solve has been substantially solved — not by better management practice but by a technological shift in the cost structure of intelligence. What remains is the question that the hierarchy's designers never had to ask explicitly, because information scarcity made it moot: what, in the absence of that constraint, is the organization actually for?

That question is now the executive's to answer.
