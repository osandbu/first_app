# What Becomes More Valuable

In 1455, Johannes Gutenberg completed his first Bible. Within fifty years, printing presses across Europe had produced more books than all the scribes of the previous thousand years combined. The scribes—who had controlled access to written knowledge with the same gravity that executives today control access to organizational intelligence—did not disappear immediately. But their authority did. The ability to copy a manuscript, which had been rare and therefore powerful, became irrelevant as text production became abundant.

What happened to value in the aftermath is instructive. It did not evaporate. It relocated. The theologian who could interpret scripture became more valuable, not less—because now that everyone could hold the text, the question of what it meant and how to act on it became pressing in a way it had never been when most people couldn't read at all. The jurist who could synthesize the emerging body of printed law became indispensable. The scientist who could evaluate and interpret the natural philosophy flowing from the new presses became the century's intellectual heroes. The printing press made information abundant; it made interpretation scarce. And scarcity, as always, is where value concentrates.

We are inside a structurally identical transition. AI is making information, analysis, and execution abundant. The question is not whether this changes the executive's job—it does, profoundly—but where value concentrates as a result. This chapter argues that the answer is already visible in the economics of scarcity: judgment under uncertainty, the capacity to build trust, organizational design sensibility, aesthetic taste, the courage to ask the right questions, and the distinctly human ability to give meaning to work. These capabilities are becoming scarcer as AI makes everything else more abundant. And scarcity, as always, is where value concentrates.

## The Economics of Scarcity

Jay Barney's framework for competitive advantage argues that sustainable value derives from resources that are valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable. As AI systems make analysis, synthesis, and execution abundant, these capabilities fail the rarity test. Any organization that deploys comparable AI infrastructure can produce comparable analytical output. The competitive moat that once derived from having better information—a more sophisticated planning process, a larger research function, a more rigorous reporting cycle—disappears when every organization can produce that output with the same tools.

What satisfies Barney's criteria in an AI world? Executive judgment that is unique to an individual's experience and context, shaped by decisions made under real stakes over years. Trust relationships that are built through consistent behavior over time and cannot be transferred, sold, or replicated by deploying software. Organizational culture that is genuinely specific to the people in it and the history it has lived through. Aesthetic sensibility that reflects a cultivated point of view about what excellence looks like in a particular domain. These capabilities are not evenly distributed, cannot be easily observed, cannot be instantly deployed, and become more valuable—not less—as execution becomes commoditized.

This is not sentiment. It is economics. The value of any capability is partly a function of its scarcity relative to its complements. When analytical execution was scarce, it commanded premium pricing. When AI makes it abundant, the complement—the human capacity to judge what the analysis means and what to do about it—becomes the scarce input. The same logic that drove up the wages of the knowledge worker when physical labor was abundant now drives up the value of the judgment worker when analytical labor is automated.

History shows this pattern repeating across major technology transitions. When electrification spread through manufacturing in the early twentieth century, engineering competence—which the transition itself had made more widely available—gradually became less of a premium. What became scarce was design and brand: the capacity to determine what to produce and for whom. Alfred Sloan understood that when production becomes cheap, the question of what to produce becomes the irreplaceable function. His strategy of variety and market segmentation overtook the Model T's engineering triumph. AI follows the same pattern: when execution is cheap, the question of what to execute and why is where value concentrates.

## Judgment Under Uncertainty

The word "judgment" is used loosely. It is worth being precise about what it means, because precision makes clear why AI cannot replicate it and why it compounds with experience in ways that information processing does not.

Judgment, in the sense that matters here, is not having the right answer. It is the capacity to make the best decision available given incomplete information, under genuine time pressure, with significant consequences for being wrong, in circumstances where additional information would not resolve the uncertainty. The last part is crucial: genuine judgment situations are those where more data, better models, and longer analysis would not definitively settle the question. The judgment call is required precisely because the situation exceeds what analysis can resolve.

Philip Tetlock's research on forecasting is relevant here. His work on "superforecasters"—people who outperform experts and prediction markets at making probabilistic judgments—established that the qualities distinguishing the best forecasters are intellectual humility, the willingness to update beliefs when evidence changes, comfort with genuine uncertainty rather than false precision, and the discipline to aggregate diverse perspectives rather than overweight their own. These qualities are not technical. They are dispositional and they are developable. They are also precisely the qualities that AI cannot replicate—not because AI lacks intelligence, but because the forecaster's edge comes from knowing the limits of their own knowledge, a metacognitive orientation that AI systems are not designed to maintain.

AI does not diminish the importance of judgment—it reveals it. When AI handles information synthesis, the quality of the executive's judgment becomes more visible and more consequential. Before AI, a poor judgment call could be attributed to incomplete data or inadequate analysis. When the analysis is excellent and rapidly produced, the quality of the decision made from it is the only variable left. AI strips away the analytical noise and exposes the signal of judgment quality.

A global bank's Chief Risk Officer described this experience directly after deploying AI for credit risk analysis. The models were more accurate on historical patterns than any human team could achieve. But what the models could not do was assess the risk of a client relationship in a context with no historical precedent, evaluate a borrower's character in a privately negotiated deal, or make the call when regulatory pressures and competitive dynamics pointed in opposite directions. His job became almost entirely concentrated in the decisions the model explicitly flagged as beyond its confidence interval—the judgment calls at the tail of the distribution, where human accountability mattered most. His value did not decrease. It became more specific and more legible.

This is the deeper implication for executives. AI does not ask less of your judgment. It asks more, because it concentrates your working time on the decisions where judgment is genuinely required and removes the information-gathering and synthesis work that used to consume most of your day. The executive who responds to this by becoming better at judgment—more deliberate, more honest about uncertainty, more skilled at integrating diverse perspectives—becomes dramatically more valuable. The one who uses AI to do more of what they were already doing, faster, misses the opportunity.

## Trust as Organizational Infrastructure

Trust is not a soft attribute. It is organizational infrastructure—the mechanism by which decisions propagate through an organization quickly, by which stakeholders extend credit to leaders whose direction they cannot independently verify, and by which institutions maintain credibility in a world where much of what they produce can be questioned.

The organizational behavior literature distinguishes between two forms of trust that map directly onto the AI transition. Transactional trust is the expectation that a counterpart will deliver what they committed to—reliable, predictable, verifiable. AI systems can earn and manage transactional trust. They are consistent, accurate, and do not have bad days. An AI system that consistently produces high-quality outputs earns the kind of trust that automation has always earned: the trust that the machine will do what it is designed to do.

Transformational trust is different. It is the trust you extend to a person's judgment in novel situations that you cannot fully specify in advance—situations where you cannot verify their reasoning, where you are betting on who they are rather than on what they have been told to do. In high-stakes decisions—a merger that changes the company's identity, a personnel choice that defines the culture, a strategic pivot made in a crisis before the evidence is complete—the people involved are extending transformational trust. They are following a leader's direction not because they have verified the analysis, but because they trust the leader's judgment and character.

AI cannot earn transformational trust. Not because it lacks analytical capability, but because trust of this kind requires shared vulnerability. It requires the possibility of sacrifice, of being wrong in ways that matter personally, of accountability that goes beyond contract terms. The regional director who is trusted in a crisis is trusted because people have watched her make difficult calls, acknowledge mistakes, and stand by commitments that were costly to honor. That trust is built through accumulated behavior and cannot be produced by deploying the right software.

As AI handles more operational decisions, the trust relationships between senior leaders—and between leaders and their organizations—become the primary connective tissue determining how fast good decisions propagate and how well organizations hold together when the environment changes faster than processes can adapt. A professional services firm that deployed AI for analytical work found that clients would not accept recommendations from the firm without a trusted partner standing behind them. The AI could produce analysis indistinguishable in quality from junior analyst work, but clients continued to pay for the partner who would put their professional reputation behind the recommendation and be held accountable for its quality. The AI raised the floor of what the firm could deliver; trust determined what clients were willing to pay for.

The implication is not abstract. Building and sustaining trust requires investment—in consistency, in transparency, in the willingness to be accountable in ways that are costly. It requires showing up when things are hard, acknowledging uncertainty rather than projecting false confidence, and maintaining commitments even when circumstances make it expensive. None of this can be delegated to AI systems. All of it becomes more valuable as AI makes the analytical and execution work that used to distinguish organizations more widely available.

## Taste, Aesthetic Sensibility, and the Curation Function

James March, the organizational theorist, introduced a concept he called aesthetic rationality—the capacity to make choices that are not fully justified by data but reflect cultivated taste, shaped by deep familiarity with what excellence looks like in a domain. March's insight is that this is not irrational. It is a different form of rationality, particularly important when the future is uncertain enough that optimization against specified criteria is less valuable than the judgment to set the criteria well.

AI systems optimize for specified criteria. They are extraordinary at this. Give an AI system a clear objective function and large amounts of data, and it will find better solutions faster than any human team. What it cannot do is set the objective function in the first place—cannot determine what excellence looks like in a domain where excellence is contested, culturally inflected, and not fully articulable. The specification of criteria is precisely where aesthetic sensibility operates.

A major consumer goods company deployed AI to generate product variants, packaging designs, and campaign concepts at scale. The volume of technically competent options the AI produced was overwhelming. Hundreds of options, each defensible on the metrics the AI had been given. What became scarce and therefore valuable was the small group of executives with genuine taste, deep category expertise, and cultural intuition who could identify the one concept worth building from the five hundred that were merely adequate. The brand director's job shifted from creative commissioning to curatorial judgment—a fundamentally more demanding role, because selecting from abundance requires a more developed aesthetic faculty than commissioning from scratch.

This pattern recurs wherever AI makes execution abundant. A retailer that deployed AI to optimize pricing and assortment across thousands of SKUs found that the models outperformed human merchants on every quantifiable metric. What they could not do was identify the category that did not yet exist, recognize the emerging cultural shift that would create demand before it appeared in transaction data, or make the conviction bet on a new brand before the evidence materialized. The most valuable merchandising executives became those who could articulate a point of view about where the culture was heading and make a bet before the data caught up.

Taste is learnable, though not quickly. It develops through deliberate exposure to excellence and failure—through studying what works and why, through developing a vocabulary for quality that goes beyond intuition, through accumulating enough failure to recognize its early signatures. Executives who have invested in developing aesthetic sensibility in their domain—the CMO who understands what makes a brand conviction durable, the product leader who can distinguish a genuinely useful feature from a technically impressive one, the CFO who can read the character of a capital allocation choice rather than just its returns profile—are investing in capabilities that AI cannot replicate.

The implication is that "taste" deserves to be taken seriously as a strategic asset. It has historically been dismissed as subjective—a soft preference rather than a professional capability. In an AI world, the ability to curate from abundance, to distinguish excellent from merely competent, to hold and act on a cultivated point of view about what matters in a domain, is one of the clearest differentiators available.

## The Right Questions

AI systems answer questions extraordinarily well. Given a clear question and access to relevant information, AI produces answers with a speed and comprehensiveness that no human team can match. The capability is so impressive that it is easy to miss what it does not do.

AI is essentially inert on the question of which questions to ask.

This is not a technical limitation that more capable AI systems will eventually overcome. It is a structural feature of how AI systems work: they generate answers within a problem space defined by the human asking. If the question is wrong—if it assumes the wrong causal structure, defines the wrong objective, or misses the risk everyone in the room has implicitly agreed not to discuss—the AI's answer is excellently produced and fundamentally misdirected.

The best executive questions do something specific: they violate assumptions. They challenge the implicit consensus that has formed around a problem. They surface the risk that everyone is treating as outside the scope of discussion. They ask why something that is taken for granted is taken for granted. These questions require intellectual courage—the willingness to interrupt comfortable narratives, to be wrong about assumptions in ways that are socially costly, to raise the possibility that the frame itself is the problem.

AI systems cannot do this, because formulating a genuinely disruptive question requires knowing what the room is implicitly assuming and being willing to challenge it. That knowledge is social and contextual. The courage to act on it is a character disposition, not a computational capability.

Research on decision quality consistently shows that the most consequential interventions in organizational decision processes are not analytical—they are structural: the question that reframes the problem, the challenge to the assumption underlying the options, the articulation of the risk that was being treated as out of scope. These interventions come from humans who combine domain knowledge, social intelligence, and the willingness to create friction in service of better thinking.

Executives who are developing this capability—who are investing in the art of asking better questions—are building something genuinely resistant to AI displacement. The skill involves learning to distinguish productive friction from mere contrarianism, to time interventions for when they will be heard rather than simply when they are true, and to hold discomfort long enough to let a room work through assumptions that are genuinely hard to examine. None of these are skills that AI develops for you. They require practice and a disposition toward honest engagement that AI cannot model.

## Giving Meaning to Work

The deepest capability this chapter has to discuss is also the one most at risk of being dismissed as sentimental. Humans are motivated by meaning. Not only by compensation, status, or the intrinsic interest of the work itself—though all of these matter—but by the sense that what they do matters, is understood by people who care, and contributes to something that goes beyond the transaction of employment.

Research in organizational psychology—from Viktor Frankl's foundational work on meaning, through Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's self-determination theory, through Dan Ariely's empirical studies on meaningful work—converges on a consistent finding: the sense that one's work has significance, and that it is genuinely seen and valued by others, is one of the most powerful determinants of sustained engagement and performance.

AI can produce the syntax of recognition. It can generate the performance review that sounds appreciative, the all-hands message that sounds inspiring, the one-on-one summary that sounds like it understands what the employee is going through. But employees can feel the difference—not always consciously, but consistently—between a leader who genuinely sees their contribution and a process that has been optimized to simulate it.

This distinction will become more, not less, important as AI takes on more of the communication and recognition functions that leaders currently perform. The organizations that sustain high engagement through this transition will be those whose leaders invest in the genuinely human functions of recognition and meaning-making—the capacity to understand what makes each person's work feel significant and to reflect that understanding back in ways that are specific and real.

This is not a soft skill. It is the reason that organizations in high-trust environments consistently outperform those in low-trust environments on innovation, speed of decision-making, and adaptation to change. As AI removes the barriers to execution speed, the trust and meaning that connect people to their work and to their leaders become the primary organizational accelerants. The executive who can build environments where people feel seen, where the purpose of the work is articulated in ways that resonate with the humans doing it, and where the contribution of individuals is recognized in ways that feel genuine—this executive will be commanding a capability that no AI system can provide.

The deepest version of the book's central argument is visible here. Gathering information has become cheap. Producing analysis has become cheap. Executing operationally has become cheaper than at any point in organizational history. The executive who understands what this means for where value concentrates will invest in exactly the opposite of what is becoming cheap: the judgment that cannot be reduced to optimization, the trust that requires consistent human behavior over time, the aesthetic sensibility that sets the criteria before AI optimizes against them, the questions that challenge what the room is assuming, and the human presence that makes people feel their work is seen and matters.

These are not new virtues. They are ancient ones, newly scarce. And scarcity, as always, is where value concentrates.
