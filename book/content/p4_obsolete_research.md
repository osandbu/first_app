# Research Notes: p4_obsolete — What Skills Become Obsolete

---

## 1. Historical Parallels and Analogies

### The Navigator and GPS
For centuries, ship navigation was a prestige skill. A master navigator commanded
respect because the ability to compute position from celestial observation, tide tables,
and dead reckoning was genuinely rare and genuinely consequential. The navigator's role
required years of training and conferred real status. GPS did not make navigation
unimportant — ships still must go places — but it made the old skill set essentially
worthless. The executives who thrived were those who understood this early and
redirected their expertise toward route planning, weather routing, and operational
logistics that required judgment, not computation.

The parallel for executives: information-gathering and analytical synthesis have been
prestige skills because they were genuinely hard. AI is GPS for knowledge work. The
skill of "synthesizing the competitive landscape" or "building the board deck" is not
about to become more valuable.

### The Bookkeeper and the Ledger Machine
In the early 20th century, large organizations employed armies of bookkeepers — skilled
workers who maintained financial ledgers, reconciled accounts, and produced summaries
for management. The job required numeracy, discipline, and deep familiarity with the
firm's accounts. It was a respectable career path. The arrival of mechanical tabulating
machines in the 1920s and 1930s, followed by early computers in the 1950s, did not just
speed bookkeeping — it eliminated the occupation's core value proposition entirely.
Surveyors of the 1950s who framed their expertise as "I know how to manage a team of
bookkeepers" were soon in trouble. Those who reframed to "I understand what the numbers
mean and what decisions they should drive" survived and often prospered.

This parallels directly the executive who has built identity and status around managing
large analytical teams — the chief strategy officer with a 40-person research function,
the CFO whose gravitas derives from overseeing the planning and reporting cycle. The
work these teams do is exactly the work AI systems now replicate at lower cost and
higher speed.

### The Encyclopedia Editor and the Internet
Encyclopedia Britannica employed a staff of expert editors who gathered, verified,
synthesized, and organized knowledge. This was a genuine professional skill — knowing
what to include, how to contextualize it, what sources to trust, how to structure a
coherent summary of a complex topic. The internet did not make synthesis unimportant;
it made the particular form of synthesis that Britannica editors performed essentially
worthless because the information abundance problem was solved by a different
architecture entirely.

Executives who built their careers on "I know where to find the relevant information
and how to put it together" face the same structural challenge. The skill was real;
the scarcity that made it valuable has been eliminated.

## 2. Key Frameworks from Management Literature

### The Skill Decay Curve (Kaufman / Anders Ericsson traditions)
Deliberate practice research shows that skills maintained through repeated application
stay sharp; skills not practiced decay. When AI systems take over the day-to-day
exercise of certain cognitive tasks — analysis, report synthesis, coordination
management — executives lose the repetitions that kept those skills sharp. This is not
purely theoretical: pilots who rely heavily on autopilot demonstrate measurable
degradation in manual flying skill. The chapter should name this dynamic: AI can
create skill atrophy in precisely the executives who use it most.

### Clayton Christensen — Jobs to Be Done (inverted)
Christensen's framework asks: what job is the customer hiring this product to do?
Applied to executive skills: what job is the organization hiring this executive to do?
When the jobs that justified certain skills (synthesizing competitive intelligence,
managing the analytical layer, coordinating operational reporting) can be performed
by AI at lower cost, the executive's value proposition shifts. The chapter should
help executives audit which jobs they are hired to do and which of those jobs are
now being automated.

### Herbert Simon — Bounded Rationality
Simon argued that human decision-makers satisfice rather than optimize — they settle
for "good enough" because the cost of gathering more information exceeds the marginal
value. When AI removes the cost of gathering and synthesizing information, bounded
rationality shifts. The chapter can use this to explain why the executive skill of
"I know how to work with incomplete information" changes character: it is no longer
about tolerating information scarcity, it is about exercising judgment when information
abundance creates its own distortions (overconfidence, analysis paralysis, false
precision).

### Sumantra Ghoshal and Christopher Bartlett — The Individualized Corporation
Their research on large corporations showed that much of middle and senior management
time was spent in the "integration" role — gathering information from different parts
of the organization and synthesizing it for decision-making up the hierarchy. This
integration function was the core justification for extensive management layers. When
AI systems perform integration continuously and at low cost, the human roles built
around that integration function lose their rationale. The chapter should use this to
argue that the obsolescence is structural, not individual — whole layers of management
will require rethinking.

## 3. Concrete Business Examples

### A Global Insurance Company and Its Actuarial Reporting Layer
A large insurance company maintained a team of fifty actuaries and analysts whose
primary output was a monthly report synthesizing risk data for senior underwriters
and the executive committee. AI systems reduced the cycle time for that report from
four weeks to four hours. The company did not need fifty analysts to produce a
four-hour report. More significantly, the executives whose weekly rhythm was organized
around receiving, reviewing, and discussing that report found their coordination role
hollowed out. The monthly review meeting — the core venue for executive synthesis and
alignment — no longer justified its length or frequency. The skill of "running the
monthly business review" ceased to be a distinguishing executive capability.

### A Retail Conglomerate and the Merchandising Intelligence Function
A large retail group employed a dedicated competitive intelligence team whose job was
to monitor competitor pricing, promotional activity, and product ranges. Senior
merchandisers spent significant time in briefings from this team. When AI systems
automated competitive monitoring with near-real-time output, the briefing function
became redundant, but — more importantly — the merchandisers who had built their
expertise partly on "knowing the competitive landscape" had to reckon with the fact
that this knowledge was now available to every junior buyer in the organization.
The knowledge was no longer a source of positional advantage.

### A Media Company and the Research-to-Strategy Pipeline
A major media company maintained a strategy group tasked with synthesizing audience
data, content performance, and market trends into quarterly strategic recommendations.
When AI systems took over the synthesis layer — producing continuous strategic
summaries rather than quarterly reports — the strategy team found its traditional
deliverable automated away. The executives who adapted quickly reframed their role:
instead of "producing the synthesis," they focused on "what does this synthesis mean
for which bets we place?" Those who could not make this shift found their function
increasingly hard to justify.

### A Professional Services Firm and the Knowledge Management Function
A large consulting firm had invested heavily in a "knowledge management" capability —
teams whose job was to curate and organize prior work, making institutional knowledge
accessible to client-serving teams. This was considered a strategic asset and was
managed by senior professionals. AI systems that can search, retrieve, and synthesize
prior work across millions of documents made the knowledge management function
structurally redundant in its traditional form. The lesson is not that knowledge
management became unimportant — it became even more important — but that the human
labor of curation and cataloguing was the thing that was automated away.

### A Global Bank and the Management Information Function
A global bank's management information function — responsible for producing executive
dashboards, financial summaries, and variance analyses — consumed dozens of analysts
across multiple geographies. When AI systems automated the production of these outputs,
bank executives faced an unexpected problem: the rhythm of their decision-making had
been built around the cadence of manual report production. The monthly close, the
quarterly business review, the annual planning cycle — all of these were paced by
the speed of human analytical work. AI systems did not just automate the work; they
disrupted the organizational calendar that was organized around it. Executives who
had developed their "reading of the business" through those periodic reviews had to
develop new capabilities for continuous, real-time interpretation.

### A Logistics Company and the Operational Coordination Role
A large logistics company employed a layer of regional operations managers whose
primary function was coordination: communicating exceptions up the hierarchy,
directing resources across operational units, and synthesizing operational status
for senior leaders. AI systems that monitored operations in real time and flagged
exceptions automatically made this coordination layer largely redundant. The operations
managers who survived were those whose value extended beyond coordination into
judgment about novel exceptions — situations the AI system could not classify.

## 4. Counter-Arguments and Responses

### Counter-Argument 1: "These skills won't disappear — AI will augment them"
The strongest version of this argument: AI does not eliminate analytical and
information-synthesis skills; it removes the drudge work and lets executives apply
those skills at a higher level. The CFO who used to spend 60% of their time producing
financial reports now spends that time interpreting them. The skill is elevated,
not eliminated.

Response: This argument is partly right but misses the structural point. When AI
systems can interpret financial reports with at least comparable sophistication to
a trained finance professional, the "elevated" analytical work faces the same
pressure as the drudge work. The augmentation argument assumes that there is a
stable floor of analytical work that requires human judgment. The honest answer
is that this floor is rising continuously. The chapter should acknowledge the
augmentation reality — it is true in the near term — while being clear that the
trajectory is toward displacement of the analytical skill set, not just elevation
of it. The question for executives is not "how do I apply my analytical skills at
a higher level?" but "which of my skills are genuinely resistant to AI displacement,
and am I investing in those?"

### Counter-Argument 2: "Experience and pattern recognition are irreplaceable"
Experienced executives often argue that their value lies not in producing analysis
but in recognizing patterns — knowing which numbers to worry about, which market
signals to trust, which competitive moves to take seriously. This pattern recognition,
the argument goes, comes from years of lived experience and cannot be replicated
by AI systems trained on historical data.

Response: Pattern recognition from historical data is precisely what AI systems do
extraordinarily well. The implicit claim in this counter-argument — that human
pattern recognition across business domains is somehow different from statistical
pattern recognition — is not supported by the evidence. Where the counter-argument
has real force is in genuinely novel situations: when the competitive environment has
no historical analog, when the strategic decision requires integrating ethical
considerations that resist quantification, or when the judgment involves understanding
the psychology of specific individuals in ways that no data set captures. The chapter
should concede the pattern recognition argument's validity at the margins while
being clear that most "pattern recognition" in business is exactly the kind of
pattern that AI systems are well-suited to. Executives who tell themselves their
pattern recognition is irreplaceable should ask honestly: irreplaceable in which
situations? For how long?

## 5. Data Points and Studies to Reference

- A WEF study on the future of jobs identifying information-processing and data-entry
  roles as among the highest at risk of automation, including roles within management
  layers whose primary function is aggregation and synthesis.

- Research from McKinsey Global Institute estimating the percentage of time senior
  managers spend on information gathering and report production — consistently
  found to be 30–40% of working time — with projections for how much of this
  is susceptible to automation.

- HBR research on how much of managerial time is spent in meetings whose primary
  function is information sharing (rather than decision-making), typically estimated
  at 70–80% of meeting time, suggesting that AI-driven information sharing could
  restructure the meeting cadence dramatically.

- Studies from the automation wave of the 1980s–1990s in manufacturing showing
  that middle management layers that existed primarily for coordination and
  information aggregation were the roles most affected by enterprise software —
  a historical precedent for what AI will do to equivalent knowledge-work roles.

- Research from organizational behavior on role identity threat: when a skill
  central to professional identity is automated, individuals tend to overestimate
  the continued value of that skill (cognitive dissonance) and underinvest in
  acquiring the new skills that would actually increase their value.

- A Bain analysis or similar showing the correlation between organizational layers
  and the ratio of "integrators" (managers whose primary job is synthesizing and
  communicating information) to "operators" (managers whose primary job is making
  judgment calls), with the implication that AI disrupts the integrator role
  disproportionately.

## 6. Suggested Chapter Structure

### Section 1 — The Skills That Built Your Career May Not Sustain It
Open with the navigator analogy. Introduce the central provocation: many of the skills
that got executives to the senior level are the skills most exposed to AI displacement.
This is not comfortable, and the chapter will not pretend it is. Establish the audit
frame: every executive has a skill portfolio. Some skills are appreciating assets;
others are depreciating liabilities. The chapter helps executives tell the difference.

### Section 2 — The Three Skill Categories AI Is Replacing
Organize the obsolescence argument into three buckets:

1. **Information gathering and synthesis** — competitive intelligence, market research,
   business performance summaries, board materials. If the primary deliverable is
   a synthesized view of available information, AI does it better, faster, and cheaper.

2. **Operational coordination** — exception management, status reporting, cross-functional
   alignment meetings, management information functions. The coordination role existed
   because information did not flow automatically; AI makes it flow automatically.

3. **Analytical team management** — the executive whose primary management challenge
   was directing a large team of analysts, researchers, or data professionals. When
   AI systems produce the analytical output, the job of managing the people producing
   it diminishes proportionally.

### Section 3 — The Organizational Archaeology: Why These Skills Accumulated
Explain why executives accumulated these skills in the first place — they were
genuinely scarce and genuinely valuable. Use the Ghoshal/Bartlett integration
framework. The information scarcity argument: these skills built management layers
because information did not flow freely and someone had to move it. Now it flows
freely. This is structural displacement, not individual failure.

### Section 4 — The Honest Audit
Give executives a framework for auditing their own skill portfolio. Three questions:

1. What percentage of my working week is spent producing or reviewing synthesized
   information rather than making decisions?
2. Which of my team's functions exist to gather, aggregate, or communicate information
   that AI could now generate continuously and automatically?
3. If the synthesis work I currently do were performed by an AI system with at least
   my competence, what would I be doing instead — and is that higher-value work I
   am capable of?

The audit is uncomfortable by design. The chapter should name this discomfort and
invite the reader to sit with it.

### Section 5 — The Skeptic's Case: Augmentation, Not Elimination
Address the strongest counter-arguments directly. Acknowledge the augmentation
reality — in the near term, many executives will find AI making them more productive
at the skills they already have. But distinguish between near-term productivity gains
and long-term skill value. Use the pilot autopilot analogy: short-term, autopilot
makes flying easier; long-term, it degrades the manual skill. The executive who
outsources their analytical thinking to AI systems may find, in five years, that
their judgment has atrophied alongside the skill they stopped practicing.

### Section 6 — The Skill Portfolio Reset
End with implications. Not a laundry list of "learn these new skills" — that would
be preachy and unrealistic. Instead, a reframe: the question is not which new skills
to acquire but which existing skills to invest in more deeply. The skills that
compound in an AI world (judgment, trust, organizational design, ethical reasoning)
are extensions of existing human capabilities, not alien additions. The executive
who is honest about their skill depreciation has a genuine opportunity to redirect
effort toward what will matter. The one who is not has a genuine risk.

## Thematic Throughline

The chapter's argument is fundamentally about honesty. Executives are not well served
by consultants or books that tell them their experience is irreplaceable. Some of it
is. Much of it is not. The most useful gift this chapter can give is a clear-eyed
framework for telling the difference — and a model for how executives who have
made this kind of honest reckoning have emerged with their authority intact and
their organizations stronger.

The historical parallels all share one feature: the individuals who adapted earliest
were not those who had the least invested in the old skill set, but those who were
most honest about what the new environment required. The navigator who acknowledged
that GPS had changed his profession could still build a career in maritime operations.
The one who insisted that nothing had changed could not.
