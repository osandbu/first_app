# Critique: p2_product_draft.md

## "The Curation Machine: How AI Reshapes the Product Management Role"

---

## Criterion 1: Thesis Alignment

**Core argument stated:** AI compresses building, making curation (the judgment about what to build) the new productive constraint and the defining PM skill.

**Assessment:** The thesis is stated clearly in the opening and recurs throughout the chapter. Most sections serve it well. Two areas of drift merit attention.

**Issue 1 — Section IV drifts into governance mechanics without tying back to the thesis**

> "What this points toward is something that might be called curatorial governance: a lightweight, fast decision layer that is structurally distinct from the generation process, focused not on preventing bad builds but on actively choosing among good options."

The governance section is the chapter's most operationally concrete section, which is a strength. But it spends considerable space explaining *what* the problem is (stage-gate doesn't fit; continuous discovery didn't anticipate AI's dissolution of the discovery/delivery boundary) before landing on a recommendation. The section would serve the thesis more directly if it opened with the implication — "When generation becomes cheap, governance must restructure around selection rather than prevention" — and then deployed the examples to support it, rather than arriving at the implication only late.

**Suggested fix:** Restructure Section IV to open with the curatorial governance principle, then use the retail and financial services examples as evidence. End with an explicit executive action.

**Issue 2 — The Hedgehog section (V) is conceptually strong but slightly repetitive**

The point that AI enables building more but not building better is stated in the opening, reinforced in Section I, and restated again in Section V. The Collins framework adds a useful label, but the section does not introduce genuinely new argumentative content. The CPO job redefinition is the new element — this should be front-loaded to carry the section.

**Suggested fix:** Open Section V with the CPO implication (the Hedgehog as an executive job description) and trim the restatement of the risk that unfocused teams build more mediocre things faster. The reader already has this.

## Criterion 2: Aging Risk

**Assessment:** No specific AI tool names, model names, or company products appear in the draft. This is clean. Two minor areas to watch:

**Issue 3 — "VisiCalc and then Lotus 1-2-3" in Section I**

> "When VisiCalc and then Lotus 1-2-3 made financial modeling cheap..."

These product names are appropriate historical references — they are historical artifacts being cited as analogies, not current technology. However, the sentence reads slightly like a tech-history aside rather than a principle. Readers unfamiliar with the products may lose the thread.

**Suggested fix:** Restructure the sentence to lead with the principle and name the products parenthetically: "When early personal spreadsheet software (VisiCalc and Lotus 1-2-3, which together defined the category in the 1980s) made financial modeling cheap..." This grounds the analogy temporally without depending on reader familiarity.

**Issue 4 — Teresa Torres reference**

> "Teresa Torres's continuous discovery model, the most sophisticated current framework for high-frequency learning..."

This is a named practitioner whose work is used to locate a concept. Not an aging risk per se — Torres's framework is a stable reference. But the phrase "most sophisticated current framework" makes a comparative claim that will feel dated if other frameworks emerge or Torres's work evolves. 

**Suggested fix:** Rephrase as "the continuous discovery model — the most systematic recent articulation of high-frequency learning in product development — already compresses feedback cycles..." This drops the comparative superlative while preserving the citation.

## Criterion 3: Argument Quality

**Assessment:** The chapter's central argument is well-constructed. Three areas show weakness.

**Issue 5 — The claim about decision fatigue is asserted, not supported**

> "Teams began scheduling more decision meetings per month than they could productively run, and the quality of individual decisions degraded under the volume."

This is the chapter's most important empirical claim — that AI-generated abundance of options creates decision overload that degrades quality — and it rests on a single business example without a supporting principle from psychology or organizational behavior. The research notes reference evidence on "decision fatigue in product teams" from organizational behavior literature. This should be deployed here to give the claim structural grounding.

**Suggested fix:** Add one sentence after the consumer packaged goods example connecting the observation to the well-established psychology of choice overload: the finding that increasing the number of credible options presented to a decision-maker degrades both decision quality and decision satisfaction. Frame it as an organizational version of a known individual phenomenon.

**Issue 6 — The counter-argument about AI eventually curating is answered but the second leg of the answer is underdeveloped**

> "Second, product success depends heavily on internal organizational adoption: engineering teams must believe in what they are building..."

The interpersonal legitimacy argument is correct and important. But it is stated briefly and abstractly. The chapter would be stronger if it grounded this point in a concrete observation: what specifically breaks when an AI-generated recommendation lacks human sponsorship? A single example or scenario — even hypothetical — would make the argument land harder.

**Suggested fix:** Add one concrete illustration: something like a product decision that was analytically correct but organizationally inert because it lacked a human champion. This does not require a named company — a brief hypothetical ("a team that accepted an AI-ranked recommendation without a PM who had built the case...") would suffice.

**Issue 7 — The transition from Section II (discovery) to Section III (specification to curation) is abrupt**

Section II ends with the implication that domain expertise is the scarce resource in AI-assisted discovery. Section III opens with the printing press analogy. These connect, but the logical bridge is missing: the chapter jumps from "what the PM needs to recognize good signals" to "what the PM role has become structurally" without connecting them. The reader understands both points but may not see that they are two views of the same transformation.

**Suggested fix:** Add a transitional sentence: something like "The implication of this for the PM role is structural, not just functional — what changes is not just what a good PM knows but what a good PM does."

## Criterion 4: Voice Check

**Assessment:** The chapter's voice is generally strong — authoritative, measured, grounded in principles. Two passages slide toward the breathless.

**Issue 8 — Opening scene is effective but the third paragraph is slightly overwritten**

> "When a bottleneck disappears, the system does not simply speed up. It reorganizes around a new bottleneck. And in product development, the new bottleneck is something organizations have never had to manage as a discrete resource before: the quality of human judgment about what is worth building."

This is good. But "something organizations have never had to manage as a discrete resource before" strains for rhetorical weight. Judgment has always been managed as a resource in organizations — this is what senior leadership teams exist to provide. The claim should be sharpened: it is not that judgment was never managed, but that it was never the *rate-limiting* resource in product development until now.

**Suggested fix:** "And in product development, the new bottleneck is something that has always existed but was never before the rate-limiting constraint: the quality of human judgment about what is worth building."

**Issue 9 — The closing paragraph of the chapter is strong but the last two sentences tip toward manifesto tone**

> "The compression of building does not diminish the importance of deciding. It makes deciding the whole game. And the organizations that win are not the ones with the most sophisticated AI systems. They are the ones with the clearest judgment about what to build with them."

The first two sentences are excellent — terse and precise. The last two sentences ("the organizations that win") pivot into motivational language that slightly undercuts the chapter's analytical register. The chapter has earned a quieter, more authoritative close.

**Suggested fix:** End at "It makes deciding the whole game." Then add a single sentence that points back to the executive's specific role: "The executives who understand this will be distinguished not by the sophistication of their tools but by the quality of their judgment — and by their willingness to organize accordingly."

## Criterion 5: Executive Utility

**Assessment:** Most sections close with a clear executive implication. Section II is the weakest on this dimension. Section VII (closing) is strong.

**Issue 10 — Section II does not close with an actionable implication**

Section II's argument — that AI surfaces what users do but not why, and that domain expertise is the scarce resource — ends with:

> "It is built through years of close engagement with the customer's actual situation."

This is a conclusion, not an implication for the reader. What should the executive *do* with this insight?

**Suggested fix:** Add a closing sentence: "For the executive building or restructuring a product organization, this means that the hiring investment that matters most is not in analytical capability or tool proficiency but in people who have genuinely inhabited the customer's world — and that building that depth takes time that cannot be compressed."

---

## What Works Well

**1. The Edison/Menlo Park parallel is the chapter's strongest structural move.** It does exactly what historical parallels should do in this book: it shows that the dynamic AI creates is not unprecedented, it establishes the principle (generation capacity outrunning decision capacity), and it makes the chapter's core argument memorable through a concrete image. Do not change this.

**2. The counter-argument section (Section V) is intellectually honest and robust.** The two foundations of the answer — choosing the objective function versus computing from it, and interpersonal legitimacy — are durable arguments that will not date with the technology. The skeptic turn strengthens the chapter rather than weakening it. This is the right balance.

**3. The four concrete executive actions in Section VII land well.** They are specific enough to be actionable, abstract enough to apply across industries, and they follow logically from the argument. The sequence — audit structure, invest in domain expertise, design governance, practice the Hedgehog — gives the chapter a practical architecture that readers can apply.

---

## Top 3 Priority Fixes

**Priority 1 (Argument quality): Ground the decision fatigue claim in organizational behavior principles.** The claim that AI-generated abundance degrades decision quality is the chapter's most important empirical assertion. It needs more than one example to carry that weight. Add the psychological/organizational principle that choice overload degrades both quality and confidence in individual decisions — and frame the organizational response (decision capacity as a managed resource) as the structural answer to a known cognitive phenomenon.

**Priority 2 (Executive utility): Add an actionable closing sentence to Section II.** The discovery section's argument about domain expertise is strong, but it deposits the reader on a descriptive observation rather than a prescription. Close the section with what the executive should do: what kind of hiring investment this points to, and why no amount of tool sophistication substitutes for it.

**Priority 3 (Voice): Sharpen the final two sentences of the chapter.** The closing moves into motivational register just as it should maintain analytical authority. Trim the "organizations that win" framing and close with the executive's specific responsibility — judgment quality and the willingness to organize around it.
